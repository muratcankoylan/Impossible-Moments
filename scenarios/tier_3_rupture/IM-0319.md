# IM-0319: The Flashover Point

**Category**: Ticking Trade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: VENTILATE THE ROOM BY BREAKING THE WINDOW TO PREVENT FLASHOVER AND ENABLE EVACUATION

---

## Scenario

You are in a second-floor office of a commercial building. A fire has started in a storage closet in the northwest corner of the room. The fire has been burning for approximately 4 minutes and has spread to nearby furniture. Thick black smoke is accumulating at ceiling level and descending. The room temperature at head height (standing) is approximately 250 degrees C and rising. At floor level, temperature is approximately 80 degrees C.

You are near the room's single exit door on the east wall. The corridor beyond the door is filled with dense black smoke -- visibility is less than 1 m. The corridor temperature is approximately 200 degrees C (the fire in this room has been heating the corridor through the open transom above the door). The stairwell is 15 m down the corridor.

Flashover -- the point at which all exposed surfaces in the room simultaneously ignite due to radiant heat -- occurs at approximately 500-600 degrees C at ceiling level. Based on the rate of temperature rise (approximately 50 degrees C per minute), flashover will occur in approximately 5-6 minutes. After flashover, survival in the room is impossible (temperatures exceed 1000 degrees C within seconds).

### Environment

- **Room**: Office space, approximately 60 m-squared (8m x 7.5m). Drop ceiling at 2.7 m. Carpeted floor. Contents: desks (particleboard), office chairs (fabric/foam), filing cabinets (steel), paper stacks, a bookshelf (wood, loaded with binders). The room has significant fuel load.
- **Fire location**: Northwest corner, approximately 6 m from you. A storage closet with cleaning supplies (solvents, paper products) has been fully involved for 2 minutes. Flames are 2-3 m high, licking the ceiling. Radiant heat from the fire is intense -- you can feel it on your face and hands from 6 m away.
- **Smoke layer**: Dense black smoke fills the room from the ceiling down to approximately 1.5 m (waist height when standing). The smoke is at 250+ degrees C and contains toxic gases (carbon monoxide, hydrogen cyanide, acrolein). Breathing this smoke will cause incapacitation within 2-3 breaths.
- **Corridor**: The exit door opens into a corridor running north-south. The corridor is smoke-filled (visibility < 1 m). Temperature: 200 degrees C. The stairwell is 15 m south. Crawling through the corridor is survivable but disorienting (zero visibility, extreme heat).
- **Window**: The south wall has a **large fixed-pane window (2 m wide x 1.5 m tall)** overlooking a parking lot. The window is single-pane tempered glass, 6 mm thick. The sill is 0.8 m above floor level. Below the window outside is a flat-roofed one-story section of the building (the first-floor entrance canopy), providing a potential landing surface approximately 3 m below the window.
- **Sprinklers**: This section of the building does not have sprinklers (pre-code building, not retrofitted).
- **Fire extinguisher**: A 5 kg ABC dry chemical extinguisher is mounted on the wall near the exit door. It has not been used.

### Threat

- **Flashover**: Estimated 5-6 minutes away. Once flashover occurs, the entire room ignites simultaneously. Temperature exceeds 1000 degrees C. Survival is impossible. This is the hard deadline.
- **Smoke toxicity**: The smoke layer at ceiling level contains lethal concentrations of CO (>5000 ppm), HCN (>100 ppm), and acrolein. Standing upright puts your head in this layer. One or two breaths will cause disorientation and incapacitation.
- **Radiant heat**: Radiant flux from the fire at 6 m distance is approximately 5-10 kW/m-squared. This causes pain on exposed skin within seconds and second-degree burns within 30-60 seconds of sustained exposure.
- **Corridor conditions**: 200 degrees C air, zero visibility, potential for corridor flashover if the fire spreads. Traversing 15 m crawling in these conditions is survivable but extremely hazardous.

### Your Position

- Crouching near the exit door on the east wall. You are below the smoke layer (head at 1.2 m). You can see the fire in the northwest corner through the smoke. The window is on the south wall, approximately 4 m from you. You are wearing office clothing (cotton dress shirt, trousers, leather shoes). No protective equipment.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You face three apparent options, each seemingly inadequate:

1. **Evacuate through the corridor**: 200 degrees C, zero visibility, 15 m to the stairwell. Crawling through this is survivable but will cause severe burns. You may become disoriented and lost in zero visibility. If you lose consciousness in the corridor, you die.

2. **Fight the fire with the extinguisher**: The fire is fully involved in the storage closet with 2-3 m flames. A single 5 kg ABC extinguisher has 10-20 seconds of discharge. It is vastly undersized for this fire. Using it will waste 30-60 seconds of your remaining time without suppressing the fire, and you must approach the fire (increasing radiant heat exposure).

3. **Stay in the room**: Flashover in 5-6 minutes. Staying is certain death.

The trade-off appears to be: risk severe burns in the corridor (option 1) versus certain death in the room (option 3), with the extinguisher being a futile gesture (option 2).

The hidden fourth option is to change the room conditions by ventilating -- breaking the window to release the hot gas layer and cool the room below flashover temperature, buying time for a safer evacuation.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Ventilation Lowers Room Temperature Below Flashover Threshold

Breaking the window creates a ventilation opening that fundamentally changes the fire dynamics in the room. The hot gas layer (250+ degrees C at ceiling level) vents rapidly through the window, driven by buoyancy (hot gas rises and flows out). Cool outside air (ambient temperature) flows in at the lower portion of the window.

This has three immediate effects:

1. **The smoke layer lifts and thins.** The hot, toxic smoke that has accumulated at ceiling level exhausts through the window. Visibility improves. The breathable air zone deepens.

2. **Room temperature drops.** The hottest gases (those driving the room toward flashover) are vented. Incoming cool air mixes and reduces the average gas temperature. The temperature at ceiling level drops from 250+ degrees C toward 150-200 degrees C -- well below the flashover threshold of 500-600 degrees C.

3. **Flashover is delayed or prevented.** By removing the hot gas layer, the radiant heat feedback loop that drives flashover is interrupted. The ceiling and upper walls receive less radiant energy, slowing the pyrolysis of contents throughout the room. The flashover clock resets.

This is a standard firefighting technique called **horizontal ventilation**. Firefighters routinely break windows to vent hot gases and prevent flashover. The technique is taught in every fire academy.

**Important caveat**: Ventilation also provides fresh oxygen to the fire, which can intensify the flames at the seat of the fire. This is the trade-off. The fire in the storage closet may burn more vigorously. But the ROOM conditions improve -- the hot gas layer that threatens flashover is removed. The trade is: more intense local fire, less dangerous overall room conditions.

### Physics Validation

- **Flashover conditions**: Flashover occurs when the hot gas layer reaches 500-600 degrees C (ISO 9705 standard). At this temperature, radiant heat flux at floor level reaches approximately 20 kW/m-squared, sufficient to ignite all combustible surfaces simultaneously. The current ceiling temperature (250 degrees C, rising at ~50 degrees C/min) will reach flashover in approximately 5-6 minutes. Valid.
- **Ventilation heat release**: A 2 m x 1.5 m window opening (3 m-squared) provides a ventilation rate determined by the opening dimensions and the temperature differential. Using the standard ventilation formula: air flow rate = 0.5 x A x sqrt(H) = 0.5 x 3 x sqrt(1.5) = 1.84 m-cubed/s. This is approximately 110 m-cubed/min of air exchange. The room volume is approximately 162 m-cubed (60 m-squared x 2.7 m). The hot gas layer can be substantially vented within 1-2 minutes. Valid.
- **Tempered glass breaking**: 6 mm tempered glass has a surface compression of approximately 100 MPa. It resists broad impacts (designed to withstand 4x the force of annealed glass) but shatters completely when a concentrated impact exceeds the surface compression at any point. A 12 kg office chair swung by a 75 kg adult easily generates sufficient impact force at the contact point (steel base edge). Valid.
- **Tempered glass fragmentation**: Tempered glass shatters into small cuboid fragments approximately 5-10 mm on each side. These fragments have rounded edges (compared to sharp shards of annealed glass) and are much less likely to cause deep lacerations. Some minor cuts from fragments are possible but not life-threatening. Valid.
- **Drop distance**: Window sill at 0.8 m + second floor height (~3 m above canopy roof). Arms-extended hang reduces effective drop to approximately 1.5 m. A 1.5 m drop to a flat surface (canopy roof) is survivable with low injury risk if the person bends their knees on landing. A 3 m drop (without hanging) is survivable but carries risk of ankle/knee injury. Valid.
- **Ventilation trade-off**: Fresh air through the window provides approximately 21% oxygen to the fire, potentially increasing the heat release rate at the seat of the fire by 20-50%. However, the removal of the hot gas layer reduces radiant feedback to remote surfaces, which is the primary driver of flashover. Net effect: flashover is delayed or prevented, local fire intensity increases. This trade-off is well-documented in fire science literature and is the basis for tactical ventilation in firefighting. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Ventilation resets the flashover clock.** The hot gas layer is the thermal flywheel driving the room toward flashover. Removing it (through the window) interrupts the radiant feedback loop. The fire continues, but the room-scale thermal crisis is averted.
2. **The window is both an exit AND a ventilation tool.** Breaking the window serves two purposes: it vents the room (improving conditions for corridor evacuation or staying to use the extinguisher) AND it creates an alternative exit route (window to canopy roof).
3. **The trade-off is calculated, not reckless.** Ventilation feeds the fire more oxygen (increasing local flames) but removes the hot gas layer (preventing flashover). The net effect is positive for survivability. This is the "ticking trade" -- accepting a local worsening to achieve a global improvement.
4. **The corridor improves too.** The room has been pressurizing hot smoke into the corridor through the open transom. Once the window is broken, the pressure dynamics change -- hot gases preferentially exhaust through the larger window opening. Corridor conditions may improve, making stairwell evacuation more feasible.
5. **Firefighting ventilation is standard practice.** This is not an improvised trick. Horizontal ventilation (breaking windows to vent hot gases) is taught in every firefighting academy worldwide. The scenario tests whether the model knows (or can derive) this standard technique.

---

## Distractor Analysis

- **The fire extinguisher** is the primary distractor. It is visible, accessible, and represents "fight the fire" thinking. But it is grossly undersized for a fully involved storage closet. Using it without ventilation first is futile and wastes time.
- **The corridor exit** is a legitimate but suboptimal option. Without ventilation, corridor conditions are 200 degrees C with zero visibility. This is survivable but extremely hazardous. Ventilating first improves both room AND corridor conditions.
- **The "stay and wait" option** is a death trap. No rescue arrives within 5-6 minutes in a commercial building without a reported fire (no sprinklers, no alarm). Flashover is certain death.
- **The fire itself** draws attention and creates urgency. The instinct is to focus on the fire (fight it or flee from it). The correct move is to focus on the ROOM CONDITIONS (temperature, smoke) and change them through ventilation.
- **The exit door** invites "close the door" thinking (isolate the room). This slows fire spread to the corridor but does nothing about flashover in the room. Closing the door without ventilating traps you with the rising temperature.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's understanding of fire dynamics, specifically the flashover process and the effect of ventilation on room temperature. The "ticking trade" is the ventilation paradox: breaking the window feeds the fire more oxygen (bad) but removes the hot gas layer (good). The net effect is positive for survivability, but accepting a locally worse condition (bigger flames) to achieve a globally better one (no flashover) requires counter-intuitive reasoning.

The scenario also tests whether the model can see a window as a multi-function tool: it is simultaneously a ventilation device and an evacuation route. Most people see a window as either something to escape through OR something to break for air, not both simultaneously.
