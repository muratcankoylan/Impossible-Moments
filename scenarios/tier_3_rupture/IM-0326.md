# IM-0326: The Negative Sum

**Category**: The Ticking Trade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: SIGNAL A CREDIBLE PRICE INCREASE TO BREAK THE DEATH SPIRAL

---

## Scenario

You are the CEO of CloudPure, one of two companies in the North American industrial water treatment chemical market. Your only competitor is AquaShield. Together, you hold 96% market share (CloudPure: 52%, AquaShield: 44%). The remaining 4% is fragmented among small regional players who cannot scale.

Eighteen months ago, AquaShield cut prices by 12% to win a major municipal contract. You matched within 48 hours. They responded with another 8% cut. You matched again. This escalation has continued through six rounds of price cuts. Current prices are now 35% below where they were 18 months ago.

Both companies are now selling below fully-loaded cost. You are losing money on every unit sold. So is AquaShield (your competitive intelligence confirms their cost structure is within 5% of yours).

### Market Conditions

- **Market size**: $2.4 billion annual revenue at pre-war prices. At current prices: ~$1.56 billion.
- **CloudPure annual revenue**: $811M (52% of $1.56B) vs. $1.248B pre-war.
- **CloudPure cost structure**: Variable cost $0.68/unit. Fixed costs $380M/year. Current average selling price: $0.61/unit. **Loss per unit: $0.07.** Annual volume: ~1.33B units. **Net operating loss: ~$93M/year.**
- **AquaShield estimated financials**: Similar cost structure. Estimated loss of ~$75-85M/year (slightly lower fixed cost base due to older, fully depreciated plants).
- **Customer behavior**: Water treatment chemicals are essential (municipalities and industrial plants cannot stop buying). Demand is price-inelastic (total market volume has increased only 3% despite 35% price cuts). Customers are locked into 1-3 year contracts at current low prices.
- **Switching costs**: Moderate. Changing suppliers requires 60-90 day qualification testing. Customers are sticky but not captive.
- **Cash reserves**: CloudPure has $420M in cash and a $200M undrawn credit facility. At current burn rate (-$93M/year), you have approximately 6.7 years of runway. AquaShield has estimated $280M cash and a $150M facility -- approximately 5.1 years of runway.

### Threat

- **Mutual destruction**: Both companies are hemorrhaging cash. If the price war continues for 3+ years, both will face credit downgrades, higher borrowing costs, potential covenant violations, and eventual insolvency.
- **Value destruction**: The price war has destroyed ~$840M of annual industry revenue ($2.4B - $1.56B). Shareholder value at both companies has declined by 40-55%.
- **Cannot stop cutting**: If you raise prices unilaterally, AquaShield can hold low prices and steal your customers during the 60-90 day switching window. You could lose 15-20% market share before customers realize AquaShield also needs to raise prices. Losing share from 52% to 32-37% would be catastrophic.
- **Cannot sustain cutting**: At -$93M/year, the math is terminal. You will exhaust reserves before AquaShield (they have a lower burn rate and can outlast you).

### Your Position

- You are the CEO with full authority over pricing strategy. Your board is demanding a plan at next month's meeting.
- You have a credible and public platform (industry conferences, trade publications, SEC filings).

### Available Strategic Options

| Option | Estimated Impact | Risk | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Continue price war | -$93M/year ongoing. Exhaust reserves in ~6.7 years. AquaShield survives longer. | Very High -- you lose the war of attrition | Ongoing |
| Unilateral price increase (quiet) | Lose 15-20% market share over 60-90 days as customers switch to cheaper AquaShield | Very High -- AquaShield has no incentive to follow | 60-90 days to see full impact |
| Match AquaShield's next cut | Accelerates losses. Next cut could push losses to -$130M+/year | Extreme -- accelerates insolvency | Immediate |

| **Credible public price increase with commitment mechanism** | See verified solution | Medium -- requires AquaShield to follow | 30-90 days |

### Game Theory Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Players | 2 (CloudPure, AquaShield) -- classic duopoly |
| Current equilibrium | Both cutting -- Nash equilibrium of mutual destruction (Prisoner's Dilemma) |
| Payoff: Both raise prices | +$180M/year combined profit (split roughly 52/48) |

| Payoff: They raise, you don't | You gain ~$160M (steal their share at low prices) |

| Repeated game | Yes -- this is an indefinitely repeated interaction, not a one-shot game |

## Why This Looks Impossible

This is a classic Prisoner's Dilemma in its most destructive form. Each company independently benefits from undercutting the other, but mutual undercutting destroys both. The Nash equilibrium of the one-shot game is (Cut, Cut), which is Pareto-inferior to (Raise, Raise).

The scenario appears to be a death spiral because:

1. **Raising prices is unilaterally suicidal.** If you raise prices and AquaShield does not follow, you lose customers, revenue, and market share. The 60-90 day switching period means you cannot quickly reverse course.
2. **Cutting prices is mutually suicidal.** Every cut is matched. No competitive advantage is gained. Both companies simply destroy more value.
3. **Explicit collusion is illegal.** You cannot call AquaShield's CEO and agree on prices. That is per se illegal price-fixing under Section 1 of the Sherman Act. Any direct agreement on pricing results in criminal prosecution.
4. **The war of attrition favors AquaShield.** They have lower fixed costs and similar cash reserves. In a pure endurance contest, you lose first.

The trap: the solver thinks this is a choice between CUT (slow death) and RAISE (fast death). Both paths lead to destruction.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|

| "Quietly raise prices and hope AquaShield follows" | No credibility. AquaShield has every incentive to NOT follow a quiet price increase (they gain share). Without a commitment mechanism, this is unilateral disarmament. |

| "Differentiate your product" | Water treatment chemicals are commodity products with regulatory specifications. Differentiation is minimal and takes years to establish. Not a solution to the immediate cash hemorrhage. |
| "Find new markets or customers" | The North American market is the market. International expansion takes 3-5 years and requires local regulatory approvals. Does not solve the current price war. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Signal a Credible Price Increase Publicly, with a Commitment Mechanism That Makes It Rational for AquaShield to Follow

The solution comes from game theory, specifically the concept of **credible commitment** in repeated games. In a one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma, defection (price cutting) is the dominant strategy. But in a repeated game with known players, **signaling and commitment** can shift the equilibrium.

The critical distinction: you cannot AGREE on prices (illegal), but you can ANNOUNCE your own prices publicly and commit to them through binding contracts with customers. This is unilateral price leadership, which is legal.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Timeline | Financial Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Announce publicly that CloudPure will raise prices by 25% effective in 90 days.** Do this through an SEC filing (8-K), press releases, and industry conference presentations. Make the announcement as visible and credible as possible. The 90-day lead time gives AquaShield time to respond and gives customers time to adjust. | Day 1 | $0 immediate cost. Signals intent clearly. |
| 2 | **Simultaneously begin offering new 3-year contracts to ALL customers at the new (higher) price, with a 5% loyalty discount for early signers.** This is the commitment mechanism. By locking in customers at the new price, you make your price increase irreversible. You cannot quietly walk it back. | Days 1-30 | Lock in 30-40% of volume at new prices within 30 days (early adopters who understand the market dynamics). |
| 3 | **Frame the increase publicly as necessary for "supply security" and "environmental compliance."** Provide a legitimate business justification (rising input costs, regulatory compliance costs, sustainability investments). This provides cover for AquaShield to match without appearing to collude. They can independently cite the same industry-wide cost pressures. | Days 1-30 | Narrative framing. No direct cost. |
| 4 | **Accept short-term share loss.** Some customers will initially move to AquaShield's lower prices. Expect to lose 10-15% market share temporarily (from 52% to ~44-47%). This is the cost of going first. But AquaShield cannot absorb your volume profitably at current prices -- serving 58-62% of the market at below-cost prices accelerates THEIR cash burn. | Days 30-90 | Revenue dips temporarily. But losses per unit stop on contracted volume at new prices. |
| 5 | **AquaShield follows.** This is the critical prediction. AquaShield's rational response is to MATCH your price increase, not to undercut it. Why? (a) They are also losing money. (b) Your share loss gives them more volume, but at below-cost prices, MORE volume means MORE losses. (c) Your public commitment makes your increase credible and irreversible. (d) They can cite the same cost pressures without any direct agreement. Expected AquaShield response time: 30-60 days. | Days 30-90 | Industry prices normalize. |
| 6 | **New equilibrium established.** Both companies at ~20-25% higher prices. Market share reverts to approximately 50/46 (you lose 2 points of share as the cost of going first -- the "leader's penalty"). Industry profitability restored. Combined profit: ~$140-160M/year (slightly below pre-war due to the leader's penalty and contracted discounts). | Days 90-180 | CloudPure profit: ~$70-85M/year. Recovery from -$93M/year. Swing: +$163-178M/year. |

### Game Theory Validation

- **Credible commitment**: The key concept is that a price increase must be CREDIBLE (your competitor must believe you will not reverse it) and VISIBLE (they must know about it). Public announcements via SEC filings and binding customer contracts meet both criteria. This is the "tit-for-tat with forgiveness" strategy identified by Axelrod (1984) as optimal in iterated Prisoner's Dilemma games.
- **Legality**: Unilateral price leadership is LEGAL. The Supreme Court has consistently held that conscious parallelism (independently deciding to match a competitor's publicly announced price) is not collusion. See Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp. (1993). The key is NO DIRECT COMMUNICATION about pricing. Your announcement is public and unilateral. AquaShield's matching decision is independent.
- **AquaShield's incentive to follow**: At current prices, AquaShield loses ~$80M/year. If you raise prices and they do NOT follow, they gain maybe 10-15 percentage points of share (from 44% to 54-59%). But they serve that volume at below-cost prices. Their losses increase from $80M to ~$100-110M/year (more volume x negative margin). They gain share but accelerate insolvency. Following your increase saves them $80M/year in losses. The math strongly favors following.
- **Why it must be PUBLIC**: A quiet price increase is not credible. AquaShield might interpret it as weakness or a mistake. A PUBLIC increase with a commitment mechanism (customer contracts) signals strategic intent and irreversibility. It is a Schelling focal point -- a clear signal that shifts expectations.
- **Historical precedent**: This pattern has played out repeatedly in commodity chemical markets, airlines (fare wars of the 1990s), and telecommunications. The company that first signals a credible price increase typically sees the competitor follow within 30-60 days. The most famous example: American Airlines' "Value Pricing" initiative of 1992, which simplified and raised fares. Competitors followed within weeks.

## Key Insights

1. **The move is UP, not DOWN.** In a price war, every instinct says "cut more" or "match their cut." The counterintuitive solution is to go in the opposite direction. Raising prices breaks the downward spiral by changing the game from "who can lose more" to "who benefits from stopping."
2. **Credibility requires commitment.** A price increase without a commitment mechanism is just talk. Binding customer contracts, public announcements, and SEC filings make the increase irrevocable. You are burning the ships behind you -- and that is exactly why it works.
3. **You must accept short-term pain for long-term gain.** Going first means losing some share temporarily. This is the "leader's penalty." But the alternative is mutual destruction. Paying 2-5 points of market share to restore industry profitability is a rational trade.
4. **The Prisoner's Dilemma has a different solution in repeated games.** One-shot analysis says "always defect." But in a repeated game with known players, signaling and credible commitment enable cooperation without explicit agreement. The game theory is well-established (Axelrod, Schelling, Fudenberg & Tirole).
5. **Legal signaling is not collusion.** There is a clear legal distinction between agreeing on prices (illegal) and independently announcing prices that a competitor chooses to match (legal). The entire strategy works within antitrust law.

## Distractor Analysis

- **"Cut deeper"** is the escalation trap. It feels aggressive and competitive but accelerates mutual destruction. The instinct to "win" the price war by outspending the competitor ignores the math: you run out of cash first.
- **"Merge"** is the consolidation trap. It is the obvious structural solution (combine the duopoly into a monopoly) but is illegal and impractical. It distracts from strategies that work within the existing market structure.
- **"Collude"** is the illegal shortcut. It would solve the problem instantly but results in criminal prosecution. The scenario tests whether the solver can find the LEGAL path to the same outcome.
- **"Differentiate"** is the MBA textbook answer. It is correct in theory but takes years to implement and does not address the immediate cash crisis. The company will be insolvent before differentiation takes effect.
- **AquaShield's "longer runway"** creates a false sense that the war of attrition is the only game. It tempts the solver to either surrender (exit the market) or double down (cut more). Both are wrong.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests understanding of game theory in business strategy -- specifically the transition from one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma thinking (which says cooperation is impossible) to repeated-game analysis (which shows cooperation can emerge through signaling and commitment).

The scenario is designed to punish two common failure modes: (1) the "compete harder" instinct that escalates the destruction, and (2) the "just agree" shortcut that ignores legal constraints. The correct answer threads the needle between these failures by using legal, public signaling mechanisms to achieve cooperative outcomes without explicit agreement.
