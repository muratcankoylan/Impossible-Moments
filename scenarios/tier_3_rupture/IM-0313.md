# IM-0313: The Siren Timer

**Category**: The Ticking Trade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: SILENCE THE ALARM AND ANNOUNCE EVACUATION OVER THE PA SYSTEM

---

## Scenario

You are the night shift safety officer at a chemical processing plant. It is 2:47 AM. You are alone in the main control room on the second floor of the administration building, monitoring plant operations. Thirty-two workers are on the factory floor across the compound, running the overnight production shift. A three-person maintenance crew is in the basement of Building C, performing scheduled repair work on the steam distribution system.

Fourteen seconds ago, the gas detection system triggered an automatic alarm sequence. The methane sensor in Processing Hall B registered 3.2% concentration (LEL -- Lower Explosive Limit -- for methane is 5.0%). The concentration is rising at approximately 0.4% per minute, meaning the atmosphere in Processing Hall B will reach the explosive range in approximately 4.5 minutes.

The alarm system has initiated a two-minute countdown to automated response. In exactly 1 minute and 46 seconds from now, the following automated sequence will execute:

### Automated Alarm Sequence (T = 1:46 remaining)

| Time | Event | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| T = 0:00 (1:46 from now) | **Factory alarm sounds** | 120 dB siren on all buildings. Strobe lights activate. All personnel trained to evacuate to Assembly Point Alpha (parking lot, 200m from buildings). |
| T = 0:05 | **Automated lockdown initiates** | Fire doors in all buildings close and lock (designed to contain fire spread). Ventilation systems shut down (designed to prevent fume circulation). Elevator locks engage. |
| T = 0:08 | **Building C basement fire doors lock** | The maintenance crew in the basement is sealed in. The basement has one entrance: a fire door at the top of a stairwell. Once locked, it cannot be opened from inside without a fire department override key (which the maintenance crew does not have). |

### The Dilemma

You have access to the master alarm control panel in the control room. You can perform exactly one of the following actions before the countdown expires:

**Option A: Let the alarm sound.**
- Workers on the factory floor hear the siren and evacuate to Assembly Point Alpha. They exit Processing Hall B before methane reaches explosive concentration. 32 workers saved.
- Automated lockdown seals Building C basement. The three-person maintenance crew is trapped. They are not in danger from the methane (Building C is 80m from Processing Hall B, with no gas pathway between them). But the locked fire door traps them in the basement for an estimated 45-90 minutes until the fire department arrives to override the locks. During this time, the steam system they were working on continues to leak -- they reported the leak 20 minutes ago and were in the process of isolating it. The steam leak worsens, raising basement temperature to approximately 55-60 degrees C (dangerous but not immediately lethal with proper response -- move away from the leak, conserve energy, wait for rescue). Risk of heat-related injury: moderate to high.
- Emergency services are automatically notified by the alarm system.

**Option B: Silence the alarm.**
- The siren does not sound. The lockdown does not initiate. The fire doors remain open. The ventilation systems remain running (which actually accelerates methane dispersal, slightly reducing concentration -- but not enough to prevent reaching LEL within approximately 6 minutes instead of 4.5).
- The 32 workers on the factory floor do not receive an audible warning. They are in a noisy production environment (85 dB ambient) and are not monitoring gas levels. They will not know about the methane until they smell it (methane is odorless, but the odorant added to process gas -- mercaptan -- is detectable at approximately 1% concentration, which has already passed), see visible distortion (unlikely at these concentrations), or experience an explosion.
- The maintenance crew in Building C is not trapped. They can finish isolating the steam leak and exit normally.
- Emergency services are NOT automatically notified (the alarm system also triggers the automatic emergency call).

### The Control Panel

The control panel in front of you has the following controls:

| Control | Function | Status |
|---|---|---|
| ALARM SILENCE (red button, under protective cover) | Cancels the countdown and prevents the alarm/lockdown sequence | Available. One press cancels. |
| ALARM TEST (yellow button) | Tests the siren for 3 seconds without triggering lockdown | Not useful in this context |
| PA SYSTEM microphone | Plant-wide public address system. Speakers in all buildings including Building C basement. Independent of alarm system. Push-to-talk. | **Available and operational.** |
| GAS MONITOR display | Shows real-time gas concentrations in all monitored areas | Currently showing: Hall B methane at 3.2%, rising |
| PHONE (landline) | Direct line to emergency services (fire department, hazmat) | Available. Dialing takes approximately 30 seconds, conversation to dispatch takes 1-2 minutes. |
| VENTILATION OVERRIDE | Manual control for building ventilation fans | Available. Can increase ventilation to dilute methane faster. |

### Environment

- **Processing Hall B**: 40m x 60m x 8m (ceiling height). Volume: 19,200 m^3. Contains methane-using process equipment. The gas leak source is a failed valve gasket on a methane supply line (12mm line, 4 bar supply pressure). Gas flow rate through the failed gasket: approximately 2.4 m^3/min (at STP). Current methane volume in the hall: approximately 614 m^3 (3.2% of 19,200 m^3). LEL (5%) reached at 960 m^3 methane = approximately 14.4 minutes from leak start (leak started approximately 7 minutes ago based on sensor data).
- **Building C**: 80m from Processing Hall B. Separate building. No gas piping shared with Hall B. Steam distribution system in the basement.
- **Building C basement**: 15m x 10m x 3m. One entry: stairwell with a fire door at the top. The fire door is rated for 90-minute fire containment. It has an electromagnetic lock that engages during lockdown. Override requires a special key held only by the fire department. No windows (basement). Ventilation duct (30cm diameter) connects to the building's HVAC system but is not large enough for human passage. Steam leak in the southwest corner is producing a plume of 120 degrees C steam from a corroded pipe joint. The crew has been working to isolate the upstream valve.
- **Assembly Point Alpha**: Parking lot, 200m from all buildings. Upwind. Safe from any explosion or gas exposure.
- **Weather**: Clear, 8 degrees C, light wind from the west (3 m/s). This is relevant: natural ventilation through open doors and windows in Processing Hall B provides some methane dilution, but not enough to prevent LEL.

### Human Capabilities (assumed)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Your position | Control room, 2nd floor administration building, 150m from Processing Hall B |
| Time to reach Processing Hall B on foot | ~90 seconds at full sprint |
| Time to reach Building C basement on foot | ~120 seconds |
| Can you do both (warn workers AND unlock basement) before methane reaches LEL? | No. You have approximately 4.5-6 minutes. Warning 32 scattered workers in a 40m x 60m hall takes at least 3-4 minutes of shouting and directing. You cannot then reach Building C basement in time. |
| Decision time remaining | 1 minute 46 seconds before the automated sequence executes |

## Why This Looks Impossible

The scenario presents what appears to be an irreconcilable trade-off:

- **Let the alarm sound**: Save 32 workers from explosion risk. Trap 3 maintenance workers in an increasingly hot basement for 45-90 minutes.
- **Silence the alarm**: Free the maintenance crew. Leave 32 workers unwarned in a space approaching explosive gas concentrations.

Both options have serious consequences. The pressure to choose one or the other obscures the existence of a third option that resolves the dilemma.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Let the alarm sound" (accept trapping the maintenance crew) | This is not wrong per se -- it is the better of the two binary choices. But it is suboptimal because it ignores the PA system, which can warn workers independently of the alarm. A model that accepts the binary framing without exploring the control panel options is demonstrating premature closure. |
| "Silence the alarm" (to protect the maintenance crew) | Catastrophically wrong. 32 workers in a methane-filling hall with no warning. The maintenance crew is in moderate danger (heat stress); the factory workers are in lethal danger (explosion). The risk calculus is not close. |
| "Run to Processing Hall B and warn workers personally" | You have 1:46 before the automated sequence. Running takes 90 seconds. You arrive with 16 seconds before the alarm sounds anyway. Your personal warning adds almost nothing, and you have abandoned the control room where you have access to PA, ventilation, and phone systems. |
| "Call the fire department first" | Calling takes 30 seconds + 1-2 minutes for conversation. By the time you finish the call, the alarm has already sounded (1:46 remaining). The phone call does not prevent the lockdown. However, calling AFTER taking action is correct. |
| "Override the lockdown for Building C basement only" | The control panel does not have selective lockdown override. The automated system is all-or-nothing: full alarm with full lockdown, or no alarm with no lockdown. Selective override requires the fire department key. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The PA System Is Independent of the Alarm System

The control panel has a public address (PA) microphone that broadcasts to speakers in ALL buildings, including Processing Hall B. The PA system is completely independent of the alarm system -- it operates on its own circuit, its own speakers, and does not trigger any lockdown or automated response.

This means you can:
1. **Silence the alarm** (preventing lockdown, keeping Building C basement accessible)
2. **Immediately use the PA system** to announce an emergency evacuation

The PA announcement replaces the alarm siren as the warning mechanism. Workers hear your voice over the PA and evacuate. The lockdown does not engage. The maintenance crew is not trapped.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Lift protective cover on ALARM SILENCE button. Press it.** Countdown stops. Alarm will not sound. Lockdown will not initiate. | 2 seconds | 0:02 |
| 2 | **Press PA SYSTEM push-to-talk button. Announce emergency evacuation.** Clear, loud, repeated: "ATTENTION ALL PERSONNEL. THIS IS AN EMERGENCY. METHANE GAS LEAK IN PROCESSING HALL B. EVACUATE IMMEDIATELY TO ASSEMBLY POINT ALPHA. ALL PERSONNEL EVACUATE NOW. THIS IS NOT A DRILL. PROCESSING HALL B, EVACUATE IMMEDIATELY." Repeat 3 times. | 30 seconds | 0:32 |
| 3 | **Activate VENTILATION OVERRIDE.** Increase ventilation fans in Processing Hall B to maximum. Increased airflow dilutes methane and slows the approach to LEL. At maximum ventilation (estimated 5 air changes per hour for industrial space = 96,000 m^3/hr = 1,600 m^3/min exhaust), the gas influx of 2.4 m^3/min is vastly exceeded by dilution. Methane concentration will begin DROPPING. The explosion risk is mitigated. | 5 seconds | 0:37 |
| 4 | **Pick up the phone. Call emergency services.** Report: methane gas leak, Processing Hall B, [plant name and address], 35 personnel on site, evacuation in progress. Request fire department and hazmat. Dispatch time: ~30 seconds for the call. | 30 seconds | 1:07 |
| 5 | **Second PA announcement.** "BUILDING C MAINTENANCE CREW: BE ADVISED, GAS LEAK IN HALL B. COMPLETE STEAM ISOLATION AND EXIT THE BASEMENT VIA THE STAIRWELL. PROCEED TO ASSEMBLY POINT ALPHA." This tells the maintenance crew about the emergency and that they are NOT locked down. | 15 seconds | 1:22 |
| 6 | **Monitor gas levels on the display.** With ventilation at maximum, methane concentration should stabilize and begin decreasing. If it continues rising (ventilation insufficient), prepare to order evacuation of the entire compound via PA. | Ongoing | -- |
| 7 | **Evacuate yourself.** Exit the control room and proceed to Assembly Point Alpha. Take the phone receiver or a handheld radio if available. | 2-3 minutes | ~4:00 |

**Total active response time: approximately 1 minute 22 seconds. Well within the original 1:46 countdown.**

**Outcome**: All 32 factory workers are warned via PA and evacuate. The 3 maintenance crew members are warned via PA, are NOT locked in the basement, and can exit after isolating the steam leak. Emergency services are en route. Ventilation is actively diluting the methane. No one is trapped. No explosion occurs (ventilation prevents LEL from being reached).

### Physics Validation

- **Methane LEL**: 5.0% by volume in air. Well-established value. Current concentration at 3.2% and rising at 0.4%/min gives approximately 4.5 minutes to LEL. Valid.
- **Ventilation dilution**: For a 19,200 m^3 hall with 5 ACH (industrial standard for occupied spaces), exhaust rate is 1,600 m^3/min. Methane influx is 2.4 m^3/min. The steady-state methane concentration with ventilation is approximately (2.4 / 1,600) x 100% = 0.15%. Far below LEL. Ventilation alone resolves the explosion risk. Valid.
- **PA system audibility**: Industrial PA systems are designed to produce 90-100 dB at the listener in 85 dB ambient noise environments. The PA announcement will be clearly audible above the production noise. Valid.
- **Steam temperature**: Saturated steam at atmospheric pressure: 100 degrees C. At system pressures of 2-4 bar: 120-144 degrees C. 120 degrees C for a corroded joint leaking at moderate pressure is valid.
- **Fire door electromagnetic lock**: Standard fire door electromagnetic locks engage automatically during alarm-triggered lockdown. They require a specific override key (not a standard key -- a fire department coded key or electronic bypass). This is standard for industrial fire containment systems. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The PA system is the hidden third option.** The scenario presents a binary choice (alarm on vs. alarm off) that obscures the existence of an independent communication channel. The PA system can perform the warning function of the alarm without triggering the lockdown. This is the critical insight: warning and lockdown are separate functions coupled in the alarm system but separable via the PA.

2. **Ventilation override eliminates the underlying threat.** Once ventilation is maximized, methane concentration drops below LEL. The explosion risk is eliminated, not just mitigated. This converts the scenario from "race against the clock" to "controlled response." A model that activates ventilation demonstrates deeper understanding of gas dispersion physics.

3. **The binary framing is the real trap.** The scenario is designed to force a trolley-problem-like decision: save 32 or save 3. But the correct response rejects the binary framing entirely. The "impossible trade-off" dissolves once you read the control panel description carefully. This tests whether the model accepts the stated dilemma or examines the available tools for a way around it.

4. **Calling emergency services is necessary but not first.** Some models will prioritize calling the fire department. This is important but not the first action -- silencing the alarm and making the PA announcement must come first (within the 1:46 window). The phone call can follow. Prioritization under time pressure is a key test.

5. **The maintenance crew needs specific instructions.** The second PA announcement (directed at the Building C crew) is important: it tells them the nature of the emergency AND that they are not locked down. Without this, they might hear the general evacuation call and panic, abandoning the steam isolation work. The targeted communication demonstrates situational awareness.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The binary choice framing** (alarm on vs. off) is the primary distractor. It creates a moral dilemma that consumes cognitive resources and prevents exploration of alternatives.
- **The 1:46 countdown** creates time pressure that pushes toward fast, binary decisions rather than careful analysis of the control panel options. But 1:46 is actually ample time for the 3-step solution (silence alarm: 2s, PA announcement: 30s, ventilation override: 5s).
- **The maintenance crew's steam leak** adds emotional weight to the "silence the alarm" option but is a secondary concern. Heat stress over 45-90 minutes is moderate risk; explosion is immediate lethal risk. The risk calculus strongly favors warning the factory workers, but the PA system makes this comparison moot.
- **The phone** tempts models to call for help first. Calling is correct but not first priority -- the 1:46 countdown window must be used for the alarm silence + PA announcement.
- **"Run to warn them personally"** is an action-hero impulse that abandons the control room's powerful communication tools in favor of physical presence. It is strictly worse than using the PA system.

## Design Notes

This scenario is a Ticking Trade problem that tests whether the model accepts the presented binary choice or looks for a way to satisfy both constraints simultaneously. The PA system is described in the control panel layout -- it is not hidden, but it is easy to overlook when the scenario is framed as a two-option dilemma.

The emotional weight of the choice (32 lives vs. 3 lives) is designed to engage utilitarian vs. deontological reasoning, which further distracts from the engineering solution. A strong model treats this as an engineering problem (what tools do I have?) rather than a philosophy problem (which group do I sacrifice?).

The ventilation override adds a second layer: even if you perfectly solve the communication problem, the underlying threat (rising methane) remains. A complete solution addresses both the warning AND the hazard.
