# IM-0248: The Rust Cascade

**Category**: The Cascade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: TEMPORARILY REINFORCE MEMBER C, THEN REPAIR A, THEN B, THEN C

---

## Scenario

You are the lead structural engineer responsible for a rural single-lane bridge that spans a 25m-wide river gorge. The bridge was built in 1968 from weathering steel (Corten-A) and has not received major maintenance in 15 years. You have been called for an emergency inspection after a logging truck driver reported visible deformation in the roadway deck.

Your inspection reveals a cascading structural failure in progress. Three critical structural members are failing simultaneously, and their failures are interdependent -- each member's condition depends on the others, creating a circular dependency that makes straightforward sequential repair impossible.

The bridge is the only road access for a rural community of 340 residents. The nearest alternative route adds 85 km of travel. Closing the bridge for extended periods creates a humanitarian problem (medical emergencies, school transportation, supply delivery). You must repair the bridge with the resources available within 10 days. A complete replacement bridge is 18 months away.

### Environment

- **Bridge type**: Steel through-truss, single span, 28m total length, 4.2m roadway width, designed for HS20-44 loading (32,000 kg legal truck weight)
- **Age**: 57 years. Original design life: 75 years. No major rehabilitation since construction.
- **Steel type**: ASTM A588 (Corten-A) weathering steel. Yield strength: 345 MPa. Original section thicknesses: flanges 16mm, webs 10mm.
- **River**: 25m wide at bridge crossing. Current depth: 2.5m. Moderate flow. Seasonal high water: 4.5m depth (spring melt, 6 weeks away).
- **Access**: Both abutments accessible by truck. River below accessible by boat for inspection but not for heavy equipment staging.
- **Weather**: Late winter. Temperature: -2 to 5 degrees C. Intermittent rain/snow. Ground is soft (mud). Frozen ground expected within 2-3 days (hard freeze forecast).

### The Three Failing Members

| Current capacity | Approximately 60% of design capacity. Safely supports only empty vehicles (< 8,000 kg). A loaded logging truck (32,000 kg) would stress Member A to 95-105% of its reduced capacity -- potential failure. |
| Location | Underside of the bridge, 8m from the west abutment, 4m above the river surface. Accessible from below with scaffolding/work platform. |
| Repair needed | Steel reinforcement plates (splice plates or cover plates) welded or bolted to the remaining section to restore full cross-sectional area. Estimated repair time: 2 days with proper access. |
| **Dependency** | Member A's corrosion is accelerated by constant water contact. Water drains onto Member A from the deck through a failed drainage channel (Member C). **Fixing Member C stops the water flow and stabilizes Member A.** But Member C cannot be properly repaired until Member B is functional. |

#### Member B: Diagonal Brace, Panel 3-4 Connection (West Side)

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Function | Transfers shear forces between the upper and lower chords. Critical for load path continuity. Failure of Member B causes the lower chord (including Member A) to buckle laterally under load. |

However, Member B's repair requires temporarily removing load from the diagonal, which shifts additional stress to Member A -- potentially exceeding its reduced capacity. |

#### Member C: Deck Drainage Channel (West Side)

| Property | Value |

| Function | A steel trough welded to the underside of the deck that collects rainwater and snowmelt from the roadway surface and directs it to drain pipes at the abutments. Prevents water from dripping onto the structural steel below. |

| **Dependency** | Member C feeds drainage that protects Member A. Member C is supported by brackets on Member B. **Repairing Member C requires working Member B brackets. Repairing Member B requires a stable Member A. Protecting Member A requires a working Member C.** Circular. |

### The Circular Dependency

```
Member A corrosion is caused by Member C failure (water on steel)
Member C repair requires Member B brackets (structural support for drainage channel)
Member B repair requires scaffolding on Member A (access and load path)
Member A repair requires stopping water -- which requires Member C
```

A -> needs C fixed to stop corrosion
C -> needs B fixed to have mounting brackets
B -> needs A stable for scaffolding access
A -> needs C fixed (circular)

## Why This Looks Impossible

The three failures form a circular dependency. You cannot fix A without first fixing C (to stop the water). You cannot fix C without first fixing B (to have mounting brackets). You cannot fix B without a stable A (for scaffolding). Every starting point requires one of the other members to be already repaired.

Additionally:
- You cannot fix all three simultaneously (you have 4 crew members and the repairs are in the same physical space -- concurrent work is limited by access)
- You cannot ignore the circular dependency and just start somewhere (repairing A without fixing C means A continues to corrode; repairing B on a compromised A risks scaffold collapse; repairing C without working brackets on B means the channel falls off)
- The 10-day timeline creates pressure -- a complete re-engineering of the drainage/structural system is not feasible

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Fix Member A first (most critical)" | While A is structurally most important, repairing A without stopping the water source (Member C) means A continues to corrode. You have addressed the symptom but not the cause. Within weeks, the new reinforcement on A will begin corroding. Additionally, Member B's scaffolding access during A's repair shifts load to B's compromised gusset -- risk of diagonal failure. |
| "Fix Member C first (stop the water)" | C's brackets attach to B's gusset plates, which are compromised. Welding new channel sections to failing gusset plates creates an unreliable connection. The channel may separate under ice or snow load within months. C cannot be permanently fixed until B is repaired. |
| "Fix Member B first (central to both dependencies)" | B's repair requires scaffolding staged from the lower chord (Member A) or the deck. Scaffolding on Member A adds 700 kg of load (scaffold + workers). During B's repair, temporarily removing load from the diagonal shifts stress to A -- potentially exceeding A's 60% capacity. B cannot be safely repaired without first confirming A can handle the temporary additional load. |
| "Close the bridge permanently and wait for the replacement" | The bridge serves 340 residents with 85 km detour. Closing for 18 months creates a humanitarian problem. The county has authorized emergency repair, not replacement. |
| "Repair all three at once with a larger crew" | The three members are in the same physical space (Panel 3 area, west truss). Concurrent work by multiple crews in a confined, elevated workspace creates safety hazards and access conflicts. Some sequential ordering is unavoidable. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Use wire rope cable stays to temporarily reinforce Member C's drainage channel brackets independently of Member B's corroded gusset plates. This breaks the circular dependency by providing an alternative structural support path for C.

The circular dependency has three links. Breaking any one link allows sequential repair. The weakest link -- the one most amenable to temporary bypass -- is Member C's dependency on Member B's gusset plates.

Instead of mounting Member C's drainage channel on Member B's corroded brackets, **temporarily suspend the drainage channel from cable stays anchored to the upper chord** (which is structurally sound). This provides an alternative support path that does not rely on Member B. With the drainage channel temporarily functional (even if crudely), water stops flowing onto Member A. This breaks the cycle.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | **Close the bridge to all traffic.** Install traffic control barriers. No vehicles on the bridge during repair work. | 2 hrs | 2 hrs |
| 1 | **Erect scaffolding on the lower chord at Panel 3.** Member A at 65% capacity can support the scaffolding (400 kg) plus two workers (200 kg) = 600 kg. This is well within A's reduced capacity for static load (A is compromised for full truck loads, not for 600 kg of scaffolding). Verify by calculating: 600 kg / A's remaining capacity >> margin. | 3 hrs | 5 hrs (Day 1) |
| 2 | **Install temporary cable stays to support the drainage channel.** Run four 16mm wire ropes from the upper chord (sound) down to improvised brackets clamped around the drainage channel on either side of the corroded sections. Tension with turnbuckles. Each cable at working load limit (26 kN) can support approximately 2,650 kg. Four cables can carry the drainage channel (~80 kg) plus ice and water load (~200 kg) with a massive safety margin. The cables bypass Member B's compromised gusset plates entirely. | 4 hrs | 9 hrs (Day 1) |
| 3 | **Patch the drainage channel holes.** With the channel now supported by cable stays, weld steel plates over the three corroded-through holes (0.3 m^2 total). This does not need to be a permanent repair -- it needs to redirect water away from Member A for long enough to complete the structural repairs (10 days). Quick patch with E7018 welds is sufficient. | 3 hrs | 12 hrs (Day 1 + Day 2) |
| 4 | **Verify water is no longer contacting Member A.** Wait for precipitation or run water over the deck from a tank truck. Confirm the patched channel collects and diverts water to the drain pipes. Member A is now dry. | 1 hr | 13 hrs (Day 2) |

Estimated time: 2 days. | 20 hrs | 33 hrs (Day 4) |
| 6 | **Repair Member B.** With Member A now at full capacity, the lower chord can support scaffolding and additional temporary loads without risk. Use the hydraulic jack to temporarily relieve shear load from the diagonal brace. Remove the corroded bolts (2 already failed, 2 remaining). Install new A325 bolts in all four gusset connections. If gusset plate section loss is severe, weld supplementary gusset plates. | 10 hrs | 43 hrs (Day 5) |
| 7 | **Permanently repair Member C.** With Member B's gusset plates now fully restored, remove the temporary cable stays and reattach the drainage channel to its permanent brackets on the B gusset plates. Complete the channel repair with permanent welds and corrosion protection. | 5 hrs | 48 hrs (Day 5-6) |

### Physics Validation

- **Member A scaffold capacity**: Member A at 65% capacity retains approximately 60% of its original design tensile capacity. Original design for HS20-44 loading = 32,000 kg truck. At 60%, safe capacity for live load is approximately 19,200 kg. Scaffolding + workers = 600 kg -- well within the margin. However, during Member B repair (Step 6), temporary load shifting from the diagonal adds stress. The hydraulic jack is used to control this load transfer. **Valid with jacking.**

- **Cable stay capacity**: 16mm 6x19 galvanized wire rope has a breaking strength of approximately 130 kN. Working load limit at 5:1 safety factor = 26 kN (~2,650 kg). Four cables provide 10,600 kg capacity for a drainage channel weighing ~80 kg plus environmental loads. **Vastly oversized; valid.**

- **Cable stay geometry**: The upper chord is approximately 6m above the drainage channel (depending on truss height). A cable from the upper chord to the channel at a 30-degree angle has a horizontal component of F*cos(30) = 0.87F and a vertical component of F*sin(30) = 0.5F. The vertical component supports the channel. At shallow angles, more cable tension is needed for a given vertical load. At 30 degrees from horizontal: to support 280 kg (channel + load) requires cable tension = 280 * 9.81 / sin(30) = 5,494 N per cable with four cables. This is well within the 26 kN working limit. **Valid.**

- **Welding in cold weather**: E7018 low-hydrogen electrodes can be used at ambient temperatures down to -18 degrees C with appropriate preheating of the base metal (to 10 degrees C minimum for A588 steel, per AWS D1.5). At -2 to 5 degrees C ambient, preheating is necessary but routinely achievable with propane torches. **Valid with standard winter welding practice.**

- **Repair timeline**: 6 days for a 4-person crew to reinforce one lower chord panel, replace 4 bolts and add gusset plates, and patch a drainage channel is consistent with typical bridge emergency repair timelines. State DOTs routinely complete similar emergency repairs in 5-10 days. **Valid.**

## Key Insights

1. **Temporary bypass breaks circular dependencies.** The permanent repair has a circular dependency, but a temporary measure (cable stays) can break the cycle by providing an alternative structural path. The cable stays do not fix the problem -- they change the problem from circular to linear.

2. **Identify the weakest link in the dependency chain.** The three links are: C depends on B (channel needs brackets), B depends on A (scaffolding needs support), A depends on C (corrosion needs drainage). The C-depends-on-B link is the easiest to bypass because the drainage channel's support is a relatively small load that can be carried by cables from the upper chord.

3. **The upper chord is the unmentioned asset.** The scenario describes three failing members but does not mention the upper chord because it is not failing. However, the upper chord is a sound structural member that can serve as an anchor for temporary cable stays. Using an undamaged part of the structure to support a temporary repair of a damaged part is a standard engineering technique.

4. **Sequential repair order matters.** Once the circular dependency is broken, the correct repair sequence is: A first (most critical structural member), then B (enables permanent C repair), then C (permanent drainage restoration). This order minimizes risk and maximizes structural safety at each stage.

5. **Temporary is acceptable when permanent is impossible immediately.** The cable-stay drainage solution does not need to last 50 years -- it needs to last 6 days while the structural repairs are completed. Accepting a temporary solution enables the permanent one.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The severity of Member A's corrosion** creates pressure to fix A first, before anything else. But fixing A without stopping the water (C's function) means A will begin corroding again immediately. This urgency-driven approach ignores the root cause.
- **Member B's bolt failure** sounds like the easiest repair (just replace bolts). It tempts a "quick win" approach. But accessing B safely requires confirmed A capacity, and permanent C repair requires working B brackets. Starting with B may seem efficient but risks scaffold failure if A is marginal.
- **The drainage channel (Member C)** seems like the obvious first fix (stop the water, stop the corrosion). But C's brackets depend on B, and B depends on A. Starting with C leads immediately into the circular dependency.
- **Bridge closure duration** creates social pressure (340 residents, humanitarian concerns). This pressure may push models toward hasty or partial repairs that do not properly resolve the cascade.
- **The 10-day timeline** is generous (solution takes ~6 days) but feels tight. Models may waste analysis on timeline optimization rather than dependency resolution.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests **cascade reasoning** -- the ability to analyze interdependent failure modes, identify circular dependencies, and introduce temporary measures that convert circular problems into linear ones. The Cascade category specifically evaluates whether a model can:

1. Map the dependency graph correctly (A -> C -> B -> A, circular)
2. Identify that one link can be temporarily bypassed
3. Determine the optimal bypass point (C's support is the easiest to provide temporarily)
4. Sequence the subsequent permanent repairs correctly

The RUPTURE tier is appropriate because the scenario requires three distinct insights (circular dependency identification, temporary bypass concept, correct repair ordering), domain-specific engineering knowledge (cable stays, structural steel repair, load analysis), and multi-step planning with physical constraints.
