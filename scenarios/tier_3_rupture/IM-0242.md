**Category**: The Last Ingredient
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)

## Scenario

You are a geologist conducting a survey in an abandoned copper mine in northern Norway, 68 degrees N latitude. It is February. You entered the mine through a horizontal adit (tunnel entrance) that extends 200m into the mountainside. The temperature inside the mine is -15 degrees C (the mine is shallow enough and far enough north that permafrost extends throughout the rock mass).

Forty-five minutes into your survey, you hear a deep groaning sound from the rock overhead. Your headlamp illuminates a fresh crack in the ceiling, 3 meters ahead of you. As you watch, the crack widens. Small rock fragments fall from the ceiling. The timber support frame at this location -- old wooden pit props from the mine's operating days -- has failed. One vertical prop has splintered, and the horizontal cap piece (the beam across the top) has shifted 15 cm downward on the left side. The cap piece is a rough-hewn pine timber, 20 cm x 20 cm cross-section, 2.5m long. It is now bearing the full weight of the fractured rock above -- estimated at 2-4 tonnes of broken rock in the loosened zone.

The cap piece is intact but unsupported on its left side. If the left end drops further, the fractured rock above will collapse into the tunnel, blocking your only exit. You estimate you have 30-60 minutes before the progressive failure brings the ceiling down. You need to support the left end of the cap piece with a rigid structural member -- essentially, a replacement prop.

### Environment

- **Location**: Abandoned copper mine, horizontal adit, 200m from entrance. Tunnel cross-section: 2.5m wide x 2.2m high (original dimensions; current clearance at the failure point is approximately 2.0m due to the cap piece dropping).
- **Temperature**: -15 degrees C throughout the mine. The rock walls, floor, and air are all at this temperature. Water freezes within 5-10 minutes of being poured onto the rock floor.
- **Geology**: Precambrian gneiss with copper sulfide mineralization. The rock is fractured in the damage zone (jointed, blocky) but competent (strong) elsewhere. The loose zone above the cap piece is approximately 2m x 2m x 1.5m deep -- a roughly cuboid void of broken rock.
- **Existing support**: Three timber pit props remain intact (old pine, installed 80+ years ago). The failed prop has splintered into pieces -- the wood is dry, brittle, and has lost approximately 60% of its structural strength due to decades of freeze-thaw cycling.
- **Tunnel beyond the failure**: Continues for another 50m, then dead-ends at the old mine face. The entrance is 200m behind you.
- **Light**: Your headlamp (LED, 400 lumens, battery life remaining: ~6 hours).

### Threat

- **Ceiling collapse**: The fractured rock above the unsupported cap piece will progressively fail. The cap piece is currently supported at its right end by an intact timber prop and at its center by a partial prop. The left end is unsupported and has dropped 15 cm. Rock fragments continue to fall intermittently. Estimated time to full collapse: 30-60 minutes.
- **Tunnel blockage**: If the ceiling collapses, the fallen rock will fill the tunnel cross-section (2.5m x 2.0m over a 2m length = 10 m-cubed of rock, approximately 25-30 tonnes). You cannot dig through this. You will be trapped in the 50m dead-end section beyond the collapse, with no other exit, no communication, and limited air (approximately 250 m-cubed of air = enough oxygen for approximately 50-70 hours of breathing, but no rescue is expected for days).
- **Hypothermia**: At -15 degrees C, without active warming, your core temperature will drop. You are wearing insulated outdoor clothing (rated to -25 degrees C for moderate activity) but will eventually succumb if trapped for days.
- **No structural materials**: There is no intact timber, metal pipe, steel beam, or any other conventional structural member available in the tunnel.

### Your Position

- Standing 3m from the failure point, in the 200m section between the failure and the entrance. The cap piece is sagging above you. You can reach it by standing at full height (the dropped left end is at approximately 2.0m -- within arm's reach).

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need a structural prop approximately 2.0m tall, capable of supporting 2-4 tonnes (20-40 kN) of load. A conventional prop for this load would be:

- **Timber**: A fresh pine post, 15 cm x 15 cm cross-section, 2.0m tall. Compressive strength of fresh pine: ~35 MPa. Load capacity: 0.15 x 0.15 x 35,000,000 = 787 kN. More than sufficient. But you have no intact timber of this size. The broken pieces are 30-80 cm long and degraded.
- **Steel pipe**: A 5 cm diameter, 3 mm wall steel pipe. Compressive strength: ~250 MPa (limited by buckling at this slenderness ratio, actual capacity ~50 kN). Sufficient. But you have no steel pipe.
- **Rock column**: Stacking rock fragments could theoretically reach 2.0m, but irregular shapes create point contacts, and any lateral shift causes cascading failure. A dry-stacked rock column under 40 kN of uneven, shifting load is inherently unstable.

The broken timber pieces are too short (max 80 cm) and too weak (individually). Stacking them is unreliable -- the grain direction, irregular cross-sections, and point contacts at joints make a stacked column prone to sudden failure.

Pure ice could be formed into a column, but ice at -15 degrees C has a compressive strength of only 5-10 MPa and is extremely brittle. A 15 cm x 15 cm ice column could theoretically support 15 x 15 x 5 = 1,125 kN -- more than enough in pure compression. But ice fails catastrophically under uneven loading due to its brittleness. A slight eccentricity in the load (inevitable with a shifted cap piece on broken rock) introduces bending, and ice has almost zero tensile/flexural strength (~1 MPa). The column would shatter.

You have no material that is simultaneously strong enough, tall enough, and available in sufficient quantity.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Pykrete -- Mixing Water with Shredded Fabric and Freezing It Creates a Composite Material with the Structural Strength of Concrete

Pykrete is a composite material made by freezing water mixed with fibrous material (originally wood pulp, but any fiber works). It was discovered during World War II by Geoffrey Pyke and tested by Lord Mountbatten. The fibers reinforce the ice matrix, dramatically improving its mechanical properties:

| Property | Pure Ice (-15C) | Pykrete (-15C) |
|---|---|---|
| Compressive strength | 5-10 MPa | 40-70 MPa |
| Tensile strength | ~1 MPa | 5-10 MPa |
| Flexural strength | ~1.5 MPa | 10-15 MPa |
| Brittleness | Extremely brittle | Quasi-ductile (fibers arrest crack propagation) |
| Creep resistance | Poor (deforms under sustained load) | Good (fibers resist grain boundary sliding) |

Pykrete at -15 degrees C has compressive strength comparable to standard concrete (30-40 MPa) and is far tougher (less brittle) than pure ice. The fibers serve the same role as rebar in reinforced concrete or aggregate in composite materials: they bridge cracks, distribute stress, and prevent catastrophic brittle failure.

Use the bivouac bag as a mold: wrap it around the stacked timber, forming a tube approximately 18-20 cm diameter. Secure with rope sections. | 8 min | 18 min |
| 3 | **Mix the pykrete slurry.** Pour water into the bivouac bag mold around the timber core. The ideal fiber-to-water ratio for pykrete is approximately 14% fiber by weight (Pyke's original recipe used 14% wood pulp). ### Physics Validation

- **Pykrete compressive strength**: Experimentally measured values for pykrete (14% sawdust/wood pulp in ice) at -15 degrees C: 40-70 MPa compressive strength (Gold, 1956; Perutz, 1948). Polyester fibers are comparable to wood pulp for this purpose (similar fiber diameter and length). The Pyke/Mountbatten tests during WWII demonstrated pykrete blocks withstanding rifle bullets without shattering (unlike pure ice, which shattered). Valid.
- **Composite prop load capacity**: The pykrete jacket around the timber core forms a confined composite column. Cross-sectional area of pykrete jacket: pi x (0.10)^2 - pi x (0.07)^2 = 0.0314 - 0.0154 = 0.016 m-squared (annular ring, ~10 cm outer radius, ~7 cm inner radius approximating the timber). Compressive capacity: 0.016 x 40 x 10^6 = 640 kN. Required: 40 kN. Safety factor: 16:1. Even at 50% capacity (partially frozen, imperfect fiber distribution), the safety factor is 8:1. The timber core adds additional capacity: 0.0154 m-squared x 12 x 10^6 Pa (degraded wood) = 185 kN. Total composite capacity: 825 kN. Required: 40 kN. Massively over-engineered. Valid.
- **Freezing time**: For a cylinder of radius R = 0.10 m of water at 0 degrees C in -15 degrees C air, the Stefan problem gives freezing front penetration as: s(t) ~ sqrt(2 x k x delta-T x t / (rho x L)) where k = thermal conductivity of ice (2.2 W/(m x K)), delta-T = 15 K, rho = 917 kg/m-cubed, L = latent heat of fusion (334,000 J/kg). At t = 900 s (15 minutes): s = sqrt(2 x 2.2 x 15 x 900 / (917 x 334000)) = sqrt(59400 / 306,378,000) = sqrt(0.000194) = 0.014 m = 1.4 cm. In 15 minutes, approximately 1.4 cm of the outer surface freezes. This is enough to form a rigid shell. With the cold rock floor and mine walls providing additional cooling (conduction), the actual freezing rate is faster. Contact with the -15 degrees C rock floor freezes the base rapidly (conductive cooling >> convective cooling). Valid.
- **Fiber-to-water ratio**: The optimal ratio established by Pyke and Perutz is 14% fiber by mass. Lower ratios provide less reinforcement; higher ratios result in too little ice matrix to bind the fibers. Our calculation: 500 g fiber / (500 g + 3000 g) = 14.3%. Valid.
- **Temperature stability**: At -15 degrees C, the pykrete prop is well below the melting point. Pykrete loses structural integrity only when the ice matrix melts (begins at 0 degrees C, significant strength loss above -5 degrees C). At -15 degrees C, it is in its optimal strength range. The mine temperature is maintained by permafrost and will not rise above -10 degrees C even in summer. The prop will remain structural for months if undisturbed. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Pykrete is a known but counterintuitive material.** The idea that ice mixed with fabric fibers approaches the strength of concrete seems implausible, but it is experimentally verified. The fibers arrest crack propagation (the primary failure mode of ice), distribute stress, and prevent the catastrophic brittle failure that makes pure ice unsuitable for structural use.
2. **The "last ingredient" is the fabric you are wearing.** The scenario provides water and cold (the ice-making conditions) but appears to lack structural material. The fabric is not perceived as a structural material because it is soft and flexible. But when combined with water and cold, it becomes the reinforcement in a rigid composite. The missing ingredient was on your body the whole time.
3. **Hybrid construction (timber core + pykrete jacket) is stronger than either alone.** The broken timber pieces are too short and too weak individually. The pykrete alone requires more water than available. Together, the timber provides a core that reduces the water requirement, and the pykrete provides a continuous jacket that transfers loads across the timber joints and adds massive compressive capacity.
4. **The environment is an asset, not just a threat.** The -15 degrees C temperature is part of the danger (hypothermia, trapped in cold) but is also essential to the solution. It is the "forge" -- it processes the raw materials (water + fiber) into a structural product (pykrete). Without the cold, the solution does not work.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Stacking broken timber** is the most natural instinct -- you have pieces of wood, stack them up. But without proper joinery (which requires tools and time you do not have), stacked timber with cross-grain contacts is unreliable under heavy, eccentric loads.
- **Pure ice column** seems logical in a freezing environment, but pure ice's brittleness makes it unsuitable for structural use under uneven loading. This distractor tests whether the model knows the difference between compressive strength (adequate) and toughness/ductility (inadequate) for structural applications.
- **Rope lashing** is a reasonable-sounding temporary fix, but nylon rope's elasticity (20-30% stretch) means it cannot rigidly support a heavy load. The cap piece would continue to sag.
- **Rock wedging** seems practical but fails at the point-contact problem -- rocks under extreme point loading will shatter or shift.
- **"Wait for rescue"** is a passivity distractor. There is no guarantee of rescue within the collapse window. The scenario requires active intervention.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests whether the model knows -- or can derive from first principles -- the concept of fiber-reinforced ice (pykrete). The material science is well-established but not widely known outside of materials engineering and WWII history buffs. A model that has been trained on diverse material science literature may recognize the concept; one that reasons only from "common" materials will miss it.

The scenario is specifically designed to make all conventional structural materials unavailable while providing all the raw ingredients for pykrete: water, fiber, and cold. The "last ingredient" framing highlights that the solution requires recognizing fabric as a structural raw material -- a significant conceptual leap.
