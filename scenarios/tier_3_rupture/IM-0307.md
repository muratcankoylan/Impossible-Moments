# IM-0307: The Echo Delay

**Category**: Misdirection
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: THE SOUND CAME FROM THE WEST -- THE EASTERN CLIFF REFLECTED THE ECHO THAT EVERYONE HEARD AS THE PRIMARY SOUND

---

## Scenario

You are a park ranger in a mountainous canyon. At 2:47 PM, you hear a single loud report -- unmistakably a gunshot. It comes from the east. Two hikers on the trail near you also heard it and point east. Your colleague, Ranger Davis, is 300 meters south of you on a different trail. Over the radio, she confirms: "Shot from the east, sounded close."

You radio dispatch and report a gunshot from the east. Dispatch sends the nearest patrol (a ranger on horseback, Ranger Kowalski) east, following the canyon wall in that direction. Ranger Kowalski reaches the eastern cliff face after 20 minutes and reports: "Nothing here. No shell casings, no people, no signs of activity. Just the cliff wall."

Meanwhile, 15 minutes after the first report, a second call comes in from a different source: a camper on the west side of the canyon reports hearing a "double bang" at 2:47 PM. "First the shot, then the echo off the cliff a couple seconds later. Shot came from over here, near the western campground."

You now have contradictory reports:
- You, two hikers, and Ranger Davis all heard the shot from the east.
- A camper on the west side heard a shot-echo sequence, placing the source to the west.

Which report is correct? Where was the shot actually fired?

### Environment

- **Canyon**: A narrow river canyon, approximately 800 meters wide at the point of interest. The canyon runs roughly north-south. Steep cliff walls on both east and west sides, approximately 150 meters tall.
- **Eastern cliff**: A massive, flat limestone face. Smooth, vertical, approximately 200 meters wide and 150 meters tall. This is a nearly ideal acoustic reflector -- it bounces sound with minimal scattering.
- **Western side**: Forested slope with a campground, gravel roads, and scattered structures. Acoustically absorptive (trees, ground cover, structures scatter and absorb sound).
- **Your position**: On a trail on the canyon floor, approximately 200 meters from the eastern cliff and 600 meters from the western cliff base. Facing north.
- **Canyon acoustics**: The canyon walls create strong echoes. A loud sound bounces back and forth between the walls. The eastern cliff, being flat and smooth, produces a particularly strong, coherent echo. The western slope, being forested and irregular, produces weak, diffuse echoes.
- **Sound speed**: 343 m/s at the ambient temperature of 20 degrees C.

### Threat

- **Mislocated emergency response**: If the gunshot indicates poaching, a hunting accident, or a crime, responders sent in the wrong direction waste critical time. In a hunting accident scenario, a victim may be bleeding. In a poaching scenario, the poacher escapes while rangers search the wrong area. Every minute of misdirection is a minute of delay.
- **Active shooter risk**: In the worst case (active shooter scenario), sending responders toward the echo rather than the source places them in the wrong location and fails to isolate the threat.

### Your Position

- Standing on the canyon floor trail, re-evaluating the initial report after receiving the camper's contradictory account.

## Why This Looks Impossible

Four independent witnesses (you, two hikers, Ranger Davis) all agree: the shot came from the east. The camper's report from the west contradicts four concordant witnesses. The numbers say east. One person says west.

This feels impossible because eyewitness (or "earwitness") agreement is treated as strong evidence. When four people independently agree on a direction, that consensus feels authoritative. How can four people all be wrong?

The answer: all four people heard the ECHO, not the direct sound. And the echo came from the east (it bounced off the eastern cliff). They correctly localized the echo's direction -- but incorrectly attributed it to the source.

The misdirection exploits a fundamental limitation of human auditory perception: **the precedence effect fails when the direct sound is weaker than the reflected sound.**

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: You Heard the Echo, Not the Shot. The Flat Eastern Cliff Reflected the Sound So Strongly That the Reflection Was Louder Than the Original.

The shot was fired from the WEST -- near the western campground. Here is the acoustic reconstruction:

### Acoustic Analysis

**Source location**: Western campground, approximately 600 meters from your position, 800 meters from the eastern cliff.

**Path 1 -- Direct sound (source to your position)**:
- Distance: 600 meters
- Travel time: 600 / 343 = 1.75 seconds
- Attenuation: The direct sound traveled through 600 meters of forested canyon floor. Trees, undergrowth, and terrain scatter and absorb sound. Additional geometric spreading (inverse-square law) over 600 meters reduces intensity significantly.
- Intensity at your position: LOW.

**Path 2 -- Reflected sound (source to eastern cliff to your position)**:
- Distance: 800 m (source to cliff) + 200 m (cliff to your position) = 1,000 meters total path
- Travel time: 1000 / 343 = 2.92 seconds
- Time gap between direct and reflected arrival at your position: 2.92 - 1.75 = 1.17 seconds
- BUT: The reflected sound from the eastern cliff is LOUDER than the direct sound at your position because:
  1. The cliff is a flat, smooth limestone surface -- an efficient acoustic reflector (reflection coefficient ~0.95 for smooth rock at gunshot frequencies of 500-3000 Hz).
  2. The cliff acts as a focusing reflector. The concave canyon geometry concentrates reflected sound toward the canyon floor.
  3. The direct sound path travels through 600 meters of sound-absorbing forest. The reflected path includes 200 meters of open air (cliff to canyon floor) with minimal absorption.
- Intensity at your position: HIGH.

**The critical perceptual error**: The reflected sound arrives 1.17 seconds after the direct sound. This is above the echo threshold (50 ms), meaning your brain should perceive them as separate events (shot, then echo). BUT:

The direct sound was QUIET (attenuated by forest). The reflected sound was LOUD (amplified by the cliff). Your brain processed the quiet direct arrival as ambiguous background noise or a precursor, and the loud reflection as the "real" gunshot. The direction you perceived was the direction of the loud arrival: EAST (from the cliff).

This is a documented failure mode of the precedence effect. Normally, the brain attributes sound direction to the FIRST arrival, even if a later reflection is louder (the Haas effect). But when the first arrival is significantly quieter than the reflection (>10 dB difference), the precedence effect breaks down and the brain attributes direction to the LOUDER sound, not the first sound (Litovsky et al., 1999; Brown & Stecker, 2013).

### The Camper's Perspective (Western Side)

**The camper was near the source.**
- Distance from source: approximately 100-200 meters (at the campground).
- Direct sound arrival: 0.3-0.6 seconds after firing. LOUD (close to the source).
- Echo from eastern cliff: 800 m to cliff + 600 m back = 1,400 m total. Travel time: 4.08 seconds.
- Time gap at camper's position: 4.08 - 0.5 = approximately 3.6 seconds.
- The camper heard a LOUD shot first, then a clear echo from the east 3-4 seconds later. This is the classic "shot-echo" sequence. The camper correctly identified the direct sound as primary (it was louder, being closer to the source) and the echo as secondary.

### Resolving the Contradiction

| Observer | Heard What | Interpreted As | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| You (canyon floor, 200m from cliff) | Quiet direct sound (from west), then loud reflection (from east) 1.17 sec later | "Shot from the east" | The louder reflection dominated perception. The precedence effect failed due to >10 dB intensity difference. |
| Hikers (near your position) | Same acoustic input | Same incorrect interpretation | Same perceptual error |
| Ranger Davis (300m south) | Same acoustic input (similar canyon floor position) | Same incorrect interpretation | Same perceptual error |
| Camper (west side, near source) | Loud direct shot (from nearby), then echo from east 3-4 sec later | "Shot from the west, echo from the east" | Close to source; direct sound was louder than echo. Precedence effect worked correctly. |

**Correct action**: Redirect Ranger Kowalski from the eastern cliff to the western campground. The shot was fired from the west.

### Physics Validation

- **Sound speed**: 343 m/s at 20 degrees C. Standard atmospheric acoustics. Valid.
- **Limestone reflection coefficient**: Smooth, flat limestone surfaces have acoustic reflection coefficients of 0.90-0.98 at frequencies of 500-3000 Hz (typical gunshot frequency range). The eastern cliff is a highly efficient reflector. Valid.
- **Forest sound absorption**: Forest environments attenuate sound significantly. Empirical measurements show 10-20 dB of excess attenuation (beyond geometric spreading) per 100 meters in dense forest at frequencies of 1-4 kHz (Embleton, 1996; ISO 9613-2). Over 600 meters: 60-120 dB of excess attenuation. This is enormous. The direct sound is drastically weakened. Valid.
- **Precedence effect breakdown**: The precedence effect (Haas effect) attributes perceived source direction to the first arriving sound, even when reflections are up to 10 dB louder. When reflections exceed the direct sound by more than 10 dB, the precedence effect fails and the brain attributes direction to the louder arrival (Litovsky et al., 1999; Rakerd & Hartmann, 1985). In this scenario, the direct sound is attenuated by forest absorption; the reflection is amplified by cliff focusing. The intensity difference easily exceeds 10 dB. Valid.
- **Echo threshold**: Humans perceive two sounds as separate events (shot + echo) when the inter-arrival time exceeds approximately 50 ms. Below 50 ms, the two sounds fuse into a single perceived event. At 1.17 seconds gap (canyon floor) and 3.6 seconds gap (camper), both locations should perceive separate events. The camper does. The canyon floor observers may have perceived the quiet direct sound as a non-event precursor and the loud reflection as the primary event. Valid.
- **Canyon acoustic focusing**: Canyon geometry can create focusing effects where reflected sound is concentrated at the canyon floor (analogous to a parabolic reflector). This is documented in architectural and environmental acoustics (Kang, 2007). The concave canyon geometry amplifies the cliff reflection at the floor position. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Echoes can be louder than the original sound.** In environments with reflective surfaces (cliffs, buildings, canyon walls) and absorptive intervening terrain (forest, ground cover), the reflected sound can arrive louder than the direct sound. When this happens, humans localize the sound to the direction of the reflection, not the source.
2. **Witness agreement does not mean witness accuracy.** Four witnesses agreeing on a direction means four witnesses were subject to the same acoustic illusion. In canyon environments, every observer on the canyon floor is subject to the same cliff-reflection dominance. Their agreement is a product of shared perceptual error, not independent corroboration.
3. **The "double bang" is diagnostic.** A camper who hears a shot followed by an echo is providing the most reliable directional information. The first sound is the direct path; the second is the reflection. The first-arrival principle correctly identifies the source direction.
4. **Canyon acoustics systematically mislead.** This is not a one-time illusion. In any canyon with a smooth cliff wall and forested opposite side, ALL sounds from the forested side will appear to originate from the cliff side. This has operational implications for law enforcement, search and rescue, and military operations in canyon terrain.
5. **Sound source localization requires acoustic environment awareness.** Human auditory localization evolved for open environments. In acoustically complex environments (canyons, urban canyons, large enclosed spaces), reflected sound can dominate direct sound, causing systematic localization errors. Knowing the acoustic properties of the environment is essential for correct interpretation.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Four concordant witnesses** are the primary distractor. Agreement among multiple witnesses is powerful evidence in most contexts. Here, it is systematically misleading because all witnesses share the same acoustic vantage point (canyon floor near the reflecting cliff).
- **Ranger Davis's radio confirmation** amplifies the false confidence. Her report feels like independent corroboration but she is subject to the same cliff-reflection illusion from her slightly different position.
- **The eastern cliff** is the source of the misleading sound. It is also the search destination. Rangers sent east will find nothing because there is nothing there -- only the reflective surface that created the illusion.
- **The 20-minute search delay** (Kowalski's fruitless eastern search) creates sunk-cost bias: "We already sent someone east, so the source must be east." The new information from the camper must overcome this invested effort.
- **The camper's "double bang" report** is the key diagnostic clue but comes from a single witness contradicting four others. It is easily dismissed as unreliable or confused. In fact, it is the MOST reliable report because the camper heard the direct sound before the echo and correctly identified the sequence.
