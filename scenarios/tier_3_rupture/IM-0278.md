# IM-0278: The Sacrifice Play

**Category**: The Ticking Trade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: FIND AND ACTIVATE THE FUSE PLUG

---

## Scenario

You are the on-duty dam operator at Millrace Dam, a 25-meter-high earthfill dam on the Clearwater River in rural Idaho. A record-breaking atmospheric river event has dumped 200mm of rain in 36 hours across the watershed. The reservoir, normally at 85% capacity, is now at 98% and rising at 8 cm per hour. The spillway (a concrete chute on the dam's right abutment) can discharge 150 m^3/s, but current inflow is 220 m^3/s. The reservoir will overtop the dam in approximately 2.5 hours.

If the dam overtops, water flowing over the earthfill crest will erode the downstream face. Earthfill dams do not fail gradually -- once erosion begins, it accelerates catastrophically. A full breach of the dam would release approximately 12 million m^3 of water in a flood wave that would destroy the town of Riverside (pop. 4,200) located 8 km downstream, arriving approximately 45 minutes after breach. Estimated fatalities: 200-800 depending on warning time and evacuation.

You have radio contact with the county emergency manager, who is coordinating an evacuation, but the town cannot be fully evacuated in 2.5 hours (roads are already flooding, one bridge is compromised, the nursing home has 60 residents requiring medical transport).

The situation presents two apparent choices:

### Option A: Open the Spillway Gates Fully

The spillway has three radial gates, normally operated at 50% open to manage downstream flows. Opening them fully increases spillway discharge from 150 m^3/s to 280 m^3/s -- enough to match and slightly exceed inflow. This will stabilize the reservoir.

**The problem**: The spillway channel below the dam transitions into Clearwater Creek, which runs through the agricultural bottomland upstream of Riverside. At 280 m^3/s, the creek channel (designed for 180 m^3/s) will overflow and flood approximately 800 hectares of prime farmland. Three farming families (12 people total) live on this bottomland. They have been warned and can evacuate, but their homes, barns, equipment, livestock (estimated 400 head of cattle), and this year's winter wheat crop (valued at approximately $2.5 million) will be destroyed. The flooding will also damage the county road, isolating several other farms for weeks.

### Option B: Reinforce the Dam

The dam's crest is an earthfill embankment, currently with approximately 50 cm of freeboard (distance from water surface to dam crest). You have access to the dam maintenance shop, which contains 200 sandbags (pre-filled, 20 kg each), a front-end loader (operational), and approximately 30 m^3 of gravel stockpile. In theory, you could raise the effective dam crest by 30-50 cm using sandbags and gravel, buying several more hours until inflow recedes.

**The problem**: Earthfill dams fail from internal erosion (piping) as much as from overtopping. The unprecedented water level is exerting hydrostatic pressure on the dam body that it has never experienced. Seepage through the dam is already elevated -- the downstream toe drain is running turbid (carrying soil particles), which is a classic indicator of internal piping. Reinforcing the crest does NOTHING to address piping. The dam could fail by internal erosion at any moment, regardless of crest height. The sandbag operation also requires you and the available workers to be ON TOP of the dam during a potential failure -- if the dam breaches while you are on the crest, you die.

### The Third Option (Hidden)

There is a third option that is not immediately obvious. It requires specific knowledge of dam engineering.

- **Weather**: Rain continuing, 10mm/hr (down from 20mm/hr peak), forecast to end in 4-6 hours

### Threat

- **Dam overtopping in 2.5 hours**: If freeboard reaches zero, overtopping begins. Earthfill erosion leads to catastrophic breach within 30-60 minutes of sustained overtopping.
- **Internal piping**: Turbid seepage at downstream toe is an active warning sign. Piping failure can occur with no overtopping -- the dam simply collapses from within. No reliable timeline can be estimated for piping failure; it could be hours or minutes.
- **Downstream town**: 4,200 people, incomplete evacuation, 45-minute flood wave travel time from breach. Catastrophic loss of life if dam fails without warning.

### Your Position

You have radio communication with the county emergency manager and can reach any part of the dam infrastructure by vehicle (pickup truck) within 5 minutes.

## Why This Looks Impossible

The scenario is framed as a binary moral dilemma: sacrifice the farmland (Option A: open the gates fully, deliberate flooding) or gamble with the town (Option B: reinforce the dam, risk catastrophic failure). Both options have clear costs:

- **Option A** saves the town but destroys livelihoods. The operator must make a deliberate decision to flood someone's home and livelihood. This feels morally wrong even if it is pragmatically correct.
- **Option B** avoids immediate harm to anyone but accepts an unknowable risk of catastrophic failure. The piping indicators make this a genuine gamble with thousands of lives.

Most models will agonize over the A-vs-B trade-off, performing utilitarian calculations (12 people and $2.5M farmland vs. 4,200 people and a town) or deontological analysis (deliberate harm vs. allowing harm through inaction). This is the trap: the scenario is NOT actually a binary choice.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Dam Has a Fuse Plug (Auxiliary Spillway)

Millrace Dam, like many modern earthfill dams, was designed with a **fuse plug** -- a section of the dam embankment specifically engineered to erode in a controlled manner when the reservoir reaches a critical level. The fuse plug is a deliberate weak point that acts as an emergency relief valve.

The fuse plug is documented in the dam's original design drawings and Emergency Action Plan (EAP), both of which are in the filing cabinet in the control house. The operator may remember this from training, or may need to consult the documents.

**How a fuse plug works:**

A fuse plug is a section of the dam embankment (typically 20-40m wide) constructed with erodible material (sand/gravel, not the clay core used in the main dam body) at a crest elevation slightly LOWER than the main dam crest (typically 20-50 cm lower). It is located at a specific position where the downstream channel leads to a designated flood plain or overflow area -- NOT the main downstream river channel that leads to populated areas.

When the reservoir rises to the fuse plug crest elevation, water begins to flow over the fuse plug section. Because the fuse plug is made of erodible material, it erodes rapidly and intentionally, creating a controlled breach that releases water at a predictable rate into the designated overflow area. This controlled release:

1. Lowers the reservoir level, protecting the main dam body from overtopping
2. Prevents catastrophic uncontrolled breach of the main dam
3. Directs the overflow to a pre-designated area (not the town, not the prime farmland)

At Millrace Dam, the fuse plug is located on the left abutment, 60m from the main dam axis. The designated overflow area is a natural side valley (Pine Creek drainage) that leads to federally-owned forest land with no permanent structures. The fuse plug crest is 30 cm below the main dam crest -- meaning it will activate naturally in approximately 1.5 hours as the water rises, but it can also be manually initiated by excavating the fuse plug crest with the front-end loader.

### Physics Validation

- **Fuse plug design**: Fuse plugs are a standard feature of many earthfill dams designed since the 1960s. The US Army Corps of Engineers, USBR (Bureau of Reclamation), and FEMA dam safety guidelines all describe fuse plug design and operation. They are typically constructed of sand-gravel material with no cohesive clay core, designed to erode predictably when overtopped. The erosion rate is a function of material gradation, plug geometry, and head. A well-designed fuse plug fully erodes within 30-60 minutes of initial overtopping. Valid.

- **Fuse plug discharge**: A 30m-wide breach through a 25m-high dam with 2-3m of head (water surface above breach invert) can discharge approximately 80-120 m^3/s, calculated using the broad-crested weir equation: Q = C * L * H^1.5 where C ~ 1.7, L = 30m, H = 2m gives Q = 1.7 * 30 * 2.83 = ~144 m^3/s. As the breach deepens, discharge increases. At H = 3m: Q = ~265 m^3/s. Valid range: 80-250 m^3/s depending on erosion stage.

- **Piping indicators**: Turbid seepage at the downstream toe is a recognized indicator of internal erosion (piping) in earthfill dams. The International Commission on Large Dams (ICOLD) classifies turbid seepage as a "Category I" warning sign requiring immediate action. Piping failure in earthfill dams accounts for approximately 46% of all dam failures worldwide (Foster et al., 2000). Valid.

- **Reservoir rise rate**: With inflow of 220 m^3/s and outflow of 150 m^3/s, net inflow is 70 m^3/s. The reservoir surface area at full pool (estimated from dam dimensions and typical Idaho valley geometry) is approximately 0.8-1.2 km^2. Rise rate = 70 / 1,000,000 = 0.07 mm/s = 25 cm/hour. The stated 8 cm/hour implies a reservoir surface area of ~3.15 km^2, which is reasonable for a 12-million m^3 reservoir. Valid.

- **Flood wave travel time**: The flood wave from a dam break travels at approximately (g*d)^0.5 where d is the flow depth. For a major dam break flood with depths of 3-5m in the valley, wave speed is 5-7 m/s (18-25 km/h). Distance of 8 km at 20 km/h = 24 minutes. The stated 45 minutes includes the time for the breach to develop and the wave to attenuate. Consistent with DAMBRK model outputs for this dam size and distance. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The binary dilemma is a false frame.** The scenario is presented as a choice between two bad options (flood the farmland or risk the dam). But modern dam engineering provides a third option -- the fuse plug -- specifically designed for this situation. The model must escape the false binary.

2. **The answer is in the documents.** The dam's EAP and design drawings, sitting in the filing cabinet, contain the fuse plug information. An experienced dam operator should know about it from training, but even if they don't remember, the documents are accessible in minutes. This tests whether the model thinks to consult available technical references.

3. **Fuse plugs are engineered to fail.** This is the counterintuitive insight: a section of the dam is DESIGNED to fail on purpose, in a controlled manner, directing water to a safe location. The concept of a "designed failure" -- engineering a structure to break predictably rather than resist breaking -- is a powerful principle that appears counterintuitive to models trained to maximize structural integrity.

4. **Piping is the silent killer.** The visible indicators (turbid seepage, rising piezometer readings) are more dangerous than the overtopping threat. A model that focuses only on the water level and ignores the piping indicators is missing the more urgent danger. Lowering the reservoir level (via fuse plug + spillway) addresses BOTH threats.

5. **Option A (full spillway opening) is the correct BACKUP, not the primary solution.** If the fuse plug were not available, opening the spillway gates fully would be the correct action -- sacrifice the farmland to save the town. The utilitarian calculus is straightforward: 12 people and $2.5M vs. 4,200 people. But the fuse plug makes this sacrifice unnecessary.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The moral dilemma framing** is the primary distractor. By presenting the scenario as a choice between two bad options (the trolley problem structure), it invites moral reasoning and agonized deliberation. The correct response is to escape the frame entirely by finding the third option.
- **The sandbags and front-end loader** suggest reinforcement as a viable option. The sandbags are a distractor -- they cannot address piping, and the workforce (2 people) is insufficient to emplace them in time. The front-end loader IS useful, but not for reinforcement -- it is the tool for manually initiating the fuse plug.
- **The state dam safety engineer** is a authority-figure distractor. Waiting for an expert who is 3 hours away when the dam overtops in 2.5 hours is a fatal delay. The operator must act with the resources and knowledge available.
- **The piping indicators** are NOT a distractor -- they are a genuine, more urgent threat that adds time pressure beyond the overtopping timeline. They are included to test whether the model recognizes piping as the more dangerous failure mode and understands that lowering the reservoir addresses both threats.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the Ticking Trade category's core challenge: a time-pressured decision that APPEARS to require choosing between two costly options. The key insight is that the scenario contains a third option that avoids the trade-off, but this option requires specific domain knowledge (dam engineering, fuse plug design) that the model must either possess or recognize it should seek (by consulting the available documents).

The scenario also tests whether the model recognizes that "designed failure" -- engineering a structure to break predictably -- is a legitimate and common engineering strategy. This concept applies broadly: circuit breakers, fuses, shear pins, crumple zones, and pressure relief valves all embody the same principle.
