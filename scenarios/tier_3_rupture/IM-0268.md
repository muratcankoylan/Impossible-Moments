### Key Insight: Each Detent Position Produces a Different Pendulum Period. The Period Deviation from a Baseline Encodes a Letter.

The inventor's note provides the decoding algorithm:
1. **T = 2 x pi x sqrt(L / g)**: This is the pendulum equation. Each detent position sets a different effective length L, producing a different period T.
2. **"The alphabet begins at the smallest step"**: The "step" is the increment in period between baseline and the encoded value. The smallest step (shortest deviation from baseline) corresponds to A.
3. **"A = 1"**: The first letter of the alphabet (A) corresponds to the smallest increment above some baseline.

### Physics Validation

- **Pendulum period equation**: T = 2 x pi x sqrt(L / g) is the standard small-angle approximation for a simple pendulum. Valid for angular amplitudes less than approximately 15 degrees (error < 0.5%). Grandfather clock pendulums typically swing with amplitudes of 2-6 degrees, well within the small-angle regime. Valid.
- **Baseline pendulum length**: For T = 2.000 s (full period), L = g x (T / (2 x pi))^2 = 9.81 x (2.000 / 6.2832)^2 = 9.81 x 0.1013 = 0.9940 m. This is the standard "seconds pendulum" length (approximately 1 meter). Valid.
- **Length for O (Delta-T = 0.075 s)**: T = 2.075 s. L = 9.81 x (2.075 / 6.2832)^2 = 9.81 x 0.1090 = 1.0695 m. The bob is approximately 7.5 cm lower than baseline. This is a physically achievable adjustment on a 1.1 m pendulum rod. Valid.
- **Length for A (Delta-T = 0.005 s)**: T = 2.005 s. L = 9.81 x (2.005 / 6.2832)^2 = 9.81 x 0.10182 = 0.9989 m. The bob is approximately 4.9 mm lower than baseline. This is a very fine adjustment -- achievable with precision machined detents but at the limit of what can be measured by timing 10 swings with a handheld stopwatch (period difference of 0.005 s over a single swing, or 0.05 s over 10 swings, is detectable with practice). Valid but challenging to measure.
- **Measurement precision**: Timing 10 swings with a handheld stopwatch has a precision limited by human reaction time (approximately +/- 0.1 s for start/stop, giving +/- 0.2 s total for the 10-swing measurement, or +/- 0.02 s per swing). This is sufficient to distinguish 0.005 s steps if measurements are repeated and averaged. Taking 3 sets of 10 swings and averaging gives precision of approximately +/- 0.012 s per swing -- marginal for the smallest step. The solver may need to take multiple measurement sets for the critical positions. Valid with careful technique.

## Key Insights

1. **The "defect" is the message.** The clock's poor timekeeping is not a flaw -- it is the communication channel. A solver who tries to "fix" the clock destroys the message. This is the core insight: recognize that apparent dysfunction can be intentional encoding.
2. **Physics provides the cipher key.** The pendulum equation converts between a measurable physical quantity (period) and a geometric parameter (length). The inventor used this relationship to embed information in a physical object. The cipher is not arbitrary -- it is grounded in Newtonian mechanics.
3. **The note is the Rosetta Stone.** Without the inventor's note, the cipher is unbreakable (26 possible alphabets, unknown step size, unknown baseline). The note provides three critical pieces: the equation (establishing the encoding domain), the step definition ("smallest step"), and the mapping ("A = 1"). With these, the cipher is straightforward.
4. **Baselines are separators.** The positions at the standard seconds-pendulum length (T = 2.000 s) are "zeros" -- they carry no letter information. They serve as word boundaries or message delimiters, similar to spaces in text or silence in Morse code.
5. **Measurement precision determines decodability.** The smallest step (0.005 s) is near the limit of handheld stopwatch precision. This is intentional -- the inventor designed the cipher to be decodable with basic tools but not trivially. It requires careful, repeated measurement.
