# IM-0260: The Domino Bridge

**Category**: Cascade
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: REINFORCE SUPPORT C (CABLE ANCHORS) FIRST, THEN BRACE SUPPORT B, THEN REPAIR SUPPORT A UNDER REDUCED LOAD

---

## Scenario

You are the lead structural engineer arriving at a highway bridge over a deep river gorge. An earthquake forty minutes ago has damaged three of the bridge's critical supports. Traffic has been stopped. Fourteen vehicles are stranded on the bridge deck, including a school bus with 30 children, because the approaches on both sides have buckled, trapping the vehicles on the span. The bridge is a cable-stayed design: a concrete deck supported by a network of steel cables anchored to towers and abutments.

Your inspection reveals three failing supports, each damaged differently, each connected to the others in a load-transfer chain. The bridge is groaning. Small pieces of concrete are spalling off Support A. You have a structural repair crew with equipment (hydraulic jacks, steel bracing, cable tensioners, concrete epoxy), but you can only work on one support at a time because you have one crew and the work zones are 40-50 meters apart. Fixing them in the wrong order will cause a progressive collapse -- the bridge equivalent of a domino chain.

### Environment

- **Bridge type**: Cable-stayed bridge, single span, 180 meters long. Two towers (pylons), one at each end of the span. Concrete deck, 12 meters wide. Steel stay cables (24 total, 12 per tower) fan from the tower tops to anchor points along the deck edges.
- **Gorge**: 60 meters deep. River at the bottom. No survivable fall.
- **Damage assessment**: Three supports are failing. They are interdependent in a load-transfer chain.
- **Stranded vehicles**: 14 vehicles on the bridge deck, total mass approximately 25,000 kg (including a loaded school bus at ~12,000 kg). The vehicles cannot exit because both approach ramps have buckled.
- **Wind**: 20 km/h, gusting to 35 km/h. Cross-wind to the bridge. Creates oscillatory loading on the cables.
- **Temperature**: 22 degrees C. No thermal stress considerations.
- **Time**: Late afternoon. Estimated 2-3 hours of daylight remaining. Night work is possible with generator-powered lights but is more dangerous.

### Threat

- **Progressive collapse**: If Support A fails, the deck section it holds drops. This transfers its load to adjacent connections, overloading the cables attached to Support B. Support B, already damaged, then fails under the additional load. This pulls the cables from Support C's anchorage (already cracked), causing total cable failure on one side of the bridge. The deck then rotates and collapses into the gorge. Estimated time to complete cascade once Support A fails: 8-15 seconds.
- **Current load state**: Support A is currently holding but deteriorating. Estimated time to spontaneous failure of Support A under current conditions: 45-90 minutes (the earthquake weakened it, and ongoing vibration from wind and vehicle loads is accelerating degradation).
- **Human risk**: 30 children on the school bus, plus approximately 20 adults in other vehicles. Approximately 50 lives at immediate risk.

### Your Position

- Standing at the south end of the bridge, near the base of the south tower. You can see all three damaged supports from this position. Your repair crew (6 workers with equipment) is standing by, awaiting your instructions.

## Why This Looks Impossible

Three supports are failing simultaneously. You can only fix one at a time. Support A is the most obviously critical -- it is visibly cracking, concrete is falling, and it carries the deck that holds 50 people. Every instinct says: FIX SUPPORT A FIRST. It is the most dramatic, the most visible, and the most immediately dangerous.

But fixing Support A first is a potential death sentence.

The three supports form a **load-transfer chain**: A depends on B, and B depends on C. If you fix A but B fails (from wind load while you are working on A for 45+ minutes), the tower sways, cables shift, and the load on C increases. C's cracked anchor block cannot handle additional tension. C fails, the backstays release, the tower tips, and the entire south cable group collapses -- taking the deck (including the now-repaired Support A) with it.

Fixing B first is also wrong. If you stabilize the tower bracing while Support A continues to deteriorate, A may fail during the repair of B (estimated 45-90 minutes before A fails spontaneously). A's failure drops the deck, jolts the cables, and the sudden asymmetric load on the already-damaged tower bracing (even partially repaired) may overwhelm it.

The scenario creates a **temporal logic puzzle** disguised as an engineering problem. The correct repair order is counterintuitive: start with the least dramatic failure (C), work backward through the dependency chain, and repair the most visibly critical element (A) last.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Load-Transfer Chain Must Be Repaired from the Foundation Up. Fix C First, Then B, Then A.

The three supports form a dependency chain: the deck (A) is held by cables, which are held by the tower (B), which is held by the backstay anchors (C). This is a series system -- failure of any element causes total collapse. But the order of repair must follow the load path from the most fundamental element (the anchor) to the most superficial (the deck).

**Why C first:** Support C is the cable anchor block. It is the foundation of the entire cable-stayed system. If C fails, the backstay cables release, the tower tips forward, and all deck support on the south side is lost. No repair to A or B matters if C goes. Conversely, if C is secured, the backstay cables remain taut, the tower remains in position, and the cable support system stays functional. C is the keystone.

**Why B second:** Once C is secure, the backstay cables hold the tower in position. Now the tower bracing (B) can be repaired. With backstays secure, the lateral loads on the tower are partially resisted by the cable geometry even if the bracing is damaged. This gives margin during the B repair. Once B is braced, the tower is fully stabilized.

**Why A last:** With C and B repaired, the entire cable-stayed support system is intact and stable. Support A (the cracked deck beam) is still deteriorating, but the deck is held by the cables above it, not just the beam below it. The cables provide a redundant load path that keeps the deck in position even if A's condition worsens during the C and B repairs. When you finally repair A, you are working on a deck that is securely held by a fully functional cable system.

### Physics Validation

- **Cable-stayed bridge load path**: In a cable-stayed bridge, the deck is supported by inclined cables that carry deck loads in tension to the towers. The towers transfer loads to the foundations through compression. The backstay cables balance the forward-leaning pull of the deck cables by anchoring the tower top to the abutment anchor blocks. This load path (deck -> cables -> tower -> backstays -> anchor block -> foundation) is a series chain. Failure at any point causes cascade failure. Valid.
- **Anchor block as keystone**: The backstay anchor block resists approximately 3,000 kN of tension from 6 backstay cables. If it fragments, the backstays release, removing the horizontal restraint on the tower top. The tower, pulled forward by the deck cables, begins to lean. As the tower leans, deck cable geometry changes (cables approach vertical), reducing their horizontal stiffness. The tower accelerates its lean. Within seconds, deck cables go slack on one side and overload on the other. Structural collapse follows. Fixing C first prevents this cascade at its root. Valid.
- **Tower bracing function**: The bracing in a bridge tower provides lateral stability (resistance to sideways sway from wind and asymmetric loading). With 2 of 4 braces buckled, the tower has reduced lateral capacity by approximately 50%. Wind gusts of 35 km/h create lateral forces of approximately 50-100 kN on the tower. The remaining two braces can handle this under steady conditions but are vulnerable to impulse loads (such as from a sudden deck failure). Valid.
- **Deck beam temporary support**: A hydraulic jack providing 50 tonnes (500 kN) of upward force partially unloads the cracked beam, reducing the shear force driving the crack. This does not repair the beam but slows crack propagation -- analogous to splinting a broken bone. The crack propagation rate is approximately proportional to the shear stress in the beam; reducing the stress by even 30-40% may double the time to failure. Valid.
- **Epoxy cure time and strength**: Structural repair epoxies (e.g., Sikadur, Hilti HIT) achieve approximately 50% of ultimate compressive strength within 30 minutes at 22 degrees C. Full cure (80 MPa) requires 4+ hours. At 50% cure (40 MPa), the epoxy provides significant crack bridging capacity -- sufficient to resist the splitting forces in the anchor block when combined with external I-beam confinement. Valid.
- **Repair timeline**: Each repair phase (C: 35 min, B: 40 min, A: 30 min) plus travel and setup (35 min total) sums to approximately 140 minutes plus 30 minutes for assessment and evacuation. This is within a 3-hour window. Support A's temporary jack support was installed at the 15-minute mark, extending A's estimated failure time from 45-90 minutes to 90-150+ minutes. This provides adequate margin. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Repair the foundation first, not the symptom.** Support A is the most visible and dramatic failure, but it is at the end of the dependency chain. Repairing it first ignores the structural vulnerabilities upstream (B and C) that can destroy the entire bridge regardless of A's condition. The correct instinct is to trace the load path to its origin and secure it there.
2. **Tension elements before compression elements.** Support C (cable anchors) and the backstay cables are in tension. Support B (tower bracing) resists compression and lateral forces. Support A (deck beam) is in compression and shear. In structural repair triage, tension elements are typically highest priority because tension failures are sudden and catastrophic (cables snap, anchors pull out), while compression failures can be gradual and can be shored with temporary supports.
3. **Temporary stabilization buys time.** Placing a single hydraulic jack under Support A in Step 2 is a quick (10-minute) action that slows A's deterioration. This is NOT the full repair -- it is a field-expedient measure that extends A's lifetime while the crew addresses C and B. This "stabilize-and-move-on" approach is critical in multi-failure scenarios.
4. **Progressive collapse is a sequence, not a simultaneous event.** The bridge does not fail all at once -- it fails in a chain: A -> B -> C (or C -> B -> deck). Understanding the sequence reveals the repair priority: interrupt the chain at its earliest point.
5. **Working backward is working forward.** The counterintuitive repair order (C, B, A -- least dramatic to most dramatic) is actually working forward along the load path from the foundation to the deck. This is the structurally logical order, even though it conflicts with visual urgency.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Support A's visual drama** is the primary distractor. Concrete spalling, visible rebar, audible cracking -- A screams "FIX ME FIRST." But A is at the end of the dependency chain. Fixing A while B and C deteriorate is treating a symptom while the disease kills the patient.
- **The stranded school bus** creates emotional urgency that drives models toward "save the children first" responses. While evacuation is important, it is secondary to bridge stabilization. Attempting to evacuate 50 people across a structurally failing bridge before repairs is complete may trigger the collapse that kills everyone. The children are safest when the bridge is stabilized.
- **The buckled approach ramps** create a sense of trapped helplessness -- "we can't get them off the bridge, so we must fix the bridge." This is directionally correct but the order of fixing matters more than the urgency to fix.
- **Splitting the crew** is tempting when three problems exist simultaneously. But undermanned repair teams working on different elements cannot execute structural repairs safely (heavy jacks and beams require multiple people), and simultaneous work on interdependent elements creates coordination risks.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests sequential reasoning under time pressure with emotional distractors. The load-transfer chain (A -> B -> C) creates a temporal logic puzzle: the most urgent-looking problem (A) is the last one that should be addressed, while the least dramatic problem (C -- cracks in a concrete block at the bridge end) is the most critical.

The school bus with children adds emotional weight that can override logical analysis. Models that prioritize emotional urgency ("save the children!") over structural logic ("secure the foundation first, which saves the children more reliably") will produce wrong or dangerous answers.
