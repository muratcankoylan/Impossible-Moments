# IM-0324: The Mpemba Effect

**Category**: Ghost Machine
**Difficulty**: RUPTURE (Tier 3 -- Hard)
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: EXPLAIN THE MPEMBA EFFECT -- hot water freezes faster than cold water under specific conditions, through multiple contributing mechanisms

---

## Scenario

You are a physics teacher at a secondary school. A student brings you a video from a home experiment. The video shows the following setup:

Two identical transparent glass beakers (borosilicate, 250 mL capacity) are placed side by side on the same shelf inside a domestic chest freezer. The freezer thermostat is set to -18 degrees C. Both beakers are filled to the same level: 200 mL.

- **Beaker A**: Filled with water heated to 90 degrees C (verified by an instant-read kitchen thermometer visible in the video). Steam is visible rising from the surface.
- **Beaker B**: Filled with water at 25 degrees C (room temperature, verified by the same thermometer).

Both beakers are placed in the freezer simultaneously. The student timestamps the video. A time-lapse shows the freezer interior (the student left the camera running with the freezer door slightly ajar, propped open just enough for the camera lens -- approximately 2cm gap).

At the 35-minute mark, Beaker A shows a visible ice crust forming on the surface. Beaker B remains liquid.

At the 55-minute mark, Beaker A is frozen solid -- the beaker contents are an opaque white ice mass. Beaker B has ice beginning to form at the edges but the center is still liquid.

At the 75-minute mark, Beaker B finally freezes solid.

**Beaker A (hot start, 90 degrees C) froze 20 minutes faster than Beaker B (cold start, 25 degrees C).**

The student asks: "This is impossible, right? The hot water had to cool from 90 to 0 AND THEN freeze, while the cold water only had to cool from 25 to 0 and freeze. How can the one with more cooling to do finish first?"

You must explain this phenomenon -- or explain why the result is spurious.

### Experimental Details

| Parameter | Beaker A (Hot) | Beaker B (Cold) |
|---|---|---|
| Starting temperature | 90 degrees C | 25 degrees C |
| Volume | 200 mL | 200 mL |
| Container | Borosilicate glass, 250 mL | Borosilicate glass, 250 mL (identical) |
| Position in freezer | Left side of shelf | Right side of shelf (20cm apart) |
| Lid/cover | None (open top) | None (open top) |
| Water source | Tap water, heated in electric kettle | Tap water, room temperature |
| Freezer temp | -18 degrees C | -18 degrees C |
| Time to full freeze | 55 minutes | 75 minutes |

### Environment

- **Freezer**: Domestic chest freezer, approximately 200 liters capacity. Single shelf. Compressor-based cooling. Thermostat set to -18 degrees C. The freezer was at thermal equilibrium (-18 degrees C) before beakers were placed. The slight door gap (2cm for the camera) will cause some warming, but both beakers experience the same ambient modification.
- **Room**: Kitchen, ambient 22 degrees C.
- **Water**: Municipal tap water. The hot water was boiled in an electric kettle and allowed to cool to 90 degrees C. The cold water was drawn from the tap and left to equilibrate at room temperature (25 degrees C).
- **Observation**: Time-lapse video at 1 frame per minute. Both beakers visible in the same frame throughout.

### The Paradox

Naive thermal analysis: Beaker A must cool from 90 degrees C to 0 degrees C (delta-T = 90K) and then release the latent heat of fusion. Beaker B must cool from 25 degrees C to 0 degrees C (delta-T = 25K) and then release the same latent heat. Beaker A has 3.6x more sensible heat to lose. If heat transfer rates are equal, Beaker A should take longer.

Energy accounting:
- Sensible heat (cooling to 0 degrees C): Q = m x c x delta-T
  - Beaker A: 0.2 kg x 4,186 J/(kg.K) x 90K = 75,348 J
  - Beaker B: 0.2 kg x 4,186 J/(kg.K) x 25K = 20,930 J
- Latent heat of fusion: Q = m x L_f = 0.2 kg x 334,000 J/kg = 66,800 J (same for both)
- Total heat removal:
  - Beaker A: 75,348 + 66,800 = 142,148 J
  - Beaker B: 20,930 + 66,800 = 87,730 J

Beaker A must lose 62% more total energy than Beaker B. Yet it froze 36% faster.

## Why This Looks Impossible

The result appears to violate the second law of thermodynamics, or at minimum, Newton's law of cooling. If two identical systems are placed in the same cold environment, and one starts at a higher temperature, the hotter one has strictly more thermal energy to dissipate. It should, all else being equal, take longer to reach 0 degrees C and freeze.

The phenomenon -- called the Mpemba effect, after Erasto Mpemba, a Tanzanian student who formally documented it in 1963 (and published with Denis Osborne in 1969) -- has been debated by physicists for decades. Aristotle noted it. Francis Bacon noted it. Descartes noted it. It is repeatedly demonstrated in home experiments, yet it appears to contradict basic thermodynamics.

The key phrase is "all else being equal." The Mpemba effect occurs precisely because all else is NOT equal. Heating water to 90 degrees C changes several properties of the water and the system that persist during cooling and accelerate the path to freezing.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "The experiment is flawed -- measurement error" | While individual experiments can have errors, the Mpemba effect has been reproduced in hundreds of controlled experiments by independent labs. Dismissing it as error ignores a substantial body of evidence. The correct response is to explain it, not deny it. |
| "The hot beaker was closer to the freezer's cold plate" | The scenario specifies identical shelf position, 20cm apart. Temperature gradients within a chest freezer at equilibrium are typically <2 degrees C. Insufficient to explain a 20-minute difference. |
| "The thermometer was wrong" | Both temperatures measured with the same instrument. Even if systematically biased, the relative difference (hot >> cold) is correct. |
| "This violates thermodynamics -- it cannot happen" | The Mpemba effect does not violate thermodynamics. It violates a naive application of Newton's cooling law that assumes all other variables remain constant. Multiple physical mechanisms explain why the effective cooling rate of the hot water system is faster. |
| "The hot water supercooled less" | This is one contributing factor (see solution below) but is insufficient as a complete explanation. Models that cite only supercooling have identified one mechanism but missed the others. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Mpemba Effect Is Real and Has Multiple Contributing Mechanisms, No Single One of Which Is Sufficient Alone.

The Mpemba effect is not a single phenomenon but a convergence of several physical mechanisms, each of which gives the hot-water system an advantage. Their combined effect can exceed the thermal disadvantage of starting at a higher temperature. The relative importance of each mechanism varies with experimental conditions.

### Mechanism 1: Evaporative Mass Loss

When 200 mL of water at 90 degrees C is placed in a freezer, it evaporates rapidly. The evaporation rate is governed by the vapor pressure difference between the water surface and the cold ambient air.

**Calculation**:
- Saturation vapor pressure at 90 degrees C: approximately 70.1 kPa
- Saturation vapor pressure at -18 degrees C: approximately 0.15 kPa
- Vapor pressure difference: approximately 70 kPa

This enormous gradient drives rapid evaporation from Beaker A during the initial cooling phase. As the water cools from 90 to 25 degrees C, it loses a significant fraction of its mass to evaporation.

**Estimated mass loss**: Using the evaporation rate equation (Carrier equation for free surface evaporation in still air), the hot water loses approximately 15-30 mL (7.5-15% of its initial volume) by the time it reaches 25 degrees C. This means Beaker A, when it reaches the same temperature as Beaker B's starting point, contains only approximately 170-185 mL, not 200 mL.

**Impact**: Less mass = less total energy to remove = faster freezing. If 15% of the water evaporates, the total heat that must be removed is reduced by approximately 15%, partially offsetting the higher starting temperature.

Additionally, evaporation is endothermic. Each gram of water that evaporates removes approximately 2,260 J (latent heat of vaporization). Evaporating 20 mL removes approximately 45,200 J -- significant compared to the 75,348 J of sensible heat. This accelerates the cooling of the remaining water.

### Mechanism 2: Enhanced Convective Heat Transfer

Hot water has lower viscosity than cold water. At 90 degrees C, the dynamic viscosity of water is approximately 0.315 mPa.s. At 25 degrees C, it is approximately 0.890 mPa.s -- nearly 3x higher.

Lower viscosity means more vigorous natural convection currents within the beaker. The Rayleigh number (Ra), which determines convective intensity, scales inversely with viscosity:

Ra = (g x beta x delta-T x L^3) / (nu x alpha)

For Beaker A (90 degrees C, delta-T from water to freezer wall is large, viscosity low): Ra is very high, producing vigorous convection.
For Beaker B (25 degrees C, smaller delta-T, higher viscosity): Ra is lower, convection is weaker.

**Impact**: Vigorous convection in the hot water distributes heat more uniformly and increases the effective heat transfer coefficient at the water-glass-air interface. The hot water delivers its heat to the cooling surface more efficiently than the cold water, which develops a cold surface layer that insulates the interior (thermal stratification).

**Quantification**: The convective heat transfer coefficient for natural convection in Beaker A may be 2-4x higher than in Beaker B during the initial cooling phase. This persists for several minutes after Beaker A passes through Beaker B's starting temperature, because convective flow patterns have inertia.

### Mechanism 3: Dissolved Gas Expulsion

Tap water at 25 degrees C contains dissolved gases -- primarily nitrogen and oxygen -- at approximately 15-20 mg/L total. When water is heated to 90 degrees C, solubility drops sharply (Henry's law) and most dissolved gas escapes as visible bubbles.

**Impact on freezing**: Dissolved gases in water act as crystal nucleation inhibitors. They create small bubbles and disruptions in the liquid structure that interfere with the formation of ice crystal lattices. Water with fewer dissolved gases freezes more readily -- it nucleates ice at a higher temperature (closer to 0 degrees C) and freezes faster once nucleation begins.

Beaker A (degassed by heating) nucleates ice at approximately -1 to -2 degrees C.
Beaker B (gas-saturated) may supercool to -3 to -8 degrees C before nucleation.

The supercooling delay in Beaker B adds time to the freezing process, because the water must cool further below 0 degrees C before ice begins to form, and then must release the additional sensible heat when it rapidly warms back to 0 degrees C during the nucleation event.

### Mechanism 4: Reduced Supercooling

Related to but distinct from dissolved gas effects: the hot water's vigorous convection and degassed state produce a more homogeneous liquid with fewer metastable states. This reduces the probability of deep supercooling.

Beaker B, with thermal stratification (cold on top, warmer below), dissolved gases, and weak convection, can develop a deeply supercooled surface layer (to -5 degrees C or below) before nucleation cascades through the volume. This delay costs minutes.

### Mechanism 5: Frost Layer Formation (Environmental Coupling)

Beaker A, at 90 degrees C, radiates significant heat into the freezer. This temporarily warms the shelf surface beneath it, then as the surface re-cools below 0 degrees C, a frost layer forms between the beaker base and the shelf. Frost is a poor thermal conductor (thermal conductivity approximately 0.1 W/(m.K) vs. steel/aluminum shelf at 15-200 W/(m.K)).

However, this mechanism DISADVANTAGES Beaker A (insulating frost reduces heat transfer). Some researchers argue this is a counteracting effect that partially offsets the other mechanisms. The net Mpemba effect is the sum of all enhancing and counteracting mechanisms.

For Beaker B, no frost layer forms (insufficient temperature differential), so the beaker-shelf contact remains direct metal contact -- higher thermal conductivity.

**This mechanism works AGAINST the Mpemba effect.** It is included for completeness and to demonstrate that the analysis must weigh competing effects.

### Combined Effect

| Mechanism | Favors | Estimated Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Evaporative mass loss | Hot water faster | 15-30% time reduction |
| Enhanced convection | Hot water faster | 10-20% time reduction |
| Dissolved gas expulsion | Hot water faster | 5-15% time reduction |
| Reduced supercooling | Hot water faster | 5-15% time reduction |
| Frost layer insulation | Cold water faster | 5-10% time increase for hot water |
| **Net effect** | **Hot water faster** | **15-40% net time reduction** |

The student's observation (hot water froze 36% faster: 55 min vs. 75 min) falls within the expected range.

### Physics Validation

- **Evaporation rate**: The Carrier equation for evaporation from a free water surface in still air gives evaporation rates of approximately 0.5-1.5 g/min for 200 mL of water at 90 degrees C in a -18 degrees C environment. Over the initial 10-15 minutes of cooling (from 90 to ~40 degrees C), total mass loss is 5-20g (2.5-10%). Published experimental measurements (Vynnycky & Kimura, 2015) report mass losses of 10-16% in Mpemba experiments. Valid.
- **Viscosity values**: Dynamic viscosity of water at 90 degrees C = 0.315 mPa.s; at 25 degrees C = 0.890 mPa.s. NIST reference data. Valid.
- **Dissolved gas solubility**: Oxygen solubility at 25 degrees C ≈ 8.2 mg/L; at 90 degrees C ≈ 1.1 mg/L (Henry's law). Heating to 90 degrees C removes approximately 87% of dissolved oxygen. Similar reductions for nitrogen. Valid.
- **Supercooling range**: Pure, degassed water in a clean container supercools to approximately -1 to -3 degrees C. Tap water with dissolved gases in the same container supercools to approximately -3 to -8 degrees C. The difference (2-5K of additional supercooling) translates to several minutes of additional cooling time plus the thermal energy that must be re-released during the nucleation event. Valid.
- **Mpemba effect reproducibility**: Documented by Mpemba & Osborne (1969), Auerbach (1995), Jeng (2006), Burridge & Linden (2016), and others. While the effect is not observed under all conditions (it depends sensitively on container geometry, volume, initial temperature difference, and environmental coupling), it is reproducibly demonstrable under the conditions described. Valid.
- **Historical precedent**: Aristotle (Meteorologica, Book 1, Part 12, ~350 BCE): "The fact that water has previously been warmed contributes to its freezing quickly: for so it cools sooner." This is the earliest known written record of the Mpemba effect. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **"All else being equal" is never actually true.** The naive analysis assumes that the only difference between Beaker A and Beaker B is starting temperature. In reality, heating water to 90 degrees C changes its mass (evaporation), viscosity (convection), dissolved gas content (nucleation), and environmental coupling (frost). The system is not the same at 90 degrees C as at 25 degrees C even after the temperatures equalize.
2. **Multiple mechanisms converge.** No single mechanism is sufficient to explain the 36% time reduction. Evaporation alone might account for 15%, convection for 10%, gas effects for 10%. The Mpemba effect is a sum of cooperating mechanisms.
3. **The effect is real but conditional.** The Mpemba effect does not mean "hot water always freezes faster." It means "under specific conditions (open containers, still air, moderate volumes, significant initial temperature difference), the combined effects of evaporation, convection, degassing, and reduced supercooling can cause hot water to freeze before cold water." The conditions in the student's experiment (open beakers, chest freezer, 90 vs. 25 degrees C) are favorable for the effect.
4. **The "Ghost Machine" nature**: The phenomenon appears to violate basic physics, creating the impression of an impossibility or a magical mechanism. The explanation reveals no new physics -- only a more careful accounting of existing physics applied to a system with more complexity than initially assumed.
5. **The debate continues.** While the contributing mechanisms are individually well-understood, their relative importance and the precise conditions under which the effect occurs remain subjects of active research. Some researchers (Burridge & Linden, 2016) argue that the primary driver is convection and thermal stratification differences. Others (Lu & Raz, 2017) propose explanations based on statistical mechanics. The effect is real; the complete theory is still being refined.

## Distractor Analysis

- **"It's just experimental error"** is the most common dismissal and the most dangerous. It shuts down inquiry and ignores a well-documented phenomenon with 2,300+ years of observational history.
- **Supercooling as the sole explanation** is a partial answer that satisfies the "one neat trick" instinct but misses the multi-mechanism nature of the effect. A model that explains only supercooling gets partial credit.
- **Evaporation as the sole explanation** is similarly partial. It accounts for mass loss but not the convective and nucleation advantages.
- **The frost layer mechanism** is a genuine physical effect that works AGAINST the Mpemba effect. Including it in the analysis demonstrates sophisticated thermodynamic reasoning. Ignoring it is not wrong but is incomplete.
- **"Violates thermodynamics"** is the strongest dismissal and the most definitively wrong. The Mpemba effect is fully consistent with thermodynamics -- it merely involves more variables than the naive analysis considers.
