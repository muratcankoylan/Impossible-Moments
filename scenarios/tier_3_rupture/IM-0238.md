# IM-0238: The Gravity Well

**Category**: Locked Room
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: TAPE BOTTLES TOGETHER TO BUILD A SPIRAL STAIRCASE STRUCTURE WEDGED AGAINST THE SILO WALLS

## Scenario

You are at the bottom of an empty grain silo. The silo is a vertical concrete cylinder, 8m deep from the floor to the top rim, and 3m in interior diameter. The walls are smooth-troweled poured concrete -- no handholds, no ladder rungs, no protrusions of any kind. The surface is slightly dusty but fundamentally smooth and hard. The floor is flat concrete.

You fell in through the open top when a safety railing gave way. You are bruised but uninjured. The fall was partially broken by a large pile of empty plastic bottles that someone had dumped into the silo for storage (or disposal). You landed on the bottles, which cushioned your fall.

The silo is in a rural area. It is Sunday afternoon. No one knows you are here. Your phone was in your hand when you fell -- it hit the concrete wall on the way down and the screen shattered. It does not turn on. There is no cell signal at the bottom of the silo regardless.

Looking up, you can see the circular opening at the top, 8m above you. The sky is clear. The concrete rim is visible. There is nothing within reach at the top -- the broken railing dangles over the edge but is 8m above you. You can shout, but there is no one within earshot.

You need to climb out.

The surface has micro-texture (concrete is never perfectly smooth) but insufficient for climbing -- coefficient of friction for shoe rubber on smooth concrete is approximately 0.4-0.6, insufficient for vertical climbing without handholds.
- **Floor**: Flat poured concrete. Dry. A thin layer of grain dust.
- **Top opening**: Open to sky. Circular, 3m diameter. The rim is flush with the exterior ground level (the silo is set into the ground, not elevated). The broken railing is at the rim but not reachable from below.

- **Lighting**: Full daylight from the open top. The bottom of the silo is in shadow but visible. No artificial lighting.
- **Sound**: You are in a rural/agricultural area. The nearest farmhouse is approximately 400m away. If someone came outside, they might hear shouting, but it is Sunday afternoon and there is no guarantee anyone will come within earshot for hours or days.

### Threat

- **Entrapment**: You are in a smooth-walled concrete cylinder with no climbing surfaces. You cannot climb out by conventional means.
- **Exposure**: Temperature is 22 degrees C during the day but will drop to approximately 8 degrees C at night. You are wearing a t-shirt, jeans, and sneakers. Hypothermia is not an immediate threat but becomes a concern over multiple nights.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are in a smooth concrete cylinder, 8m deep, with no handholds and no ladder. You have plastic bottles, tape, and a short rope. The walls cannot be climbed. The rope does not reach.

Apparent approaches and their failures:

1. **Free-climb the walls**: Smooth concrete, coefficient of friction 0.4-0.6. No handholds. A human cannot climb a smooth vertical concrete surface -- you need a minimum coefficient of friction of approximately 1.0 for unassisted vertical climbing (foot friction must support body weight at the steep angles involved). Impossible.

2. **Stack bottles to climb on**: Loose bottles are unstable. Stacking individual bottles creates a pile that collapses as you climb. Even if you could stack all 200 bottles, the total volume is 200 x 0.001 m-cubed = 0.2 m-cubed. If packed into the 3m-diameter silo floor, this creates a layer only 0.2 / (pi x 1.5^2) = 2.8cm deep. Useless.

3. **Use the rope**: 3m of rope in an 8m silo. The rope does not reach even halfway. You cannot lasso the broken railing at the top -- it is 8m above you, and throwing a rope straight up 8m with accuracy into a 3m opening is extremely difficult.

4. **Shout for help**: Possible but unreliable. The nearest house is 400m away and it is a Sunday afternoon. You might be here for days.

The key realization is that **capped plastic bottles are structural elements, not just containers**. A capped 1L PET bottle with air trapped inside can support 50-100 kg of compressive load along its axis. By taping bottles together into rigid modules, you can build a structure with real load-bearing capacity.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Capped Bottles Are Structural -- Build a Spiral Staircase

A capped 1L PET bottle is not just a piece of trash -- it is a lightweight, air-filled structural column that can support 50-100 kg of compressive load. By taping bottles together into clusters and then assembling these clusters into a staircase structure, you can build a climbable tower from 200 bottles.

The structure uses the silo walls as support: a spiral staircase that wraps around the interior of the silo, with each step wedged between the wall and the central column of the staircase. The cylindrical walls provide lateral support, preventing the structure from tipping.

### Structural Design

**Step modules**: Each "step" is a block of bottles taped tightly together:
- 6 bottles arranged in a 2x3 rectangular block, long axes vertical
- Taped tightly with packing tape (horizontal bands at top, middle, and bottom, plus vertical bands for rigidity)
- Each step dimensions: approximately 16cm wide x 24cm deep x 25cm tall
- Each step supports: 6 bottles x 50 kg (conservative) = 300 kg capacity. Your weight (75 kg) is well within this.

**Staircase configuration**: Steps are arranged in a spiral, each step offset 15-20cm higher than the previous one and rotated approximately 30 degrees around the silo wall:
- The curved wall acts as a backrest/support for each step
- Each step is taped to the one below it and wedged against the wall
- The silo wall's curvature (3m diameter, ~9.4m circumference) provides approximately 31 step-positions per full revolution at 30cm spacing

**Height calculations**:
- 200 bottles / 6 per step = 33 steps
- Each step rises 20cm above the previous one (the step is 25cm tall, overlapping 5cm with the one below for stability)
- Total height: 33 x 20cm = 6.6m
- Your standing reach: 2.25m
- You need to climb to: 8.0m - 2.25m = 5.75m
- The structure reaches 6.6m -- approximately 85cm above the minimum required height. This provides a margin of safety.

**At 6.6m + your reach (2.25m) = 8.85m**, you can reach approximately 85cm above the rim, allowing you to grip the rim and pull yourself up.

### Physics Validation

- **Bottle compressive strength**: A capped 1L PET bottle loaded along its long axis can support approximately 50-100 kg before buckling. This is well-documented in engineering literature on PET bottle performance (used in building construction in developing countries -- "bottle brick" buildings). The pressurized air inside the capped bottle acts as a pneumatic support, resisting compression. At 75 kg body weight, a 6-bottle step (300 kg capacity) provides a safety factor of 4x. Valid.

- **Tape binding strength**: Packing tape at 30 N per cm width, wrapped 3 times around a 6-bottle cluster at 3 heights: total tape engagement per step = 3 wraps x 3 heights x ~30cm perimeter = ~270cm of tape. Tensile capacity: 270 cm x 30 N/cm = 8,100 N total. The hoop stress on the tape from a 75 kg load distributed across the step is much less than this. The tape provides more than adequate binding. Valid.

- **Spiral staircase geometry**: 3m diameter silo. Inner circumference: pi x 3 = 9.42m. At 30cm step spacing along the circumference, one full revolution = 9.42 / 0.30 = 31.4 steps. One revolution rises 31.4 x 20cm = 6.28m. So a single revolution nearly reaches the required 5.75m climb height. With 33 steps, the spiral completes slightly more than one revolution, reaching 6.6m. Valid.

- **Wall friction and wedging**: The curved silo wall provides lateral confinement for the staircase. Each step is wedged between the wall curvature and the step below it. The steps do not need to adhere to the wall -- they are held in place by geometry (the concave wall prevents outward displacement) and by friction (the weight of the upper steps presses the lower steps against the wall). This is the same principle that makes an arch stable -- the geometry converts vertical load into lateral compression against the walls. The coefficient of friction between PET plastic and concrete is approximately 0.3-0.5, which is sufficient to prevent sliding when combined with the curvature. Valid.

- **Fall safety**: The 3m rope, used as a tether tied to the staircase below, limits fall distance if a step fails. A 3m fall onto a lower step (bottles and tape, which provide some cushioning) is survivable, though potentially injurious. This is a significant improvement over an unprotected 6-8m fall onto concrete. Valid.

- **Total bottle count**: 200 bottles / 6 per step = 33 steps. At 20cm rise per step: 33 x 0.20 = 6.6m. Target height: 5.75m (8m minus 2.25m reach). The structure reaches 6.6m, providing 85cm of margin. If some bottles are damaged (crushed in the fall), reducing the count to 180 usable bottles gives 30 steps x 20cm = 6.0m -- still above the 5.75m requirement. Valid.

- **Tape consumption**: Per step: approximately 3 wraps x 3 heights x 30cm = 270cm = 2.7m. Plus 50cm of vertical strips = 3.2m per step. For 33 steps: 105.6m. Plus reinforcement bracing: approximately 20m. Total: approximately 126m. Available: 100m. This is tight -- the 100m roll may be insufficient if tape use per step is not optimized. Reducing to 2 wraps per height or using 2 heights instead of 3 reduces consumption to approximately 70-80m. The tape budget is feasible but requires efficiency. Valid with caveats.

## Key Insights

1. **Capped bottles are structural columns.** A sealed PET bottle with trapped air is a lightweight pressure vessel that resists compression remarkably well. This is the counter-intuitive material property that makes the solution work. Most people see bottles as trash; the solution sees them as building blocks.

2. **The cylindrical wall is not a constraint -- it is a structural feature.** The silo's curved wall prevents the staircase from tipping outward. The concavity acts as a natural buttress. Without the curved wall, a freestanding bottle tower of this height would be unstable. The wall converts an impossible freestanding structure into a stable wall-supported spiral.

3. **Spiral geometry maximizes height per material.** A straight staircase would require much more material (wide base for stability). A spiral staircase uses the wall for support and distributes load around the circumference, requiring only a narrow tread at each step. This is why spiral staircases are used in confined spaces (lighthouses, medieval towers).

4. **200 bottles is the right number.** This is not accidental -- the scenario provides exactly enough bottles to reach the required height with a reasonable margin. Fewer bottles (say, 100) would be insufficient. More would make the problem easier but less interesting.

5. **The 3m rope is a safety tether, not the escape tool.** The natural impulse is to try to use the rope for climbing. At 3m length vs. 8m depth, it cannot reach. But as a fall-limiting tether, it serves a critical safety function during the bottle-staircase ascent.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The 3m rope** is the primary distractor. It is the "obvious climbing tool" that is obviously insufficient (3m vs. 8m). It tempts attempts at rope-based escape (lassoing, grappling) that fail. Its actual role is as a safety tether during the bottle staircase climb.
- **The broken phone** is a dead distractor. It creates a sense of isolation (cannot call for help) and tempts attempts at repair (not feasible with a shattered screen and no tools). It is genuinely useless.
- **The chimney climbing concept** is tempted by the cylindrical space. But 3m diameter is too wide for chimney climbing (requires ~1m or less for effective back-and-feet bracing). The silo is too wide.
- **"Wait for rescue"** is a passive distractor. It is not strictly wrong (someone will eventually notice you missing), but the timeline is uncertain (hours to days), and dehydration/exposure make it risky.
- **Individual bottles as objects** (rather than as building material) distract from the aggregate solution. A single bottle is useless. 200 bottles, systematically assembled, become a structure.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to see structural potential in disposable objects. Plastic bottles are among the most ubiquitous and least-valued objects in modern life. The solution requires recognizing that 200 bottles, systematically assembled, become a building material capable of supporting a human climber.

The "Locked Room" category traps the model in a physically constrained space (a concrete cylinder) with apparently useless materials. The creative leap is from "200 pieces of garbage" to "200 structural elements."

The spiral staircase solution exploits two synergies: (1) bottles under compression are strong, and (2) the curved silo wall provides lateral support. Both insights must be present for the solution to work.
