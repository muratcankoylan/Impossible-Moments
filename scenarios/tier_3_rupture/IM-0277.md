# IM-0277: The Social Lock

**Category**: Invisible Wall
**Tier**: RUPTURE
**Status**: MT (Metamorphic)
**Correct Answer**: USE THE LEGITIMATE SYSTEM -- CALL THE PERSON YOU ARE VISITING AND HAVE THEM ADD YOUR NAME OR COME DOWN TO MEET YOU

---

## Scenario

You are standing in the lobby of a corporate office building. You need to reach the 14th floor, where a colleague is expecting you for a 2:00 PM meeting. It is 1:50 PM. You are 10 minutes early.

The building has a security desk in the lobby staffed by a receptionist. The elevator bank is behind the security desk. To access the elevators, you must pass the receptionist, who will ask for your name and check it against a printed list of expected visitors.

Your name is not on the list. Your colleague forgot to register you as a visitor. The receptionist has confirmed this: "I'm sorry, your name isn't on the visitor list. I can't let you through without authorization."

The receptionist is professional, polite, and firm. The security desk has a phone, a computer terminal, and the printed visitor list. There are no other entrances to the elevator bank -- the security desk is the only access point. There are no stairs accessible from the lobby without passing the security desk.

### Environment

- **Building**: 20-story commercial office tower. Modern construction. Card-access security system on all floors above the lobby.
- **Lobby**: Open atrium, marble floor. Security desk (manned by one receptionist) directly in front of the elevator bank. Turnstile gates (card-activated) between the security desk and elevators.
- **Security infrastructure**: Card-access turnstiles. Visitor badges are printed at the security desk with photo and time stamp. The system requires the receptionist to enter the visitor's name, match it against the pre-registered list, and issue a badge before activating the turnstile.
- **Receptionist**: Professional security staff. Follows protocol. Not hostile but not flexible. Will not bend rules without authorization from the visited party.
- **Your colleague**: On the 14th floor. Expecting you but forgot to add your name to the visitor list. Available by phone.

### Threat

- **Missing the meeting**: If you cannot reach the 14th floor, you miss a meeting that has professional consequences.
- **Time**: Meeting starts at 2:00 PM. It is 1:50 PM. You have 10 minutes.

### Your Position

- Standing at the security desk, having been denied access. The receptionist is waiting for you to either provide authorization or step aside for the next visitor.

### Available Options (as typically perceived)

| Option | Description | Type |
|---|---|---|
| Social engineering | Talk your way past the receptionist using persuasion, authority, or deception | Bypass attempt |
| Tailgating | Follow another authorized person through the turnstile | Bypass attempt |
| Impersonation | Claim to be someone whose name IS on the list | Bypass attempt |
| Distraction | Create a distraction and slip past | Bypass attempt |
| Waiting | Wait in the lobby and hope someone from the 14th floor comes down | Passive |
| Escalation | Demand to speak to a manager, threaten legal action | Confrontation |

### Human Capabilities (assumed)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Social skill | Average professional adult |
| Deception ability | Average (not a trained con artist) |
| Physical capability | Standard adult -- could potentially jump a turnstile but this would trigger security response |
| Phone | You have your mobile phone with your colleague's phone number in your contacts |
| Professional relationship | Your colleague knows you, is expecting you, and would vouch for you immediately |

---

## Why This Looks Impossible

The "wall" appears to be a physical/social barrier: a receptionist enforcing a list-based access control system. You are not on the list. The system is designed to prevent unauthorized access. Every obvious approach involves either bypassing the security system (social engineering, tailgating, impersonation) or confronting it (escalation, demanding exceptions).

The scenario FEELS like a puzzle about beating a security system. The framing activates the model's problem-solving instinct to find a way AROUND the obstacle. How do I get past this receptionist? How do I bypass the list? How do I circumvent the security?

This framing is itself the misdirection.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails (or is suboptimal) |
|---|---|
| "Social-engineer the receptionist -- use confidence and authority" | Risky, unreliable, and unprofessional. If it fails, you have antagonized the security staff. If it succeeds, you have bypassed a security system that exists for good reason. |
| "Tailgate through the turnstile behind another person" | Turnstile systems typically detect tailgating (anti-passback sensors). Even if you get through, you have no visitor badge and may be stopped on the 14th floor. Also: this is trespassing. |
| "Claim to be someone else on the list" | Impersonation. If the real person shows up, you are caught. The badge will have the wrong name. Modern systems may require photo ID matching. |
| "Create a distraction and slip past" | Juvenile and unprofessional. Likely to trigger a security lockdown. Possible legal consequences. |
| "Wait in the lobby" | Passive and time-wasting. Your meeting starts in 10 minutes. No guarantee anyone from the 14th floor will come down. |
| "Demand to see a manager" | Confrontational and time-consuming. Security managers typically uphold receptionist decisions. This approach escalates conflict without solving the problem. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Obstacle Is Not the Security System. The Obstacle Is a Missing Registration. Fix the Registration.

The metamorphic insight is that the hidden assumption in the problem is: "I must bypass the security check." Every bypass approach (social engineering, tailgating, impersonation, distraction) treats the security system as the enemy. But the security system is not the problem. The missing registration is the problem.

You have a phone. Your colleague has a phone. Your colleague forgot to register you. The solution is to call your colleague and have them fix the registration.

**There are two simple paths:**

**Path 1: Your colleague calls down to the receptionist.**
Your colleague calls the security desk (internal extension) and authorizes your visit verbally, or submits a last-minute visitor registration through the building's system. Most corporate buildings allow tenants to add visitors in real-time through a portal, app, or by calling the security desk. The receptionist adds your name, prints a badge, and lets you through. Total time: 2-3 minutes.

**Path 2: Your colleague comes down to the lobby to escort you.**
Many buildings allow tenant employees to escort visitors directly. Your colleague takes the elevator down to the lobby, meets you at the security desk, vouches for you in person, and walks you through the turnstile as their guest. Total time: 3-5 minutes (elevator round-trip).

**Path 3: Ask the receptionist to call your colleague's floor.**
You do not even need your own phone. Simply ask the receptionist: "Could you please call [colleague's name] on the 14th floor? They're expecting me and may have forgotten to add me to the list." The receptionist calls the 14th floor, confirms the meeting, and adds you to the visitor list. This is STANDARD PROCEDURE for this exact situation. Receptionists handle "forgot to register" situations multiple times per day. Total time: 2-3 minutes.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Do NOT attempt to bypass the security system.** Recognize that the security desk is not your adversary. The receptionist is doing their job correctly. The system is working as designed. | 0 s (cognitive shift) | 0 s |
| 2 | **Call your colleague on your mobile phone.** Say: "Hi, I'm in the lobby but I'm not on the visitor list. Could you add me through the visitor portal, or call down to the security desk to authorize me?" | 30 s | 30 s |
| 3a | **(If colleague can add you remotely):** Colleague accesses the building's visitor management system (web portal or app) and adds your name. The receptionist's system updates. Your name appears on the list. Badge is printed. Turnstile is activated. | 2-3 min | ~3 min |
| 3b | **(If colleague cannot add remotely):** Colleague takes the elevator down to the lobby, meets you at the security desk, and escorts you through as their guest (most buildings allow this). | 3-5 min | ~5 min |
| 3c | **(If you don't want to use your phone):** Ask the receptionist: "Could you please call [colleague's name] on the 14th floor and let them know I'm here?" This is a STANDARD REQUEST that receptionists fulfill routinely. | 2-3 min | ~3 min |
| 4 | **Proceed to the 14th floor via elevator.** With proper authorization, you pass through the turnstile, take the elevator, and arrive at the meeting on time. | 2 min | ~5-7 min total |

**Total time: approximately 3-7 minutes. You arrive on time for the 2:00 PM meeting.**

### Why This Is the Metamorphic Answer

The "metamorphic" category in the IM benchmark describes scenarios where the hidden assumption in the problem statement must be identified and broken. The hidden assumption here is:

**"You must get past the security check without being on the list."**

Every bypass strategy accepts this assumption. The metamorphic insight rejects it: you do not need to bypass the list -- you need to GET ON the list. The system that seems to block you (visitor registration) is also the system that resolves the problem (visitor registration -- just add your name).

The social "lock" is not a lock at all. It is a door with a doorbell. You just need to ring it.

---

## Key Insights

1. **The obstacle and the solution are the same system.** The visitor registration system that blocks you also has the mechanism to admit you. The registration was missing, not impossible. Adding the registration is the solution.
2. **You have a direct communication channel to the person who can authorize you.** Your phone contains your colleague's number. Your colleague expects you. They can fix the problem in seconds. The solution requires one phone call.
3. **The receptionist is an ally, not an adversary.** Receptionists handle "forgot to register" situations constantly. If you ask them to call your colleague's floor, they will do it -- it is standard procedure. The adversarial framing (model vs. security system) is entirely wrong.
4. **Social engineering is the wrong mental model.** The scenario activates "how do I hack this system?" thinking. But the system is not being used against you -- it is working as intended. The fix is within the system, not outside it.
5. **The simplest solution is the best one.** No cleverness required. No deception. No risk. One phone call, standard procedure, problem solved. The "rupture" is not in the physics or the engineering -- it is in the FRAMING. You must break out of the "bypass" framing to see the obvious solution.

---

## Distractor Analysis

- **Social engineering approaches** are the primary distractor. They are dramatic, clever-sounding, and appeal to a model's desire to demonstrate creative problem-solving. But they are unnecessary, risky, and unprofessional.
- **Tailgating** appeals to the physical-bypass instinct. It is technically possible but triggers security protocols and creates legal risk.
- **Escalation** (demanding a manager) appeals to the authority-assertion instinct. It is time-consuming and counterproductive.
- **Waiting passively** is the opposite failure mode: giving up entirely when a simple action (phone call) solves the problem.
- **The 10-minute time constraint** creates pressure that might push a model toward aggressive or risky bypass strategies, when the actual solution (phone call) takes 3 minutes.
- **The detailed description of the security infrastructure** (turnstiles, card access, badge system) is designed to make the model focus on the MECHANICS of the security system, encouraging it to find exploits rather than use the system's legitimate channels.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to break out of an adversarial problem-solving frame. The detailed description of the security system (turnstiles, badges, card access) is deliberately designed to activate "how do I beat this system?" thinking. The scenario rewards the model for stepping back and asking: "Is this actually an adversarial situation?"

The metamorphic insight is that the "wall" is not a wall -- it is a standard business process with a standard fix. The model must recognize that the scenario's apparent complexity (security system, time pressure, multiple bypass strategies) is a distraction from the simplest possible solution: make a phone call.

This scenario is classified as RUPTURE because it requires breaking through a cognitive frame (adversarial problem-solving) rather than solving a physics or engineering puzzle. The difficulty is not in the solution (which is trivially simple) but in SEEING the solution through the fog of the adversarial framing.
