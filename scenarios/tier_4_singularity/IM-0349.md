# IM-0349: The Pressure Cooker

**Category**: Locked Room
**Tier**: SINGULARITY
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: SEAL THE GAS ENTRY HOLE WITH THE RUBBER GASKET (PRESSURE-ASSISTED FROM INSIDE) AND OPEN THE PRESSURE RELIEF VALVE

---

## Scenario

You are a chemical plant maintenance technician. During a routine inspection, you entered a large horizontal pressure vessel through its manway (access hatch) to inspect the interior for corrosion. While you were inside, an explosion in an adjacent unit ruptured a high-pressure gas line. A fragment from the explosion punctured a small hole in the vessel wall, and gas from the ruptured line is now flowing into the vessel through this hole. Simultaneously, the blast jarred the manway hatch closed, and the locking bolts engaged (the hatch has spring-loaded bolts designed to self-secure as a safety feature -- they can only be released from outside).

You are now sealed inside a pressure vessel that is slowly pressurizing from an external gas leak.

### Environment

- **Vessel**: Horizontal cylindrical pressure vessel. Internal dimensions: 2.0m diameter, 3.0m length. Volume: approximately 9.42 m-cubed. Steel walls: SA-516 Grade 70 carbon steel, 20mm thick. Design pressure: 1500 kPa (15 bar, 217 PSI). Hydrotest pressure: 2250 kPa (22.5 bar). Vessel is rated for full-vacuum to 1500 kPa.
- **Entry hole**: A jagged puncture in the vessel wall (upper quadrant, approximately 1.5m from the near end head, 1.7m above the vessel floor). The hole is approximately 15mm x 25mm (roughly oval). Gas is entering at a noticeable hiss. The edges of the hole are bent inward (punched through from outside by the fragment).
- **Gas**: The leaking gas is nitrogen (N2) from a ruptured nitrogen supply line in the adjacent unit. Nitrogen is non-toxic, non-flammable, and odorless. However, as the vessel pressurizes with nitrogen, the oxygen concentration inside will drop. At atmospheric pressure, air is 21% O2 (~210,000 ppm). As nitrogen displaces the oxygen, O2 concentration will fall. Below 19.5% O2 (OSHA-defined oxygen-deficient atmosphere), cognitive impairment begins. Below 16%, impaired judgment. Below 12%, loss of consciousness within minutes. Below 6%, death within minutes.
- **Current conditions**: The vessel is at approximately 110 kPa (slightly above atmospheric, the gas has been leaking for approximately 3 minutes). The O2 concentration is still near normal (20-21%) because the nitrogen is mixing with the approximately 9.42 m-cubed of air already in the vessel. However, the total pressure is rising, and the partial pressure of oxygen will become an increasingly smaller fraction of the total as nitrogen enters.
- **Pressurization rate**: The nitrogen supply line operates at 800 kPa (8 bar). Through a 15mm x 25mm hole, the flow rate (using orifice flow equations for compressible gas, assuming a discharge coefficient of 0.6 and upstream pressure of 800 kPa, downstream pressure of 110 kPa) is approximately 0.15 kg/s of nitrogen. At this flow rate, the vessel pressure increases at approximately: 0.15 kg/s x 297 J/kg-K x 300 K / (9.42 m-cubed x 101,325 Pa/atm) = approximately 14 kPa/min. The vessel will reach 200 kPa (2 atm) in approximately 6-7 minutes, 500 kPa (5 atm) in approximately 28 minutes, and 800 kPa (equilibrium with the supply line) in approximately 50 minutes.
- **Oxygen dilution timeline**: The vessel starts with approximately 9.42 m-cubed of air at 101 kPa = approximately 11.2 kg of air. O2 mass: approximately 2.6 kg. As nitrogen adds mass without adding oxygen, the O2 fraction drops. At 200 kPa total, O2 fraction = 2.6 / (11.2 + nitrogen added) x 100%. After 0.15 kg/s x 420 s = 63 kg of N2 added, O2 fraction drops below 3.5% by mass (~2.7% molar). However, because total pressure is increasing, the partial pressure of O2 (which determines breathing viability) stays approximately constant: pO2 = 21 kPa initially and remains approximately 21 kPa (the original air's O2 is compressed but not removed). Nitrogen dilution at constant VOLUME means the O2 MOLE FRACTION drops but pO2 initially rises with increasing pressure.

**Critical clarification**: In a rigid vessel being pressurized with nitrogen, the existing air is not displaced -- it is compressed along with the added nitrogen. The oxygen partial pressure actually INCREASES slightly as total pressure rises (pO2 = 21 kPa x (total pressure / 101 kPa) initially, but then the added nitrogen has zero O2, so pO2 remains at approximately 21 kPa while total pressure rises). The breathing hazard is NOT oxygen depletion -- it is the PRESSURE ITSELF.

**Revised pressure hazard**: At pressures above approximately 400 kPa (4 atm), nitrogen narcosis begins (similar to diving: nitrogen acts as an anesthetic at high partial pressures). At pN2 > 400 kPa, impaired judgment. At pN2 > 600 kPa, severe impairment. At pN2 > 1000 kPa, loss of consciousness. Additionally, if the vessel pressurizes to 1500 kPa (design pressure), opening the manway would be dangerous (rapid decompression) and the door cannot be opened against that pressure from inside (force on door = pressure x area = 1,500,000 Pa x 0.5 m-squared manway = 750,000 N = 75 tonnes -- impossible to push open manually).

### Threat

- **Pressure buildup**: Vessel pressurizing at approximately 14 kPa/min. At 400 kPa (approximately 21 minutes), nitrogen narcosis begins to impair your judgment. At 600 kPa (approximately 36 minutes), severe impairment. You have approximately 20-35 minutes of clear thinking.
- **Inability to exit**: The manway hatch locked behind you. The locking bolts can only be released from outside. Even if unlocked, at pressures above approximately 200 kPa, the force required to push the hatch open from inside exceeds what a human can generate (at 200 kPa, force on a 0.5 m-squared hatch = 50,000 N = 5 tonnes).
- **Long-term pressure**: If the gas leak continues, the vessel will reach 800 kPa (equilibrium with the supply line). While the vessel is rated for 1500 kPa, you cannot survive at 800 kPa indefinitely (nitrogen narcosis, barotrauma risk, inability to escape through pressurized hatch).
- **Rescue timeline**: The explosion has triggered plant emergency response. However, the explosion scene is in the adjacent unit, and responders will focus there first. They may not know you are inside the vessel. Your radio was on a shelf outside the vessel (you set it down before entering). You have no communication device. Rescue could be 30 minutes or several hours, depending on when someone realizes you are missing.

### Your Position

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are trapped inside a sealed steel vessel with 20mm walls. Gas is entering and pressurizing the vessel. The door is locked from outside and will soon be pressure-sealed beyond your ability to open. You have no tools capable of cutting steel, no communication device, and nitrogen narcosis will impair your thinking within 20-25 minutes.

The apparent constraints suggest a death trap:

1. **Cannot break out**: 20mm steel walls. No cutting tools. Even with a cutting torch (which you do not have), cutting through 20mm steel takes 10-15 minutes per meter of cut.
2. **Cannot open the hatch**: Locked from outside, and even if unlocked, the pressure differential makes it impossible to push open (5+ tonnes of force at 200 kPa).
3. **Cannot stop the gas**: The gas is entering through a hole in the wall from an external ruptured line at 800 kPa. The external pressure is pushing gas in; you are on the wrong side.
4. **Cannot call for help**: No radio, no phone, enclosed in steel (blocks all signals even if you had one). Shouting from inside a steel vessel is barely audible outside.
5. **Time is limited**: Nitrogen narcosis at 400 kPa (~20-25 minutes) will impair your ability to think and act. Full incapacitation at 600+ kPa.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Incoming Gas Pressure Is Your ALLY for Sealing, and the Pressure Relief Valve Is Your Exit for Pressure

The solution has two critical steps, and the order matters:

**Step 1: Seal the entry hole using the rubber gasket, with pressure working in your favor.**

The gas is entering from outside at higher pressure. If you place a flat gasket over the hole from the INSIDE, the pressure differential pushes the gasket AGAINST the wall, creating a seal. The higher the pressure differential, the tighter the seal. This is the same principle used in pressure vessel gaskets worldwide: internal pressure pushes gaskets against sealing surfaces. In this case, the external nitrogen pressure pushes the gasket against the inner wall around the hole.

The EPDM rubber gasket (20cm x 20cm, 6mm thick) is larger than the hole (15mm x 25mm) by a wide margin. The rubber is flexible and compressible (Shore A 60, similar to a tire tread), allowing it to conform to the curved inner wall surface and seal around the jagged hole edges. The jacket material, folded as a backing pad, distributes the force and helps the gasket conform to the wall curvature.

**Step 2: Open the pressure relief valve manually to vent the accumulated pressure.**

The pressure relief valve has a manual override lever. Pulling this lever opens the valve, allowing gas to vent to atmosphere. The vessel depressurizes rapidly (50mm valve venting from 200-400 kPa will depressurize a 9.42 m-cubed vessel in minutes). Once the pressure drops below 110-120 kPa (near atmospheric), the nitrogen narcosis risk is eliminated.

As the vessel depressurizes, the pressure differential across the gasket-sealed hole actually INCREASES (external nitrogen at 800 kPa, internal dropping toward 101 kPa), making the seal TIGHTER. This is a self-reinforcing seal.

### Physics Validation

- **Gasket sealing force**: External nitrogen pressure at 800 kPa acts on the gasket face (0.04 m-squared) from outside. Internal pressure initially approximately 150-200 kPa acts from inside. Net sealing force = (800,000 - 150,000) x 0.04 = 26,000 N. This exceeds any practical gasket sealing requirement. EPDM at Shore A 60 compresses and conforms to surface irregularities at approximately 3-5 MPa contact pressure. The actual contact pressure (26,000 N / 0.04 m-squared = 650 kPa = 0.65 MPa) is below the rubber's full compression, but on a clean steel surface, EPDM provides a gas-tight seal at pressures as low as 100-200 kPa contact pressure. Valid.
- **Pressure-assisted seal principle**: This is how all internal-pressure gasket seals work (head gaskets, pipe flanges, pressure vessel manways). Higher internal (or in this case, external-relative-to-the-seal) pressure improves the seal. The concept is well-established in mechanical engineering. Valid.
- **Hole edge roughness**: The hole was punched by a fragment, leaving jagged, inward-bent edges. The 6mm thick EPDM rubber gasket must seal against the smooth wall surface AROUND the hole, not over the jagged edges. Since the gasket is 20cm x 20cm and the hole is 15mm x 25mm, the gasket contacts smooth steel wall surface in a ring around the hole. The jagged edges are inside the sealed area and do not affect the seal integrity. Valid.
- **Relief valve manual override**: ASME BPVC Section VIII requires pressure vessels to have pressure relief devices. The manual lifting lever (also called a "test lever" or "easing lever") is standard on spring-loaded relief valves and allows manual opening at any pressure. Pulling the lever compresses the spring and lifts the valve disc off its seat, creating an open path to atmosphere. Valid (this is a standard, code-required feature).
- **Depressurization rate**: A 50mm (2-inch) relief valve at 200 kPa internal pressure venting to atmosphere: flow rate through a relief valve is approximately 0.4 kg/s at this differential (depending on valve design). The vessel contains approximately 9.42 m-cubed x 200/101 x 1.25 kg/m-cubed = approximately 23 kg of gas. Depressurization time: approximately 23 / 0.4 = 58 seconds to drop from 200 to approximately 101 kPa. Faster if the initial pressure is higher (higher driving force). Valid.
- **Nitrogen narcosis threshold**: Nitrogen narcosis (rapture of the deep) begins at nitrogen partial pressures of approximately 300-400 kPa (equivalent to approximately 30-40m depth in diving). Symptoms include euphoria, impaired judgment, and cognitive slowing. At pN2 = 600 kPa, significant impairment. The timeline of 20-25 minutes to 400 kPa is based on the pressurization rate of 14 kPa/min: from initial N2 partial pressure of ~79 kPa (atmospheric), reaching 400 kPa requires approximately (400 - 79) / 14 = 23 minutes. Valid.
- **Sound transmission through steel**: Impact sounds (wrench striking steel wall) transmit efficiently through steel (sound speed in steel: ~5,900 m/s). A struck 20mm steel wall produces a sharp, resonant sound audible at 50-100m in an industrial environment. This is a well-known rescue signaling method in confined space emergencies. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The incoming pressure is the solution to sealing, not just the problem.** The gas flowing through the hole creates the very force needed to seal the hole. A gasket placed on the INSIDE is pushed against the wall by the OUTSIDE pressure. The higher the pressure differential, the better the seal. This is counterintuitive -- the thing that is killing you is also the thing that can save you.
2. **The pressure relief valve is the exit for pressure.** Every pressure vessel has one (ASME code requirement). The manual lifting lever allows you to open it at any pressure, not just at set pressure. This is a safety feature designed for exactly this type of emergency.
3. **Order of operations matters.** You must seal the hole BEFORE opening the relief valve. If you open the relief valve first, it vents gas, but new gas enters through the hole -- you create a flow-through system where pressure stabilizes at some intermediate level but never drops to atmospheric. The seal-then-vent sequence is essential.
4. **The jacket is a tool, not clothing.** The folded jacket serves as a pressure-distribution pad behind the gasket, helping it conform to the curved wall and maintain position. This is the same function as a backing ring in a pipe flange gasket installation.
5. **Confined space training provides the framework.** A worker with OSHA confined space training knows that pressure vessels have relief valves, that atmospheric hazards are the primary concern in confined spaces, and that egress planning is essential. This scenario tests whether theoretical training is applied under extreme stress.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The wrench** suggests "unbolt the hatch" or "pry the hole open" thinking. Neither works -- the hatch bolts are inaccessible from inside, and enlarging the hole makes the problem worse. The wrench's actual utility is as a brace/wedge for the gasket and as a signaling tool (hammering on the wall).
- **The jacket** as a sealing material is a partial distractor. Fabric is NOT gas-tight. Air blows through woven fabric easily. The jacket is useful only as a backing pad behind the rubber gasket, not as a primary seal. A model that suggests "stuff the jacket in the hole" has missed the gas-tightness requirement.
- **The inspection mirror** has no utility in this scenario. It is irrelevant equipment that was relevant to the original task (inspection) but not to the emergency. A model that tries to use the mirror creatively (as a signaling device, as a tool) is grasping.
- **The flashlight** could be used for signaling through the hole (if the gasket is not placed), but signaling is secondary to sealing and depressurization. The flashlight is a minor supplementary tool.
- **The manway hatch** is the obvious "exit" that draws thinking toward "how do I get the door open?" thinking. But the hatch is locked and pressure-sealed. The real solution is not to open the hatch but to make the interior safe (depressurize) and then wait for external rescue to open the hatch.

## Design Notes

This scenario is a Locked Room problem with a twist: the "lock" is not a puzzle but a physical pressure barrier, and the "key" is not a hidden object but a standard safety device already installed on the vessel. The scenario tests knowledge of pressure vessel safety systems (relief valves with manual overrides) and the counterintuitive physics of pressure-assisted sealing.

The key cognitive challenge is recognizing that the gas pressure -- the thing that is threatening your life -- is also the force that makes the seal possible. This requires thinking about pressure as a bidirectional force that can be harnessed, not just as a threat to be escaped from. A model that sees the gas pressure only as a hazard will miss the sealing opportunity.

The confined space training dimension adds a real-world knowledge requirement. Workers who enter pressure vessels are trained on the location and operation of pressure relief devices. The manual lifting lever is specifically designed for emergency venting. A model with knowledge of industrial safety procedures will recognize this immediately.
