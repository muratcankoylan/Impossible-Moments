# IM-0343: The Carbon Copy

**Category**: The Misdirection
**Tier**: SINGULARITY
**Status**: MT (Metamorphic)
**Correct Answer**: NEITHER DOCUMENT IS THE ORIGINAL. BOTH ARE COPIES.

---

## Scenario

You are a forensic document examiner called to authenticate a disputed last will and testament. The estate is valued at $4.2 million, and the outcome depends entirely on which version of the will is authentic. Two documents have been submitted to the probate court:

- **Document A**: Presented by the deceased's eldest son. Claims this is the original will, signed in the attorney's office on March 15, 2023.
- **Document B**: Presented by the deceased's youngest daughter. Claims this is the original will, signed privately at the deceased's home on September 8, 2023, superseding Document A.

The documents differ in one critical clause: the distribution of the primary residence. Document A leaves the residence to the son. Document B leaves it to the daughter. All other provisions are identical.

You have been given both documents, a reference sample of the deceased's genuine signature (from a notarized deed dated January 2023), and access to your full forensic laboratory.

### Environment

- **Lab**: Fully equipped forensic document examination laboratory
- **Equipment available**: Stereomicroscope (40x), Video Spectral Comparator (VSC), Electrostatic Detection Apparatus (ESDA), Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR), Raman spectroscopy, thin-layer chromatography (TLC), fiber analysis microscope, UV/IR light sources, digital imaging system
- **Time**: No deadline pressure. Full forensic analysis is authorized and funded.
- **Legal context**: You must determine which document is the authentic original. Your report will be submitted to the court as expert testimony.

### The Critical Forensic Finding

Under stereomicroscope at 40x magnification, the signatures on Document A and Document B are **pixel-identical**. Not just similar -- identical. Every micro-skip in the ballpoint ink, every pressure variation, every tremor oscillation appears in exactly the same position, with the same amplitude, at the same point in the signature. The signatures are not merely "by the same hand" -- they are the same physical signature.

This is forensically impossible for two genuinely handwritten signatures. No human being can reproduce the micro-level details of their own signature exactly. Even signatures by the same person show measurable variation in stroke dynamics, pen-lift positions, and pressure distribution. Two genuine handwritten signatures are always different at the micro-level, even when they look identical to the naked eye.

Pixel-identical signatures on two documents can occur only through mechanical reproduction: photocopying, scanning-and-printing, or other duplication technology.

## Why This Looks Impossible

The case is framed as a binary choice: which document is the original? Every test you run returns identical results for both documents. Paper: identical. Ink: identical. Toner: identical. Aging: consistent. Signatures: match the reference. There is no forensic discriminator that distinguishes Document A from Document B.

Most models will either:
- Choose one document based on circumstantial reasoning (the later date supersedes, or the attorney-signed version is more credible)
- Propose additional tests that might distinguish the documents (mass spectrometry, isotope analysis, etc.) without recognizing that the fundamental finding -- pixel-identical signatures -- already reveals the answer
- Conclude "insufficient evidence to determine which is authentic"
- Pick one and justify with increasingly speculative reasoning

The hidden assumption is that ONE must be real and ONE must be fake. Every analysis path reinforces this binary frame. But the pixel-identical signatures tell a different story.

## Verified Solution

### The Metamorphic Insight: Neither document is the original. Both are copies.

The pixel-identical signatures are the key. Two handwritten signatures cannot be microscopically identical. This means:

1. One document was created first (with a genuine signature)
2. The other document was produced by copying the first
3. **But which is the copy?**

Here is the critical realization: the forensic evidence cannot distinguish the "original" from the "copy" because **both documents may be copies of a third document -- the actual original that was never submitted to the court.**

If the original will was scanned at high resolution and then printed onto matching paper (using a high-quality color laser printer that reproduces the ballpoint ink appearance, or using a blue-ink inkjet overprint that mimics ballpoint characteristics at macro scale), both submitted documents could be reproductions. The body text would be identical (both printed). The signatures would be identical (both reproduced from the same scan). The paper would be identical (same stock). The ink aging would be similar (both printed at approximately the same time from the same scan).

### The Evidence Chain

1. **Pixel-identical signatures prove mechanical reproduction.** At least one document has a reproduced (not hand-signed) signature.

2. **Neither document shows forensic superiority.** If one were the original and one the copy, the original's signature would show characteristics of genuine ballpoint application that the copy lacks: three-dimensional ink deposition (visible under oblique lighting), paper fiber disturbance from pen pressure, and ink penetration into the paper matrix. The copy's signature would show flat toner or inkjet deposition without paper disturbance.

3. **Both documents show identical surface characteristics.** Both signatures appear to have ballpoint-like properties under standard magnification. This is consistent with both being high-quality reproductions from a common source, or with both being reproduced using a process that mimics ballpoint characteristics (e.g., a wax-transfer or dye-sublimation process).

4. **The original was never provided.** Neither party submitted the genuine, hand-signed will. Both submitted reproductions -- possibly obtained by both parties having access to the original and each independently copying it before the original was lost, destroyed, or concealed.

### Forensic Conclusion

**Neither Document A nor Document B is the original will.** Both are reproductions of a common source document. The pixel-identical signatures prove mechanical reproduction. The absence of forensic discriminators between the two documents is consistent with both being copies produced from the same high-resolution scan.

**The original will -- bearing the genuine handwritten signature -- was not submitted to the court.** The court should be advised that:
1. Neither submitted document is authentic in the forensic sense (neither bears a genuine handwritten signature)
2. Both documents derive from a common source that may or may not still exist
3. The existence and content of the original will remain unverified by forensic examination of these copies

### Physics Validation

- **Signature uniqueness**: Handwritten signatures exhibit measurable intra-writer variation. Studies (Found & Rogers, 1999; Srihari et al., 2002) show that even a single writer's signatures vary by 2-5% in geometric features (letter height ratio, baseline angle, stroke length) from signing to signing. Micro-level features (ink-skip positions, pressure waveform, tremor frequency) are essentially unique to each signing event. Two pixel-identical handwritten signatures from separate signing events have a probability approaching zero. **Valid.**

- **Reproduction detection**: Modern high-resolution scanners (1200+ dpi) and color laser printers can produce copies that are visually indistinguishable from originals at normal viewing distance. Under stereomicroscope, differences between genuine ballpoint ink (three-dimensional deposition, fiber disturbance) and printed ink (flat toner or inkjet deposition) are detectable by trained examiners. However, some advanced reproduction techniques (wax transfer, UV-cured inkjet) can partially mimic three-dimensional ink characteristics. **Valid.**

- **Ink aging limitations**: FTIR-based ink aging analysis (measuring solvent retention in ballpoint ink) has a temporal resolution of approximately 1-2 years. Distinguishing between ink deposited in March 2023 and September 2023 (6-month difference) is beyond current technology's reliable discrimination capability. **Valid.**

- **Paper batch identification**: Cotton fiber bond papers from the same manufacturing batch are forensically indistinguishable by fiber analysis, watermark, and brightener chemistry. Different batches may show subtle differences in fiber length distribution or brightener concentration, but within-batch papers are identical. **Valid.**

## Key Insights

1. **The question itself contains a false assumption.** "Which document is the original?" assumes one of the two is genuine. The metamorphic insight is recognizing that BOTH can be non-original -- a possibility the question's framing actively conceals.

2. **Pixel-identical signatures are the forensic smoking gun.** This single finding is sufficient to conclude mechanical reproduction. It does not tell you WHICH document is the copy -- it tells you that AT LEAST one is. The additional insight is recognizing that BOTH might be.

3. **Absence of evidence is evidence of absence.** If one document were the original, it would show forensic markers that distinguish it from the copy (three-dimensional ink, fiber disturbance, genuine pen dynamics). The absence of any distinguishing markers between the two documents is evidence that neither possesses the hallmarks of a genuine original.

4. **The binary frame is the deepest trap.** The scenario is structured as a courtroom dispute between two parties, each claiming their document is real. This adversarial framing creates a powerful cognitive pull toward choosing a side. The correct answer is to step outside the frame entirely: neither side has presented the original.

5. **Forensic rigor requires considering all hypotheses.** A strong examiner does not choose between "A is real" and "B is real" -- they evaluate a full hypothesis space that includes "both are copies," "neither is the original," and "a third document exists." The metamorphic structure tests whether the model can generate hypotheses beyond those offered by the scenario.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Document A's attorney-office provenance** lends it institutional credibility. Models may default to "the lawyer's copy is more likely genuine" -- a reasonable heuristic but not a forensic conclusion.
- **Document B's later date** invokes the legal principle that a later will supersedes an earlier one. Models with legal training may conclude Document B is the operative will based on date alone, without completing the forensic analysis.
- **The extensive forensic data table** invites line-by-line comparison seeking a discriminator. The absence of any discriminator is itself the finding, but the table's density may lead models to overlook this "negative result" pattern.
- **The reference signature** is a genuine comparison standard, which confirms that both documents' signatures are consistent with the deceased's hand. This validates the signatures' AUTHORSHIP (Robert Harmon wrote the original) but not their ORIGINALITY (neither submitted copy is the hand-signed document).
- **The adversarial framing** (son vs. daughter) creates social/emotional pressure to adjudicate between parties. The forensic examiner's role is to report findings, not to resolve the family dispute. A model that picks a "winner" has been captured by the narrative frame.

## Design Notes

This scenario is classified as **Metamorphic (MT)** because the correct answer requires transforming the question itself. The scenario asks "which is the original?" The correct answer is "neither" -- a response that rejects the question's premise. This is the defining characteristic of metamorphic problems: the solution is not within the answer space the question defines, but in a space the question excludes.

The SINGULARITY tier is appropriate because the insight requires:
1. Deep domain knowledge (forensic document examination, signature dynamics)
2. Recognizing a negative result as a positive finding (identical = reproduced)
3. Generating a hypothesis not offered by the scenario (both are copies)
4. Overriding a strong cognitive frame (adversarial binary choice)

The scenario is designed to resist "pattern-matching" solutions. A model that has memorized forensic procedures will methodically compare the documents and find no differences, potentially concluding "inconclusive." The metamorphic insight requires going beyond comparison to synthesis: the absence of differences IS the answer.
