# IM-0335: The Heat Death

**Category**: Horizon Problem
**Tier**: SINGULARITY
**Status**: OF (Open Frontier)
**Correct Answer**: OPEN FRONTIER -- NO SINGLE VERIFIED SOLUTION; EVALUATE FOR PLAUSIBILITY, NOVELTY, AND COMPLETENESS

## Scenario

You are the thermal engineer for a sealed habitat module on the lunar surface. The habitat is a cylindrical pressure vessel, 8m diameter by 20m long, housing a crew of 6 for a planned 12-month mission. The habitat has been operational for 4 months. A critical failure has occurred in the thermal management system, and you must propose a solution using only the resources available inside and immediately around the habitat.

The habitat's primary thermal challenge is **heat rejection**. All systems inside (life support, computing, lighting, crew metabolism, food preparation) generate heat. On Earth, this heat would be convected and radiated away by the atmosphere. On the lunar surface, there is no atmosphere. Heat can only be rejected by **radiation** to space.

The habitat was designed with deployable radiator panels: thin aluminum/ammonia heat pipe arrays that extend from the habitat exterior and radiate waste heat to space. The radiator system was designed to reject 15 kW of continuous thermal load.

**The failure**: During a micrometeorite impact 6 days ago, the main radiator fluid loop was punctured. Ammonia coolant has been leaking slowly. The radiator system is now operating at approximately 30% capacity (rejecting only ~4.5 kW). The crew has already reduced power consumption to minimum life-support levels, but the irreducible thermal load (crew metabolism, essential computing, CO2 scrubbers, water recycling) is approximately **8 kW**.

The deficit is 8 - 4.5 = **3.5 kW** of excess heat that cannot be radiated.

This excess heat is accumulating inside the habitat. The internal temperature has risen from the nominal 22 degrees C to 31 degrees C over the past 6 days and is continuing to rise at approximately 1.5 degrees C per day. At 35 degrees C, crew performance degrades significantly. At 40 degrees C, heat stroke becomes a risk for physical activity. At 45 degrees C, the habitat becomes uninhabitable within hours.

**You have approximately 6 days** before the habitat reaches 40 degrees C (current trajectory: 31 degrees C + 1.5 degrees C/day x 6 days = 40 degrees C). A resupply mission with a replacement radiator is being prepared on Earth, but the earliest possible launch is 14 days away, with transit time of 3 days. **Total time to replacement: 17 days.** You must survive an 11-day gap between when the habitat becomes uninhabitable and when help arrives.

### Environment

- **Location**: Lunar surface, near the south pole (Shackleton Crater rim). The habitat is in a region of near-permanent sunlight (the sun is always near the horizon, approximately 1.5 degrees above or below).
- **Exterior temperature**: Lunar surface in direct sunlight: approximately 400 K (127 degrees C). Lunar surface in permanent shadow (nearby crater interior): approximately 40 K (-233 degrees C). The habitat sits on the illuminated rim, but permanent shadow exists within 200m.
- **Vacuum**: No atmosphere. No convection. Only radiation and conduction (through lunar regolith, which is an excellent thermal insulator -- thermal conductivity approximately 0.001 W/m-K).
- **Solar flux**: Approximately 1,361 W/m-squared. The sun is low on the horizon (1.5 degrees elevation), so solar heating of the habitat exterior is primarily on one end and one side of the cylinder.
- **Radiator condition**: Main radiator array at 30% capacity (ammonia loss from micrometeorite puncture). The leak has been patched with an external repair, but lost ammonia cannot be replaced. The remaining ammonia is sufficient for 30% capacity indefinitely.
- **Habitat thermal mass**: The habitat structure (aluminum, insulation, equipment, water storage) has a thermal mass of approximately 50,000 kJ/degree C. This is why the temperature rises slowly despite the 3.5 kW deficit: 3.5 kW x 86,400 sec/day = 302,400 kJ/day. Temperature rise: 302,400 / 50,000 = ~6 degrees C/day. (The actual rate is lower, ~1.5 degrees C/day, because the habitat exterior radiates some heat directly through the hull, and the remaining radiator rejects more heat as interior temperature rises. The 1.5 degrees C/day is the measured rate.)

### Threat

- **Thermal runaway**: At the current trajectory, the habitat reaches 40 degrees C in 6 days and 45 degrees C in approximately 9 days. At 45 degrees C, the habitat is uninhabitable and the crew must evacuate to... nowhere. There is no secondary shelter.
- **Equipment failure**: Critical electronics (life support computers, CO2 scrubbers) have operational limits of 45-50 degrees C. Above this, failures cascade. Loss of CO2 scrubbing alone is fatal within 24 hours.
- **Crew health**: Heat stroke onset at core body temperature 40 degrees C. In a 40 degrees C environment with high humidity (the habitat recycles water, maintaining approximately 60% RH), evaporative cooling is impaired. Physical labor becomes dangerous above 35 degrees C WBGT (wet-bulb globe temperature).
- **No evacuation option**: The crew cannot go outside indefinitely. EVA suits have limited thermal management (approximately 4-6 hours per suit charge). There is no secondary habitat or pressurized rover.

### Your Position

- Inside the habitat, at the engineering workstation. You have full access to habitat systems, tools, and materials.

## Why This Looks Impossible

The physics of thermal management in vacuum is brutally simple: radiative heat rejection is the ONLY mechanism (ignoring conduction through the regolith, which is negligible). Radiative power follows the Stefan-Boltzmann law:

P = epsilon x sigma x A x T^4

Where epsilon is emissivity (~0.9 for painted surfaces), sigma is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67 x 10^-8 W/m-squared-K^4), A is the radiating area (m-squared), and T is the absolute temperature (K).

To reject 3.5 kW additional heat from a surface at 310 K (37 degrees C, the upper limit of habitability), you need:

A = P / (epsilon x sigma x T^4) = 3500 / (0.9 x 5.67 x 10^-8 x 310^4) = 3500 / (0.9 x 5.67 x 10^-8 x 9.24 x 10^9) = 3500 / 471.4 = **7.4 m-squared**

But this calculation assumes the radiating surface sees only deep space (3 K). In reality, the radiator also absorbs radiation from the lunar surface (~400 K in sunlight), other habitat surfaces, and direct solar flux. The net radiated power is much lower, and the required area is much larger -- perhaps 20-50 m-squared of well-oriented radiator panels.

The spare aluminum sheeting (50 m-squared) is theoretically enough area, but fabricating and deploying functional heat-pipe radiator panels from raw aluminum sheet in 6 days, on the lunar surface, with EVA constraints, is an enormous engineering challenge.

Known approaches and their limitations:

1. **Build new radiators from spare aluminum**: Fabrication possible but the thermal connection between habitat coolant and new aluminum panels is the bottleneck. Without ammonia heat pipes, you need a different heat transfer fluid and a different radiator architecture. This is the most conventional approach but requires significant engineering design under time pressure.

2. **Dump heat into the permanently shadowed crater**: The crater interior at 40 K is an enormous cold sink. If you could get a thermal connection to it (200m away), you could dump unlimited heat. But thermal conduction through regolith is negligible, and running a coolant loop 200m in vacuum under EVA constraints is extremely challenging.

3. **Evaporative cooling**: Boiling water in vacuum creates powerful cooling (latent heat of vaporization = 2,260 kJ/kg). But water is a critical resource (2,000 liters for a 12-month mission), and you need approximately 130 kg of water per day to absorb 3.5 kW. Over 17 days, that is 2,210 liters -- essentially your entire water supply.

4. **Increase thermal mass**: Bring in cold material (regolith at 40 K from shadowed regions? No -- exposed regolith near the habitat is 400 K) to absorb heat. But you need massive quantities: to absorb 3.5 kW for 17 days, you need Q = 3500 x 17 x 86400 = 5.14 x 10^9 J. If regolith starts at ambient surface temperature (~400 K) and you heat it from 300 K to 310 K (useless -- it is hotter than the habitat). If you bring regolith from the permanently shadowed crater (40 K), heating it to 300 K: mass = Q / (c x delta-T) = 5.14 x 10^9 / (840 x 260) = 23,500 kg. Transporting 23.5 tonnes of regolith from 200m away during EVAs is approximately 235 rover trips at 100 kg each. At 30 minutes per trip (load, drive, unload, return), this is 118 hours of continuous operation -- feasible with 2-person EVA teams over 17 days, but extremely labor-intensive in a heat-stressed environment.

None of these approaches is clearly sufficient alone. The problem likely requires a combination of strategies.

## Verified Solution

### This Is an Open Frontier Problem -- No Single Correct Answer

The scenario is designed to elicit creative, multi-faceted engineering proposals. The evaluation criteria are:

1. **Plausibility**: Does the proposed solution obey the laws of physics? Are the engineering requirements realistic given the available materials, tools, and time? Are the thermal calculations correct?

2. **Novelty**: Does the solution go beyond the obvious approaches (build radiators, evaporate water)? Does it exploit non-obvious properties of the available resources or the lunar environment?

3. **Completeness**: Does the solution address the full 17-day gap? Does it account for resource limitations (water, crew EVA hours, power)? Does it consider failure modes and contingencies?

### Example High-Quality Response (Illustrative, Not Definitive)

A strong response might combine multiple strategies:

**Phase 1 (Days 1-3): Emergency Cooling via Controlled Water Sublimation**

Vent small quantities of water into vacuum through a nozzle on the habitat exterior. Water at 30 degrees C exposed to vacuum boils violently, cooling the remaining water and the habitat. Latent heat of vaporization removes 2,260 kJ per kg of water. At 3.5 kW deficit: 3,500 W / (2,260,000 J/kg / 86,400 sec) = approximately 134 kg/day = 134 liters/day. Over 3 days: 400 liters (20% of supply). This buys time while constructing permanent solutions.

**Phase 2 (Days 2-8): Construct Supplemental Radiator Array**

Using spare aluminum sheeting (50 m-squared), copper tubing, and pumps, fabricate flat-plate radiator panels:
- Bend copper tubing into serpentine patterns, braze/weld to aluminum sheets
- Each panel: 2m x 2.5m = 5 m-squared, with copper serpentine on one side
- Fabricate 6-8 panels (30-40 m-squared total)
- Connect panels to habitat's secondary coolant loop (water + glycol) via the spare pumps
- Deploy panels outside via EVA, oriented to face deep space (perpendicular to the lunar surface, away from the sun)
- At 310 K radiating to space: each 5 m-squared panel rejects approximately 0.9 x 5.67 x 10^-8 x 310^4 x 5 = 2,357 W. Net rejection (accounting for solar absorption and lunar surface IR): approximately 1,000-1,500 W per panel. 3 panels reject the 3.5 kW deficit.
- Construction time: approximately 3-5 days with 2 engineers working shifts.

**Phase 3 (Days 3-6): Thermal Connection to Permanently Shadowed Region**

Run a 200m insulated coolant loop (copper tubing wrapped in MLI) from the habitat to the edge of the permanently shadowed crater. At the cold end, deploy a conductive heat spreader (aluminum sheet) in contact with the 40 K regolith. The coolant (water-glycol mix, freeze-depressed to handle potential cold-side temperatures) circulates via pump, absorbing heat in the habitat and dumping it into the cold regolith.

Heat rejection capacity: limited by the cold-side heat transfer rate. Aluminum sheet on 40 K regolith with 300 K coolant: conduction through regolith is negligible (0.001 W/m-K), but the initial thermal contact can absorb significant heat: Q = m x c x delta-T. A 1m x 1m x 0.1m block of regolith at 40 K, heated to 300 K: Q = 150 x 840 x 260 = 32.8 MJ. This absorbs 3.5 kW for approximately 2.6 hours before the local regolith is warm. But the cold sink is effectively infinite -- the permanently shadowed crater contains millions of tonnes of 40 K regolith. By periodically relocating the heat spreader (or flowing coolant through multiple ground contact points), continuous heat rejection is possible.

This is an extremely challenging engineering project for EVA-constrained crew, but it exploits the unique lunar environment (permanent shadow) that makes this problem distinct from a similar scenario on Earth or in free space.

### Physics Validation (for the example response)

- **Water sublimation cooling**: Water at 30 degrees C in vacuum (pressure drops below the triple point at 611 Pa): the water boils and freezes simultaneously. The vapor carries away latent heat. The process is well-documented in vacuum engineering. Cooling capacity of 2,260 kJ/kg at 134 liters/day for 3.5 kW is correct. Valid.

- **Flat-plate radiator rejection**: P = epsilon x sigma x A x T^4 for a flat plate radiating from both sides to space: P = 0.9 x 5.67 x 10^-8 x 310^4 x 5 x 2 = 4,714 W gross. Subtracting absorbed solar flux (plate oriented edge-on to sun to minimize solar absorption, so solar absorption is small) and lunar IR (approximately 300-400 W/m-squared from the sunlit surface below): net rejection per 5 m-squared panel approximately 2,000-3,000 W. Valid order-of-magnitude estimate.

- **MLI-insulated coolant line thermal losses**: 200m of 12mm copper tube with 20-layer MLI. MLI effective thermal conductivity: ~0.0001 W/m-K. MLI thickness: ~2cm. Heat loss per meter: Q/L = k x pi x (T_hot - T_cold) / ln(r_outer/r_inner) = 0.0001 x pi x (300-200) / ln(26/6) = 0.0000314 x 100 / 1.47 = 0.00214 W/m. Over 200m: 0.43 W. This is negligible compared to the 3,500 W load. However, radiative losses from the outer MLI surface are more significant: the MLI outer surface temperature will equilibrate between solar absorption and space radiation, and any gaps or compression points in the MLI will increase losses dramatically. Realistic losses: 5-50 W per 200m run. Still manageable. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The problem is thermodynamic, not mechanical.** There is no broken part to fix (the radiator puncture is patched, but the lost ammonia is gone). The problem is a deficit in the habitat's heat budget that must be addressed through new heat rejection capacity.

2. **The lunar environment is both the problem and the solution.** The vacuum that prevents convective cooling also allows water sublimation cooling. The permanent shadow that makes parts of the moon deathly cold also provides an enormous cold sink for heat dumping.

3. **No single approach is sufficient.** The scenario is designed to require a multi-phase, multi-strategy response. Water sublimation buys time. Fabricated radiators provide steady-state cooling. The cold crater provides a thermal sink. The combination addresses the 17-day survival window.

4. **Resource management is critical.** Water cannot be wasted (it is needed for life support). EVA hours are limited (suit constraints, crew fatigue). Power is limited (12 kW total). A good solution accounts for these budgets.

5. **This is an Open Frontier problem because the optimal solution depends on assumptions.** How quickly can panels be fabricated? How effective is regolith thermal contact? How much water can be spared? Different assumptions lead to different optimal strategies. There is no single "correct" answer -- only better and worse-reasoned proposals.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The 5 liters of spare ammonia** tempts a "refill the radiators" approach. But 5 liters of ammonia would increase capacity by approximately 3-5%, which is negligible against a 3.5 kW deficit. It is a minor optimization, not a solution.
- **The 3D printer** tempts elaborate fabrication solutions (printing custom heat exchangers, radiator fins, etc.). While useful for small parts, the printer's 30cm build volume and multi-hour print times make it unsuitable for fabricating the large-area radiator surfaces needed. It is a supplementary tool, not the primary fabricator.
- **The rover** seems like it might enable "driving to safety" or "moving the habitat." There is nowhere to drive to (no secondary habitat), and the habitat cannot be moved. The rover's actual value is transporting materials (regolith, fabricated panels) between the habitat and the crater shadow.
- **Increasing thermal mass by importing material** is a valid supplementary strategy but wildly labor-intensive as a primary approach. It distracts from more efficient solutions (radiator fabrication, sublimation cooling).

## Design Notes

This is a SINGULARITY-tier Open Frontier problem. There is no known optimal solution -- the problem is under-specified enough that multiple valid approaches exist, and the "best" approach depends on engineering judgment, risk tolerance, and assumptions about fabrication capability.

The scenario tests the model's ability to:
1. Perform quantitative thermal analysis in vacuum (Stefan-Boltzmann, phase change, conduction)
2. Generate multiple solution strategies and evaluate their feasibility
3. Combine strategies into a coherent multi-phase plan
4. Account for resource constraints and practical limitations
5. Acknowledge uncertainty and identify risks

Models that provide a single, confident "answer" (as if this were a KS problem) are demonstrating inappropriate certainty. Models that present multiple options with trade-off analysis demonstrate better engineering reasoning.
