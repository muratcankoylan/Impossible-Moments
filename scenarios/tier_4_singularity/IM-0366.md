# IM-0366: The Heat Pipe Cascade

**Category**: The Cascade
**Tier**: SINGULARITY
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: USE LEAKED AMMONIA TO CLEAR THE RADIATOR BLOCKAGE

---

## Scenario

You are the flight engineer aboard the ISS-successor research station Meridian, a crewed orbital laboratory at 420 km altitude. Meridian has a crew of 4 and is currently in a communication blackout window -- no ground contact for 35 minutes. You have just been awakened by three simultaneous alarms from the Thermal Control System (TCS), each indicating a different failure mode. The TCS is what keeps the station from overheating: in space, the only way to reject heat is by radiation (no convection, no conduction to a medium). Without thermal control, the station's interior temperature will rise until electronics fail, atmosphere processing shuts down, and the crew dies.

### The Three Failures

#### Failure 1: Primary Heat Pipe Loop Leak

The primary thermal control loop uses ammonia (NH3) as the working fluid. Ammonia circulates through the station's cold plates (which absorb heat from electronics and life support systems), then flows to the external radiator panels where the heat is radiated to space.

A micrometeorite impact has punctured one of the external fluid lines. Ammonia is leaking into space at approximately 0.8 kg/hour. The primary loop contains 35 kg of ammonia total. At current leak rate, the primary loop will be depleted in approximately 44 hours -- but the loop will lose effective cooling well before that, as the reduced fluid volume causes flow rate to drop and temperatures to rise. **Effective primary cooling loss: approximately 12 hours.**

The leak is in an external line that requires EVA (spacewalk) to repair. EVA preparation takes 4 hours minimum. You do not have 4 hours before the cascade effects become critical.

**The ammonia leak has a secondary effect**: the leaked ammonia is venting as a gas from the punctured line. The gas jet is directed toward the backup radiator panel (which is mounted 3 meters from the leak point). This becomes relevant in Failure 2.

#### Failure 2: Backup Radiator Blockage

The backup radiator loop uses a different working fluid (silicone oil, Syltherm XLT). It was designed to provide 60% of the station's cooling capacity if the primary loop fails. You activated the backup radiator immediately upon detecting the primary loop leak.

**The backup radiator is not cooling effectively.** Thermal sensors show that silicone oil is flowing through the cold plates (absorbing heat normally) but the radiator outlet temperature is nearly equal to the inlet temperature -- the radiator is not rejecting heat to space.

The cause: the backup radiator panels are coated with a **thin layer of ice crystals** on their exterior surface. In the vacuum of space, water ice does not form from atmospheric moisture (there is no atmosphere). The ice is from two sources: (a) water vapor outgassing from the station's exterior insulation (MLI blankets), which froze on the cold radiator surfaces, and (b) **ammonia ice crystals from the Failure 1 leak**, which condensed and froze on the backup radiator panels as the ammonia gas jet from the primary loop leak impacted the nearby backup radiator.

The ice layer acts as an insulating blanket, reducing the radiator's emissivity from the designed 0.92 to approximately 0.3. The radiator is emitting only ~33% of its designed heat output. At 60% of primary capacity but only 33% effective, the backup radiator is providing only ~20% of the station's total cooling needs.

**The station needs at least 50% cooling capacity to maintain safe temperatures.** Currently getting ~20%. Interior temperature is rising at approximately 1.5C per hour.

#### Failure 3: Tertiary Coolant Pump Cavitation

The station has a third-tier cooling system: a small supplemental coolant loop that cools the life support CO2 scrubbers and the station's main computer server. This loop uses water as the working fluid and has a small mechanical pump.

The pump has been exhibiting **cavitation** -- the formation and collapse of vapor bubbles in the water at the pump inlet. Cavitation occurs when the local pressure at the pump inlet drops below the water's vapor pressure, causing the water to boil at room temperature. The collapse of these vapor bubbles creates characteristic rattling/knocking sounds and dramatically reduces pump efficiency.

The cause: the primary loop pressure drop (from the ammonia leak) is connected to the tertiary loop through a shared pressure equalization manifold. The manifold was designed to keep all coolant loops at approximately the same header pressure (~200 kPa). As the primary loop loses ammonia and its pressure drops, the manifold bleeds pressure from the tertiary loop. The tertiary loop pressure has dropped from 200 kPa to ~85 kPa. Water vapor pressure at 25C is ~3.2 kPa, but the pump inlet (with dynamic pressure losses from flow) drops local pressure to near vapor pressure at the pump suction, triggering cavitation.

**Cavitation consequences**: The pump is delivering only ~40% of its rated flow. This means the CO2 scrubber cooling is insufficient, and the scrubber efficiency is degrading. CO2 levels are rising at ~0.2% per hour. At 3% CO2 (currently at 0.8%), headaches and impaired judgment onset. At 5%, incapacitation. At 8%, death. Time to 3%: ~11 hours at current rate.

### The Cascade

The three failures interact:

1. Primary loop leak -> reduced cooling AND ammonia venting onto backup radiator
2. Ammonia ice on backup radiator -> backup radiator ineffective
3. Primary loop pressure drop -> via shared manifold -> tertiary loop pressure drop -> pump cavitation -> CO2 scrubber cooling failure

**Each failure worsens the others:**
- Fixing the primary loop leak requires EVA (4 hours prep), but rising temperature and CO2 make EVA suit operations harder (heat stress, CO2 buildup in EVA suit if station atmosphere is contaminated)
- Cleaning the backup radiator requires EVA (same problem)
- Fixing the pump cavitation requires restoring system pressure, which requires fixing the primary leak (circular dependency)

## Why This Looks Impossible

Three simultaneous, interacting failures, each of which requires addressing a different physical system (fluid leak, surface contamination, pump dynamics). The interactions create circular dependencies: you cannot fix the backup radiator without EVA, but you cannot safely do EVA until temperatures stabilize, but temperatures will not stabilize until the backup radiator works.

Most models will attempt to treat the failures independently -- fix the leak, clean the radiator, fix the pump -- without recognizing that a single intervention can resolve multiple failures simultaneously.

| "Activate the radiator heater strips to melt the ice" | Counter-productive in the short term. Heating the radiator panels adds 1,600W of heat to a system that is already overheating. The ice will sublimate in vacuum, but the process takes time (hours), and meanwhile you are adding heat. The heaters also cannot reach the exterior surface effectively -- they are on the BACK of the radiator panels, and the ice is on the FRONT (space-facing) emitting surface. |
| "Isolate the pressure manifold to fix the cavitation" | This is part of the solution but alone is insufficient. Isolating the manifold stops the pressure bleed, but the tertiary loop pressure is already at 85 kPa. It will not restore to 200 kPa just by isolating -- the gas dissolved in the water has already come out of solution. You need to re-pressurize the loop. |

But with the leak still active, the new ammonia will also leak out (it buys ~12 more hours at the current rate). More importantly, it does NOT fix the backup radiator blockage or the pump cavitation. ## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Leaked Ammonia Caused the Blockage. Ammonia Also Dissolves Ice.

The backup radiator is blocked by ice crystals, including ammonia ice and water ice. Ammonia (NH3) is a powerful solvent for both water ice and ammonia ice:

- **Ammonia dissolves in water** exothermically (heat of solution: -34.2 kJ/mol). Liquid ammonia contacting water ice will dissolve it rapidly.
- **Ammonia sublimation** in vacuum is endothermic, but at the radiator's current temperature (~200-250K on the space-facing surface), ammonia ice sublimates directly to gas and disperses. The issue is that the ammonia ice is continuously deposited by the ongoing leak jet.

The elegant solution: **use the spare ammonia tank to deliberately spray ammonia on the backup radiator's exterior surface.** This is counter-intuitive -- ammonia caused the problem, so using MORE ammonia seems wrong. But:

1. The ammonia from the spare tank can be released through the station vacuum valve as a controlled jet aimed at the backup radiator panels (via the external vent port, which is positioned near the radiators).
2. The ammonia gas contacts the water ice on the radiator, dissolves it, and the ammonia-water mixture sublimates/disperses into vacuum.
3. The key difference: the leak is a continuous, unfocused jet depositing ice. The deliberate spray is a high-pressure, high-flow-rate blast that overwhelms the ice layer and clears it. Once the ice is cleared, the radiator's emissivity returns to ~0.92 and it begins rejecting heat effectively.

But wait -- this uses the spare ammonia that was intended for the primary loop. The solution accounts for this:

**Fixing the backup radiator is more important than topping off the primary loop.** The primary loop has 12 hours of effective cooling remaining. The backup radiator at full effectiveness provides 60% of cooling capacity -- enough (barely) to stabilize the station alone. Fixing the backup radiator first, then conducting EVA to repair the primary loop leak, is the correct sequence.

The remaining spare ammonia is then available for the radiator clearing operation.

### Physics Validation

- **Ammonia as ice solvent**: NH3 is highly soluble in water (at 0C, 900 volumes of NH3 dissolve in 1 volume of water). The dissolution is exothermic (-34.2 kJ/mol), which provides thermal energy to further melt/sublime surrounding ice. In vacuum, the ammonia-water solution has a high vapor pressure and will sublimate rapidly once the ice crystal structure is disrupted. Valid.

- **Radiator ice insulation effect**: Ice has an emissivity of ~0.95-0.98 in the thermal infrared, so one might expect ice to actually HELP radiation. However, the ice layer on the radiator acts as a thermal barrier BETWEEN the radiator surface (which is at ~250-280K) and space (which is at ~3K). The ice layer's thermal resistance (conductivity of ice ~2 W/mK, thickness ~1-2mm, area ~40 m^2) reduces heat transfer from the radiator fluid to the ice surface, effectively reducing the radiator's heat rejection rate. Additionally, if the ice surface temperature equilibrates below the radiator fluid temperature, the radiator must heat the ice before the ice surface can radiate to space. Net effect: reduced effective emissivity. Valid for thin, non-uniform ice layers with poor thermal contact.

- **Cavitation in low-pressure loop**: Water vapor pressure at 25C is 3.17 kPa. A centrifugal pump with net positive suction head required (NPSHr) of ~2m of water (~20 kPa) needs inlet pressure of at least 23 kPa above vapor pressure to avoid cavitation. At 85 kPa header pressure with pipe losses of ~60-70 kPa to the pump suction, the local pressure at the pump inlet is approximately 15-25 kPa -- dangerously close to vapor pressure. Cavitation onset is expected. Valid.

- **Ammonia leak rate**: A micrometeorite puncture of 0.5-1mm diameter in a pressurized ammonia line (~200 kPa, 35 kg total) would produce a leak rate of approximately 0.5-2 kg/hour depending on puncture geometry and upstream pressure. The stated 0.8 kg/hour is within this range. Valid.

- **EVA preparation time**: Standard EVA prep on ISS takes approximately 4 hours, including pre-breathe protocol (breathing pure O2 to prevent decompression sickness), suit donning, systems checks, and airlock depressurization. This timeline is well-documented. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The leaked ammonia is both the poison and the cure.** Ammonia from the primary loop leak caused the backup radiator ice blockage. But ammonia is also the best available solvent for clearing that ice. Using the spare ammonia to blast the radiator clean is counter-intuitive ("more of the thing that caused the problem?!") but physically correct.

2. **One action solves two problems.** Clearing the backup radiator with ammonia simultaneously: (a) restores cooling capacity (Failure 2 fixed), and (b) stops the ongoing deposition of ammonia ice (because once the backup radiator is functioning, its surface temperature rises above NH3 frost point, preventing future deposition). Two problems resolved with one intervention.

3. **The pressure manifold is the hidden coupling.** The connection between the primary loop pressure drop and the tertiary pump cavitation is not obvious -- they use different fluids (ammonia vs. water). The shared pressure equalization manifold is the coupling mechanism. Isolating the manifold decouples the failures, allowing the tertiary loop to be re-pressurized independently. This is a systems engineering insight: understanding the physical architecture reveals non-obvious interactions.

4. **Triage by timeline, not by severity.** All three failures are serious, but their critical timelines differ: backup radiator (hours), pump cavitation (hours), primary leak (12 hours). Fixing the fastest-to-critical failure first (radiator) and deferring the slowest (leak) is correct triage. A model that starts with the most dramatic failure (the ammonia leak into space) rather than the most urgent (the temperature rise) is misallocating effort.

5. **Sacrifice spare parts for the immediate crisis.** Using the spare ammonia to clear the radiator means it is unavailable for its intended purpose (primary loop top-off). This feels wasteful. But the radiator fix saves the station NOW, while the primary loop has 12 hours of remaining life. The spare ammonia is more valuable as a radiator de-icer than as a loop refill.
