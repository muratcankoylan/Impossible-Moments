# IM-0073: The Broken Compass

**Category**: The Last Ingredient
**Difficulty**: FRACTURE (2.2.2.3.2.2)
**Status**: KS-Multiple (Known Solution, Multiple Valid Paths)
**Correct Outcome**: NAVIGATE

---

## Scenario

You are hiking alone in a large, unfamiliar temperate forest in the northern hemisphere (approximately 45N latitude). You became disoriented two hours ago after leaving the marked trail to investigate a waterfall. Your compass -- the only navigation tool you were carrying -- fell into a rocky stream and the needle is now bent and spins freely, no longer indicating north. Your smartphone died an hour ago (dead battery).

You know from studying the trail map before your phone died that the trailhead parking lot is roughly due north of your current position, approximately 3 km away. If you can determine north reliably, you can navigate back to your car before dark. If you cannot, you risk walking deeper into the wilderness.

| Wind | Light and variable, not useful for navigation |

### Your Position

Standing on a small rise that gives you a partial view of the sky above the tree canopy. A shallow puddle of clear, still water (approximately 20 cm diameter, 2 cm deep) sits in a depression in the rock at your feet. A narrow clearing extends 3 meters to the south, providing a patch of direct sunlight on the ground.

## Why This Looks Impossible

Your compass is broken. Your phone is dead. You are in an unfamiliar forest with no trail markers in sight. The obvious navigation tools are unavailable. Most models will either propose a single method (usually the watch method) without considering its limitations, or suggest "walk toward the sun" without understanding the sun's actual position relative to cardinal directions at this time and latitude.

## Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It's Wrong or Incomplete |
|---|---|
| "Walk toward the sun -- it's in the west, so walk 90 degrees to the right for north" | At 2:30 PM in late September at 45N, the sun is approximately 220 degrees azimuth (south-southwest), NOT due west. Walking 90 degrees right of the sun would send you roughly northwest, not north. The sun's position depends on time of day, date, and latitude. This answer demonstrates imprecise solar navigation. |
| "Use moss on trees -- moss grows on the north side" | This is a persistent myth. Moss grows on the side of trees with the most moisture, which is often the north side (less sun exposure) but can be any side depending on local conditions (water sources, shade from other trees, terrain). Moss-based navigation is unreliable and should never be the primary method. |
| "Wait until night and find Polaris" | Viable but wasteful -- you have 4 hours of daylight. Using daytime methods first maximizes your travel time. Waiting until dark to navigate means walking in the dark (dangerous in forest terrain) or camping overnight (unnecessary if you can determine north now). |
| "Use the watch method" (incorrectly applied) | Many people misremember or misapply the watch method. Common errors: pointing the minute hand at the sun (should be the hour hand), bisecting the wrong angle, not accounting for daylight saving time, or applying the southern hemisphere version in the northern hemisphere. A partially correct method applied incorrectly can send you in exactly the wrong direction. |

## Verified Solutions

This is a **KS-Multiple** scenario. Three independent methods are available, and a strong answer identifies at least two (for cross-validation).

### Method 1: Shadow Stick Method (Most Reliable)

**Principle**: The sun moves from east to west. A shadow cast by a vertical stick moves from west to east. By marking the shadow tip at two different times, the line between the marks runs approximately east-west.

| Step | Action | Time | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Plant the straight stick vertically in a patch of sunlit ground (the clearing to the south). Use the flat rock surface or soft earth. Ensure the stick casts a clear shadow. | 1 min | The stick acts as a gnomon. A 60 cm stick at 2:30 PM, late September, at 45N, casts a shadow approximately 50-80 cm long (sun altitude ~35-40 degrees). |
| 2 | Mark the tip of the shadow with the pencil (or a small stone). Note the time: 2:30 PM. | 30 sec | This is Shadow Mark A. |
| 3 | Wait 15-30 minutes. | 15-30 min | The shadow tip moves as the sun moves. 15 minutes gives ~3.75 degrees of solar movement, translating to ~5-10 cm of shadow tip displacement. |
| 4 | Mark the new shadow tip position. This is Shadow Mark B. | 30 sec | Mark B is east of Mark A (shadow moves westward to eastward as sun moves westward). |
| 5 | Draw a line from Mark A to Mark B. This line runs approximately **west (A) to east (B)**. | 30 sec | The line A-to-B points east. The perpendicular to this line, toward the sun, points approximately south. Away from the sun points approximately north. |
| 6 | Stand with Mark A to your left and Mark B to your right. You are now facing approximately **north**. | Immediate | In the northern hemisphere, the sun is in the southern sky. The perpendicular to the east-west line, directed away from the sun, is north. |

**Accuracy**: +/- 10-15 degrees with a 15-minute interval. Accuracy improves with longer waiting periods. At 30 minutes, accuracy is +/- 5-10 degrees. Sufficient for forest navigation toward a 3 km target.

**Limitations**: Requires direct sunlight and a waiting period. Does not work on overcast days.

### Method 2: Improvised Compass (Magnetized Needle on Water)

**Principle**: Rubbing a steel needle against silk (or any smooth cloth) induces a weak magnetic charge through triboelectric and ferromagnetic alignment effects. A magnetized needle, floated on a still water surface using a leaf, rotates to align with Earth's magnetic field, pointing approximately north-south.

| Step | Action | Time | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Magnetize the needle.** Hold the sewing needle by one end. Stroke it repeatedly (50-100 times) in ONE direction along the silk cloth, always lifting the needle at the end of each stroke and returning to the start before stroking again. Do not stroke back and forth -- this randomizes the magnetic domains. Stroke from eye-end to point-end consistently. | 3-5 min | Stroking in one direction aligns the ferromagnetic domains in the steel, creating a weak but detectable magnetic dipole. The pointing end (last to be stroked) will become the north-seeking pole in most cases. |
| 2 | **Prepare the float.** Place the broad maple leaf flat on the surface of the puddle. The leaf should float freely with no edge touching the sides of the depression. | 10 sec | The leaf provides a low-friction platform for the needle. Surface tension keeps the leaf afloat. |
| 3 | **Place the needle on the leaf.** Gently lay the magnetized needle on the center of the floating leaf. Do not drop it -- place it delicately. | 10 sec | The needle must be centered on the leaf so it does not tip or slide off. |
| 4 | **Wait for the leaf to rotate.** The magnetized needle, floating on the leaf in the still water, will slowly rotate until the needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field (north-south). This takes 30-120 seconds. | 1-2 min | The rotation is slow because the magnetic force is weak and the leaf has rotational inertia. Still water is essential -- any current or wind will overpower the magnetic force. |
| 5 | **Determine which end points north.** The needle points along the north-south axis, but you do not know which end is north. Use the sun to disambiguate: at 2:30 PM at 45N, the sun is roughly south-southwest. The needle end pointing AWAY from the sun's general direction is north. | 30 sec | Alternatively, if you consistently stroked from eye to point, the point-end is typically the north-seeking pole. But cross-referencing with the sun is more reliable. |
| 6 | **Account for magnetic declination.** The local declination is ~10W, meaning magnetic north is 10 degrees west of true north. Adjust your heading 10 degrees east of the needle's indicated north. | Immediate | At 3 km distance, a 10-degree error translates to ~500 m lateral offset. Correcting for declination improves accuracy. The declination value was known from the trail map studied earlier. |

**Accuracy**: +/- 15-20 degrees for a field-improvised compass, improving to +/- 10-15 degrees with declination correction.

**Limitations**: Requires still water, a ferromagnetic needle, and patience. The magnetic force is weak -- wind or water disturbance can overpower it.

### Method 3: Watch Method (Analog Watch as Compass)

**Principle**: In the northern hemisphere, if you point the hour hand at the sun, the bisector of the angle between the hour hand and the 12 o'clock position points approximately south. North is in the opposite direction.

| Step | Action | Time | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Check for daylight saving time.** If DST is active (it is: late September, DST ends in early November in North America), adjust by setting the watch back one hour mentally. Your watch reads 2:30 PM; solar time is approximately 1:30 PM. Use the 1:30 position for the calculation. | Immediate | DST introduces a 15-degree error (~1 hour = 15 degrees of solar movement) if not corrected. |
| 2 | **Hold the watch flat and point the adjusted hour hand (1:30 position) at the sun.** Align the hour hand so it points directly at the sun. | 10 sec | The hour hand acts as a solar pointer. |
| 3 | **Bisect the angle between the hour hand and the 12.** The hour hand points at the sun (1:30 position). The 12 is at the top of the watch. The angle between 1:30 and 12 is approximately 45 degrees. The bisector of this angle (at approximately the 12:45 position on the watch face) points approximately **south**. | 10 sec | The bisection works because the sun moves 360 degrees in 24 hours (15 degrees/hour), while the hour hand moves 360 degrees in 12 hours (30 degrees/hour). Bisecting the angle compensates for this 2:1 rate difference. |
| 4 | **North is opposite south.** The direction directly opposite the 12:45 bisector on your watch face (i.e., the 6:45 position) points approximately **north**. | Immediate | Turn 180 degrees from the south-pointing bisector. |

**Accuracy**: +/- 15-25 degrees. Accuracy degrades at latitudes far from 45 degrees, and the DST and equation-of-time corrections introduce additional error if not applied. At 45N in late September, accuracy is reasonable.

**Limitations**: Requires the sun to be visible and an analog watch (or ability to visualize one). DST and equation-of-time corrections are often forgotten, introducing systematic error.

### Recommended Approach: Cross-Validation

A strong answer uses **at least two methods** and cross-validates:

1. **Start the shadow stick immediately** (takes 15-30 minutes of waiting, so start first).
2. **While waiting for the shadow to move, build the improvised compass** (magnetize needle, float on leaf).
3. **Apply the watch method** for a quick initial estimate.
4. **Compare all three results.** If they agree within ~20 degrees, you have a reliable north. If they disagree significantly, investigate the source of error (DST not corrected? Needle not magnetized? Shadow too short?).
5. **Navigate north toward the trailhead** using the consensus direction. Re-check every 20-30 minutes using the shadow stick or compass (as sun position changes).

**Total preparation time: ~30 minutes (dominated by shadow stick waiting time). Travel time: 3 km at ~3 km/h (forest hiking speed) = ~1 hour. Total time: ~1.5 hours. Well within the 4-hour daylight window.**

### Physics Validation

- **Shadow stick method**: The sun's azimuth at 2:30 PM local time, late September, at 45N is approximately 220-230 degrees (south-southwest). Shadow points approximately 40-50 degrees (north-northeast). Over 15 minutes, the shadow tip moves ~5-10 cm, providing a measurable east-west baseline. This method is documented in military and survival manuals (FM 21-76, SAS Survival Handbook). **Valid.**
- **Needle magnetization**: Stroking a steel needle along silk in one direction partially aligns the ferromagnetic domains. The resulting magnetic moment is weak (~10^-5 Am^2) but sufficient to rotate a leaf on still water against the ~10^-5 N restoring force from Earth's field (B ~ 50 microT). Published experiments confirm that rubbed needles align with Earth's field on water. **Valid.**
- **Leaf flotation**: A fresh maple leaf (density ~0.7-0.9 g/cm^3) floats on water. A 4 cm steel needle (mass ~0.5 g) can be supported by a large leaf without submerging it. **Valid.**
- **Watch method**: The bisection method is geometrically derived from the 2:1 ratio of hour-hand-to-sun angular velocities. At 45N in late September, the equation-of-time correction is approximately +7 minutes (sun is ~7 minutes ahead of mean solar time). Combined with DST, the total correction is ~1 hour 7 minutes. Without the equation-of-time correction, the error is ~2 degrees. With DST correction alone, accuracy is ~+/- 15 degrees. **Valid with noted limitations.**
- **Magnetic declination**: At 45N in northeastern North America (typical hiking region), declination ranges from 5W to 15W depending on longitude. The stated 10W is representative. Over 3 km, a 10-degree uncorrected error produces a lateral offset of ~520 m. With declination correction applied, the offset reduces to within forest-navigation tolerance. **Valid.**

### Key Insights

1. **Multiple independent methods exist.** The solver should not rely on a single method. Cross-validation between shadow stick, improvised compass, and watch method provides redundancy and error detection.
2. **The shadow stick is the most reliable daytime method.** It requires no special knowledge beyond "shadows move as the sun moves" and produces an east-west line with no ambiguity (the newer shadow mark is always further east).
3. **Magnetization by rubbing is real but requires proper technique.** Stroking must be in one direction consistently, not back and forth. This is a common error that prevents magnetization.
4. **The watch method requires DST correction.** Failure to adjust for daylight saving time introduces a 15-degree systematic error -- enough to send you northwest instead of north. This is the most common mistake with this method.
5. **The broken compass is not useless.** The compass housing still has accurate cardinal direction markings. If you build an improvised compass (magnetized needle), you can use the broken compass's housing as a reference dial, improving your ability to read directions from the needle.

## Design Notes

This scenario is classified as **FRACTURE** at the lower end because it requires two core insights (I=2: multiple methods exist, everyday objects contain navigation capability), features mild distractors (D=2: broken compass dismissal, moss myth), has moderate counter-intuition in that a sewing needle + silk + leaf becomes a compass (C=2), bridges terrestrial navigation, magnetism, and solar geometry (B=3), has a moderate time constraint from daylight hours (T=2), and has a moderate trap in applying the watch method incorrectly (X=2).

This is a **KS-Multiple** scenario, meaning multiple valid solution paths exist and a strong answer identifies more than one. The scenario tests the Last Ingredient skill in its purest form: the "missing ingredient" is a working compass, and the solver must synthesize its function from available materials (needle + silk + leaf + water = compass) or substitute an entirely different approach (shadow stick, watch method). The scenario rewards breadth of knowledge and the habit of cross-validation.

## Counterfactual Variants

1. **If the sky were overcast (no sun visible)**: The shadow stick and watch methods fail. Only the improvised compass works. The solver must rely on a single method without cross-validation. Difficulty increases (I increases to 3 because the single-method reliance requires higher confidence in magnetization technique).
2. **If no steel needle were available**: The improvised compass method fails. Shadow stick and watch method remain. The solver loses one method of cross-validation. Difficulty decreases slightly (fewer options to evaluate).
3. **If it were nighttime**: Shadow stick and watch method fail. Polaris becomes the primary method. The solver must identify Polaris using the Big Dipper pointer stars. The improvised compass also works at night. Different skill set entirely.
4. **If the latitude were 5N (near the equator)**: The watch method becomes unreliable (the sun passes nearly overhead, making hour-hand pointing imprecise). The shadow stick method still works but produces shorter shadows. The improvised compass is unaffected. Difficulty increases for methods 1 and 3.
5. **If the needle were aluminum instead of steel**: Aluminum is non-ferromagnetic and cannot be magnetized by rubbing. The improvised compass method fails entirely. The solver must recognize this material limitation. Only shadow stick and watch methods remain.
