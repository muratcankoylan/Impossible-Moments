## Scenario

You are visiting a remote off-grid cabin in the mountains of Norway. The cabin has no electricity, no mechanical pumps, and no pressurized water supply. It is heated by a wood-burning stove in the main room. The cabin has been in the family for three generations.

You notice something that appears impossible: water is circulating continuously through a closed loop of copper pipes. The loop runs from a coil of pipe wrapped around the inside of the stove's firebox, up through the ceiling, across the attic, through a large copper tank (approximately 200 liters), and back down through the floor to the stove. The water is warm throughout the loop and hot at the stove end.

There is no pump. You have checked thoroughly -- there is no electric pump, no hand pump, no mechanical pump of any kind anywhere in the loop. There are no moving parts visible anywhere in the system. The pipes are standard copper tubing, soldered at the joints. The tank in the attic is a sealed copper vessel with no internal mechanisms. Yet the water is clearly circulating: you can feel the warm pipe from the stove vibrating very slightly with flow, and when you open a test valve at the bottom of the loop, warm water flows out steadily at approximately 2-3 liters per minute.

The cabin owner (an elderly neighbor, not the designer) tells you: "My grandfather built it in 1952. It has always worked. When the stove is lit, the water moves. I do not know how." She has never needed to know how -- it simply works.

### Environment

- **Location**: Mountain cabin, 850 m elevation, 67 degrees N latitude (northern Norway). Remote, no road access in winter (ski access only).
- **Season**: Late autumn. Outside temperature: -5 degrees C. The cabin is heated to approximately 20 degrees C by the wood stove.
- **Stove**: Cast-iron wood-burning stove, firebox temperature approximately 350-500 degrees C during active burning. The copper coil inside the firebox heats the water to approximately 85-90 degrees C at the coil outlet.
- **Copper pipe loop**: Total length approximately 15 m. Internal diameter: 22 mm. The loop is entirely closed (no air vents, no open surfaces). It contains approximately 5.7 liters of water plus the 200 L tank.
- **Height differential**: The stove coil is at ground floor level (0 m reference). The attic tank is at approximately 4.5 m above the stove. The pipe runs vertically from the stove up to the attic (4.5 m rise), horizontally across the attic (3 m), through the tank, and back down (4.5 m descent) to the stove.
- **Tank**: 200 L copper tank in the attic, insulated with sheep's wool wrapping. Water temperature in the tank: approximately 55-60 degrees C (it stores heat for nighttime use when the stove is banked).
- **No electricity**: The cabin has oil lamps and candles. No generator, no solar panels, no battery system.
- **No moving parts**: You have inspected the entire system. There are no valves that cycle, no check valves, no impellers, no pistons, no bellows, no siphon breaks. It is a completely passive system.

### The Apparent Impossibility

Water is flowing through pipes with no pump. In everyday experience, water in a closed pipe system is static unless a pump provides the driving force. Gravity alone cannot circulate water in a closed loop -- if water falls on one side, it must rise on the other, and gravity acts equally on both sides, producing no net flow.

Or does it?

## Why This Looks Impossible

Water circulates through a closed pipe loop with no pump and no moving parts. The system appears to violate basic fluid mechanics: in a closed loop where both the rising and falling columns are the same height, gravitational potential cancels out. There should be no net driving force.

The confusion arises from an implicit assumption: that the water in both columns has the same density. If both columns have the same density, then gravitational forces on them are equal and opposite, and no circulation occurs. This is correct for a uniform-temperature system.

But the system is NOT at uniform temperature.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Thermosiphon -- Natural Circulation Driven by Temperature-Dependent Density Differences

The system is a **thermosiphon** (also spelled thermosyphon), a device that circulates fluid using only gravity and the density difference between hot and cold fluid. No pump is needed.

The physics:

1. **Hot water is less dense than cold water.** At 85 degrees C, water has a density of approximately 968.6 kg/m-cubed. At 55 degrees C (the tank temperature, which is also the approximate temperature of the descending cold leg), water has a density of approximately 985.7 kg/m-cubed. The density difference is approximately 17.1 kg/m-cubed.

2. **In the closed loop, the hot side and cold side are at different densities.** The rising column (from stove to attic tank) contains hot water (968.6 kg/m-cubed). The descending column (from attic tank back to stove) contains cooler water (985.7 kg/m-cubed). Both columns are 4.5 m tall.

3. **The heavier cold column pushes down with more force than the lighter hot column.** The pressure at the bottom of the cold column is P_cold = rho_cold x g x h = 985.7 x 9.81 x 4.5 = 43,528 Pa. The pressure at the bottom of the hot column is P_hot = rho_hot x g x h = 968.6 x 9.81 x 4.5 = 42,773 Pa. The pressure difference: Delta_P = 43,528 - 42,773 = 755 Pa.

4. **This pressure difference drives circulation.** 755 Pa is not a lot (about 0.75% of atmospheric pressure), but it is enough to overcome the friction losses in 15 m of 22 mm copper pipe at low flow velocities. The water circulates: hot water rises (because the heavier cold water pushes it up from below), flows through the tank (where it cools by transferring heat to the stored water and through insulation losses), and descends as cooler, denser water back to the stove coil.

This is NOT "hot water rises because it is hot." That is convection in an open system. In a closed loop, the mechanism is a pressure differential caused by the density imbalance between two columns of fluid at different temperatures. The gravity on the denser cold column exceeds the gravity on the lighter hot column, creating a net driving pressure.

### Quantitative Validation

**Driving pressure:**

Delta_P = (rho_cold - rho_hot) x g x h = (985.7 - 968.6) x 9.81 x 4.5 = 17.1 x 9.81 x 4.5 = 754.8 Pa

**Friction losses in the pipe loop:**

Using the Darcy-Weisbach equation for laminar or turbulent flow in a pipe:

Delta_P_friction = f x (L/D) x (rho x v^2 / 2)

First, estimate the flow velocity from the observed 2-3 L/min:
- Flow rate Q = 2.5 L/min = 4.17 x 10^-5 m-cubed/s
- Pipe cross-section A = pi x (0.011)^2 = 3.80 x 10^-4 m-squared
- Velocity v = Q/A = 4.17e-5 / 3.80e-4 = 0.110 m/s

Reynolds number: Re = (rho x v x D) / mu = (977 x 0.110 x 0.022) / (4.0 x 10^-4) = 5,917

This is in the transitional regime (2,300 < Re < 10,000). Using the Moody chart for smooth copper pipe at Re = 5,917: friction factor f approximately 0.035.

Delta_P_friction = 0.035 x (15 / 0.022) x (977 x 0.110^2 / 2) = 0.035 x 682 x 5.91 = 141 Pa

Adding minor losses (bends, tank entry/exit): approximately 100 Pa (estimate).

Total friction loss: approximately 241 Pa.

The driving pressure (755 Pa) comfortably exceeds the total friction losses (241 Pa). The system operates with a significant margin. In practice, the flow rate adjusts to the point where friction equals the driving pressure. The observed 2-3 L/min is consistent with an equilibrium flow rate at approximately 755 Pa driving pressure. **Valid.**

**Heat transfer rate:**

Q_heat = m_dot x c_p x Delta_T = (rho x Q) x c_p x (T_hot - T_cold)
= (977 x 4.17e-5) x 4,186 x (85 - 55)
= 0.0407 x 4,186 x 30
= 5,113 W approximately 5.1 kW

A wood stove producing 5-10 kW of heat output, with approximately 50% directed to the water coil, would deliver approximately 2.5-5 kW to the water loop. The calculated 5.1 kW is at the upper end but feasible for a well-designed firebox coil. **Valid.**

### Historical and Technical Context

Thermosiphon systems are not exotic or theoretical. They are widely used:

- **Solar water heaters**: Many solar thermal systems (especially in Australia, Israel, Greece, and Turkey) use thermosiphon circulation to move water between rooftop solar collectors and storage tanks. No pump is needed. The tank is positioned above the collector, and the density difference between hot (collector outlet) and cool (tank bottom) water drives circulation.

- **Gravity-fed central heating**: Before electric circulation pumps became standard in the 1950s-1960s, most hydronic (hot water) heating systems in homes used thermosiphon circulation. The boiler was placed in the basement, the radiators and expansion tank were upstairs, and the water circulated by gravity. These systems required larger-diameter pipes (to reduce friction) and careful vertical arrangement (to maximize the driving pressure head).

- **Nuclear reactor emergency cooling**: Some nuclear reactor designs use passive thermosiphon circulation as an emergency core cooling mechanism. If all pumps fail, the residual heat in the reactor core drives natural circulation through the cooling loop. This is a critical safety feature in several Generation III+ reactor designs.

- **The cabin system (1952)**: A Norwegian farmer-craftsman building this system in 1952 would have been working from a tradition of gravity-fed heating systems common in Scandinavia before rural electrification. The design requires no engineering degree -- it requires understanding that "hot water rises" and laying pipes accordingly.

## Key Insights

1. **Hot water is less dense than cold water, and in a closed vertical loop, this density difference creates a driving pressure.** This is the fundamental physics. The "impossibility" dissolves when you recognize that gravity does NOT act equally on both columns because they have different densities.

2. **The thermosiphon is not convection -- it is a pressure-driven flow.** Convection is the movement of fluid within a body of fluid due to density differences. A thermosiphon is the circulation of fluid through a closed pipe loop driven by the pressure differential between two columns of different density. The mechanism is similar but the geometry (closed pipe vs. open fluid body) makes the thermosiphon more directed and efficient.

3. **The system is self-regulating.** If the stove burns hotter, the temperature difference increases, increasing the density difference, increasing the driving pressure, increasing the flow rate. The increased flow rate carries more heat to the tank, warming it, which reduces the temperature difference, which reduces the flow. The system finds a natural equilibrium. If the stove goes out, the temperature difference decreases to zero, the flow stops, and the stored heat in the tank gradually dissipates. No controls are needed.

4. **Height differential is critical.** The driving pressure is proportional to the height of the columns (Delta_P = Delta_rho x g x h). The 4.5 m height between the stove and the attic tank is what makes this system work. If the tank were at the same level as the stove, there would be no driving pressure and no circulation. This is why thermosiphon systems always have the heat source at the bottom and the heat sink (tank) at the top.

5. **Large-diameter pipes and minimal bends reduce friction.** The 22 mm copper pipe is larger than a modern pumped system would use (typically 15 mm). The larger diameter reduces friction losses, which is critical when the driving pressure is only 755 Pa. The original builder used larger pipes because he understood (from tradition or experience) that the gravity flow needed low resistance.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the solver's understanding of fluid mechanics fundamentals -- specifically, whether they recognize that the gravitational body force on a fluid column depends on the fluid's density, and that temperature-dependent density differences in a closed loop create a net driving pressure. The "Ghost Machine" category asks: what is the invisible mechanism that makes this apparently impossible system work?

The primary cognitive trap is the assumption that "gravity cancels in a closed loop." This is true for uniform-density fluid but false when the two legs of the loop are at different temperatures (and therefore different densities). The solver must identify the broken assumption and replace it with the correct physics.
