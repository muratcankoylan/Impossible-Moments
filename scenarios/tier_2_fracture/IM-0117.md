# IM-0117: The Chimney Problem

**Category**: Locked Room
**Tier**: FRACTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: BUILD A SMOKY FIRE IN THE FIREPLACE TO SIGNAL THROUGH THE CHIMNEY

---

## Scenario

You are trapped in the ground-floor study of an old stone country house. The door behind you has just slammed shut and the deadbolt engaged automatically -- a spring-loaded mortise lock that requires a key from either side. You do not have the key. You heard it fall on the stone floor on the other side of the door when the wind blew the door shut.

The room is approximately 4m x 5m with a ceiling height of 2.8m. The walls are solid limestone, approximately 45cm thick. There is one window on the west wall, but it is fitted with wrought iron bars -- decorative but structural, 12mm diameter mild steel rods set into the stone at 10cm spacing. You cannot fit between them, and you cannot bend 12mm steel bars by hand.

On the north wall is a traditional stone fireplace. The firebox opening is 70cm wide x 60cm tall. You kneel down, look up the chimney, and see a narrow shaft of daylight far above. The chimney flue is approximately 25cm in internal diameter -- a standard Victorian-era clay-lined flue. Your shoulders are 45cm wide. Your head is 18cm wide. You cannot physically fit inside the chimney flue, let alone climb it. The chimney rises approximately 6m to the chimney pot on the roof.

It is 3:00 PM on a Saturday afternoon. The house is in a rural hamlet -- neighbors exist within 200m, and occasional foot traffic passes on the lane outside. You can hear birdsong and distant sounds of habitation through the barred window. However, the window faces the back garden, not the lane. Shouting through the barred window produces sound that dissipates across an empty garden before reaching any occupied area. You have been shouting for 20 minutes with no response.

Your phone battery died an hour ago. There is no landline in the room.

### Environment

- **Room**: 4m x 5m stone study. Limestone walls 45cm thick. Flagstone floor. Timber ceiling with plaster overlay.
- **Door**: Solid oak, 5cm thick, mounted in stone frame. Spring-loaded mortise deadbolt, key required from both sides. Key is on the opposite side of the door on the floor, unreachable.
- **Window**: Single sash window, 80cm x 120cm. Glass intact. Wrought iron bars: 12mm diameter mild steel, set 10cm apart, embedded 5cm into stone on each side. Seven bars total.
- **Fireplace**: Stone surround, cast iron firebox insert. Firebox opening 70cm wide x 60cm tall. Flue internal diameter: approximately 25cm (standard 9-inch Victorian clay liner). Chimney height: approximately 6m.
- **Weather**: Overcast, calm. Approximately 14 degrees C. No wind. Slight drizzle outside.
- **Ambient sounds**: Birdsong, occasional distant voices, a dog barking intermittently approximately 150m away.

### Threat

- **Immediate danger**: None. The room is dry, safe, and at a comfortable temperature. This is not a life-threatening scenario.
- **Duration**: If not rescued, you will be trapped until someone notices your absence. You live alone. No one is expecting you until Monday morning (work). That is approximately 42 hours away.
- **Dehydration**: No water in the room. Dehydration will become uncomfortable within 12-18 hours and concerning within 24-36 hours.
- **Hypothermia risk**: Minimal. The stone room retains warmth and the fireplace provides an option for heat if needed.

### Your Position

- Standing in the center of the room. Wearing casual clothing: jeans, a cotton shirt, leather shoes. No tools on your person. Phone is dead.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are locked in a stone room. The door requires a key you do not have. The window has steel bars you cannot bend. The chimney is the only opening to the outside -- but at 25cm diameter, it is physically impossible for a human to enter or climb. You are sealed inside.

The scenario presents three apparent action paths, all of which seem futile:

1. **Force the door**: Solid oak, 5cm thick, in a stone frame. The mortise lock is embedded in the door. You cannot kick through a 5cm oak door in a stone frame. The poker and candlestick provide no meaningful leverage point.

2. **Remove the window bars**: 12mm steel bars embedded 5cm into limestone on each side. You cannot bend them by hand (yield strength of mild steel requires ~2,400 N for a 12mm rod -- far beyond hand grip force of ~400 N). The poker is not long enough to provide sufficient leverage between bars.

3. **Climb the chimney**: 25cm diameter. Your shoulders are 45cm. Physically impossible. Even a child could not climb a 25cm flue. This is a non-starter.

The fixation is on GETTING OUT of the room. Every escape path is blocked. The room feels like a sealed box.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Climb the chimney" | 25cm flue diameter vs. 45cm shoulder width. Physically impossible. Even dislocating shoulders would not reduce skeletal width below 30cm. |
| "Break down the oak door" | 5cm solid oak in a stone frame. The poker and candlestick lack the mass and leverage to breach it. Would take hours of sustained effort with proper tools. |
| "Bend the window bars" | 12mm mild steel requires ~2,400 N bending force at the midpoint. Maximum hand-applied force with the poker as lever: ~600-800 N. Insufficient by a factor of 3-4x. |

| "Burn down the door" | Could theoretically char the oak, but fire against a vertical surface in a stone frame is extremely inefficient. The door is 5cm thick oak -- it would smolder, not burn through. The room would fill with dangerous smoke before the door weakened meaningfully, creating an asphyxiation hazard. |
| "Send a message under the door" | The gap under a mortise-locked door in a stone frame is typically 3-5mm -- insufficient for a folded note. Even if a note could be slid through, there is no one on the other side to read it. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: You Do Not Need to Go Up the Chimney. The Chimney Will Carry Your Signal for You.

The chimney is not an escape route -- it is a communication channel. The flue is 25cm across, but smoke has no shoulders. A column of smoke rising from a chimney in a hamlet on a drizzly Saturday afternoon will be noticed. Smoke from a chimney is normal; thick, dark, billowing smoke is not. And if you control what you burn, you can produce a smoke signal visible from hundreds of meters.

The key reframing: stop trying to move your BODY out of the room. Instead, move INFORMATION out of the room. Smoke is visible, persistent, and anomalous enough (in the wrong season, or with the wrong character) to attract attention.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Build a base fire in the fireplace.** Crumple 5-6 sheets of newspaper into loose balls. Arrange 2-3 smaller logs (8cm diameter) in a tepee formation over the newspaper. Confirm the damper is open (it is -- you can feel the draft). Light the newspaper with matches. Allow the fire to catch and establish a strong burn. | 5 min | 5 min |
| 2 | **Wait for the fire to establish.** A strong base of hot coals is needed to sustain combustion of damp material. Let the initial logs burn down to a bed of glowing coals with active flames. Add 1-2 more logs to build heat. | 15 min | 20 min |
| 3 | **Add green vegetation to produce thick smoke.** Place the damp ivy clippings, wet grass, and green twigs from the basket onto the hot fire. Green, damp vegetation does not burn cleanly -- it smolders and produces dense white-gray smoke. The volume of smoke will be far greater than what a normal fireplace fire produces. | 2 min | 22 min |
| 4 | **Maintain the smoky fire.** Continue adding green vegetation in batches. The hot coal base provides enough heat to smolder the damp material continuously. The chimney's natural draft (warm air rises through the 6m flue) will draw the smoke upward and out the chimney pot. | Ongoing | -- |
| 5 | **Optional: create intermittent smoke pulses.** Use the wool blanket to briefly cover and uncover the firebox opening, pulsing the smoke output. This creates an irregular pattern of smoke that is visually distinctive and more likely to attract attention than a steady column. Be careful not to hold the blanket over the fire too long (wool is fire-resistant but not fireproof). | Ongoing | -- |
| 6 | **Simultaneously shout at intervals.** While the smoke signal operates, continue shouting "HELP" or "FIRE" through the barred window every 2-3 minutes. "FIRE" is more likely to prompt investigation than "HELP." The smoke provides the visual signal; shouting provides the auditory backup if anyone approaches to investigate the smoke. | Ongoing | -- |
| 7 | **Wait for investigation.** An unusual volume of thick smoke from a chimney in a hamlet will attract attention within minutes to hours. Neighbors, passersby, or postal workers will investigate or call the fire service. When someone arrives, direct them to the front door to retrieve the key. | Variable | -- |

**Total active setup time: ~22 minutes. Remaining time is passive maintenance and waiting.**

### Physics Validation

- **Smoke production from green vegetation**: Fresh, damp vegetation (moisture content 40-60%) placed on hot coals (600-800 degrees C) undergoes incomplete combustion and pyrolysis, producing dense white-gray smoke consisting of water vapor, particulates, and volatile organic compounds. Smoke volume from 3 kg of green vegetation: substantial and sustained over 20-40 minutes of smoldering. Valid.
- **Chimney draft**: A 6m chimney with a fire burning in the firebox creates a natural draft due to the stack effect. The temperature differential between the hot flue gases (~150-300 degrees C) and the ambient air (14 degrees C) drives upward airflow. Draft velocity in a 25cm flue with this temperature differential: approximately 1-3 m/s. This is sufficient to carry all smoke upward and out the chimney pot. Valid.
- **Smoke visibility**: A dense column of white-gray smoke against an overcast sky is visible at 200-500m in calm conditions. The smoke will rise vertically in the calm air (no wind reported), creating a persistent column. In a rural hamlet, an unusually dense smoke column from a chimney will draw attention -- it suggests a chimney fire or house fire, both of which provoke investigation. Valid.
- **Wool blanket fire resistance**: Wool has a high ignition temperature (~570-600 degrees C) and is naturally flame-resistant. It chars rather than melts. Brief (2-3 second) contact with fireplace opening to pulse smoke is safe. Prolonged contact (>10 seconds) will cause charring and eventual ignition. Valid.
- **Window bar strength**: Mild steel (yield strength ~250 MPa) rod of 12mm diameter has a moment of inertia of approximately 1018 mm^4. At a span of ~80cm (window width) with a concentrated load at midpoint, the force required for plastic deformation exceeds 2,000 N. Maximum human grip-and-pull force on a smooth bar: ~400-500 N. Even using the 70cm poker as a lever between bars: mechanical advantage of ~2x, giving ~800-1,000 N. Insufficient. Valid that bars cannot be removed. Valid.
- **Oak door strength**: A 5cm (2-inch) solid oak door has a breaking strength far exceeding what can be applied by a human with a 1.5 kg poker. Oak has a modulus of rupture of ~100 MPa. The door panel would require sustained, focused impacts of several thousand Newtons to breach. Not feasible with available tools. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The chimney is a communication channel, not an escape route.** The fixation on using the chimney as a physical exit blinds you to its real utility: it is a pipe that carries material from inside the room to the outside world. You cannot send your body up that pipe, but you can send smoke.
2. **Green vegetation is not failed firewood -- it is signal-smoke fuel.** The basket of damp garden cuttings looks like useless kindling. In fact, it is the most important object in the room. Dry wood produces heat and flame; wet vegetation produces smoke. The scenario requires smoke, not heat.
3. **The threat model is discomfort, not death.** This reframes the urgency. You are not dying. You are inconvenienced. This means you have time for a signaling strategy that might take hours to work. You do not need an instant escape.
4. **"FIRE" is a better word than "HELP."** Human psychology: people investigate potential fires (property threat) more urgently than they investigate calls for help (personal involvement, bystander effect). Smoke from a chimney combined with shouts of "FIRE" creates a coherent, alarming signal.
5. **Passivity vs. activity reframing**: The scenario rewards creative use of existing infrastructure (the chimney draft) rather than brute-force attempts to breach barriers (door, bars).

---

## Distractor Analysis

- **The fireplace poker** draws attention to prying or forcing -- using it on the door hinges, the window bars, or the lock. It is too short and light to provide meaningful leverage against any of these structural elements. Its actual best use is rearranging logs in the fire.
- **The brass letter opener** suggests picking the lock. A mortise deadbolt cannot be picked with a dull brass blade. This is Hollywood thinking.
- **The books** suggest burning for heat or smoke, but leather-bound books produce acrid, toxic smoke (leather releases hydrogen cyanide when burned). Green vegetation is far superior as smoke fuel and far safer.
- **The window** draws focus as the "almost-escape" -- you can see outside, hear sounds, feel the air. The bars make it a visual taunt. But the chimney, not the window, is the solution.
- **The brass candlestick** suggests bashing the lock or the bars. At 0.8 kg, it has insufficient mass for either purpose.
- **The newspaper** is essential as fire starter but might be mistakenly seen as the primary smoke source. Newspaper burns too fast and too cleanly to produce sustained signaling smoke.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to reframe a structural element from its primary function (chimney = pathway for humans/Santa Claus) to a secondary function (chimney = communication channel for smoke). The scenario deliberately emphasizes the chimney's narrowness to close off the "climb out" path, but this emphasis also draws cognitive fixation TO the chimney, potentially making the reframing easier for some models.

The basket of green garden cuttings is the critical resource. A model that catalogs available objects carefully will note the distinction between dry firewood (produces heat, clean flame) and damp green vegetation (produces thick smoke). The insight requires connecting "smoke production" to "signaling" to "rescue."
