# IM-0066: The Paper Bridge

**Category**: The Wrong Toolbox
**Tier**: FRACTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: CROSS

---

## Scenario

You are standing on the edge of a raised concrete platform inside an abandoned warehouse. A second platform of identical height sits 2 meters away across a gap. The floor below is 3 meters down -- bare concrete, no cushioning. You must reach the far platform. The only way out of the warehouse is a door on the far platform. The platform you are standing on has no exit. There is no way to climb down safely: the platform walls are smooth vertical concrete with no handholds, and a 3m uncontrolled fall onto concrete carries a high probability of serious injury (broken ankles, spine compression, head trauma).

### Environment

- **Platform A (your position)**: 3m x 3m, concrete surface, 3m above warehouse floor
- **Platform B (target)**: 3m x 3m, concrete surface, 3m above warehouse floor, contains exit door
- **Gap**: 2m horizontal, 3m vertical drop to bare concrete floor
- **Warehouse**: Unheated, ambient temperature 18 degrees C, dry conditions, no wind
- **Lighting**: Overhead fluorescent, adequate visibility
- **Time pressure**: None explicit, but structural fatigue increases with time under load

### Threat

- **Fall**: 3m drop onto concrete. Expected injuries at this height without controlled landing: ankle fractures (60-80% probability), potential spinal compression, head injury risk if landing is uncontrolled. Not reliably survivable without injury.
- **No rescue**: You are alone. No phone signal. The warehouse is remote.

### Your Position

- Standing on Platform A, at the edge nearest the gap. Facing Platform B.

### Available Objects

| Object | Mass | Dimensions | Material | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Newspaper sheets | 0.06 kg each (x20 = 1.2 kg total) | 60cm x 40cm each (standard broadsheet, folded) | Newsprint paper, ~50 gsm | Dry, no damage, standard newspaper |
| Packing tape | 0.3 kg per roll (x2 = 0.6 kg) | 48mm wide, 66m per roll | Polypropylene backing, acrylic adhesive | Standard shipping tape, good adhesion |
| Ball of twine | 0.2 kg | ~60m total length | Jute, 3mm diameter | Tensile strength ~30 kg per strand |

### Human Capabilities (assumed)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Body mass | 75 kg |
| Standing long jump (no run-up, from platform edge) | 1.6 m maximum |
| Arm span | 1.8 m |
| Can roll newspaper tightly | Yes |
| Manual dexterity for taping/tying | Normal adult capability |
| Grip strength | 45 kg per hand |
| Time to roll one newspaper tube | ~30 seconds |

## Why This Looks Impossible

The gap is 2 meters. Your standing long jump is 1.6 meters -- 0.4 meters short. There is no run-up space that would meaningfully increase jump distance from a 3m platform (you would overshoot or land on the edge). You cannot jump across.

Your materials are newspaper, tape, and twine. Newspaper is universally perceived as flimsy, disposable, and structurally worthless. The instinctive response is: "Paper cannot support a human." This is the core misconception. Models unfamiliar with structural engineering principles will dismiss the materials entirely and declare the problem unsolvable.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Jump across the gap" | 2m exceeds standing long jump capability (1.6m). Running jump from a 3m platform edge is extremely dangerous -- miscalculation means a 3m fall. |
| "Tie twine between platforms and tightrope walk" | Jute twine at 3mm diameter cannot provide a stable walking surface. Even if anchored, human balance on a 3mm line is not feasible without training. Sag under 75 kg would also drop you into the gap. |
| "Make a rope from newspaper and swing across" | Newspaper rope has very low tensile strength in long spans. A 2m rope of twisted newspaper supporting 75 kg would fail immediately. |
| "Climb down, cross, climb up" | Platform walls are smooth vertical concrete, 3m high. No handholds. Uncontrolled descent causes injury. |
| "DIE / Impossible" | A structural solution exists using rolled newspaper tubes. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Rolled Newspaper Tubes Are Structural Members

A single sheet of newspaper, rolled tightly into a tube (approximately 3cm diameter, 60cm long), has remarkable compressive and bending strength. This is the same principle that makes cardboard tubes, paper towel rolls, and even industrial paper cores strong: a cylinder distributes load across its entire surface area. A tightly rolled newspaper tube can support 5-10 kg in compression before buckling. When multiple tubes are bundled and taped, strength multiplies.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Roll all 20 newspaper sheets into tight tubes (~3cm diameter, 60cm long each). Use packing tape to secure each tube so it does not unroll. | 15 min | 15 min |
| 2 | Bundle tubes into groups of 5, taping them tightly together in parallel to form 4 "beams" (each beam: 5 tubes side by side, ~15cm wide x 3cm tall x 60cm long). | 5 min | 20 min |
| 3 | Lay two beams end-to-end, overlapping by 15cm at the joint. Tape the overlap extensively (wrap tape around the joint at least 10 times). This creates 2 long beams, each ~105cm long. | 3 min | 23 min |
| 4 | Repeat step 3 for the other two beams. You now have 2 long composite beams (~105cm each). | 3 min | 26 min |
| 5 | Place both long beams side by side. Connect them with twine cross-bracing: tie twine diagonally between the beams every 20cm to create a triangulated truss structure. Wrap tape around the entire assembly at 15cm intervals. | 10 min | 36 min |
| 6 | Reinforce the structure further: wrap remaining tape lengthwise along the top and bottom of the truss, creating a tape "skin" that acts as a tension member. | 5 min | 41 min |
| 7 | Test the bridge on the platform by placing it across a short span and loading it with your body weight on the supported center. Verify it holds. | 1 min | 42 min |
| 8 | Slide the bridge across the gap. Each end must rest at least 30cm on each platform (overlap). The bridge span in the air is ~1.4m (2m gap minus 2 x 0.3m overlap). | 1 min | 43 min |
| 9 | Cross on hands and knees, distributing weight across the bridge surface. Total crossing load is distributed over ~0.5m of bridge length (knees + hands), giving a distributed load rather than a point load. | 1 min | 44 min |

**Total time: ~44 minutes. No time constraint violated.**

### Physics Validation

- **Rolled newspaper tube strength**: A tightly rolled broadsheet newspaper tube (3cm diameter, 60cm long) can support approximately 5-10 kg in compression before Euler buckling. This is well-documented in structural engineering demonstrations and physics education contexts. Valid.
- **Bundle strength**: 5 tubes bundled together multiply the cross-sectional area and moment of inertia. A 5-tube beam can support approximately 25-50 kg before failure. Two such beams in a truss configuration with cross-bracing can support 50-100 kg. Valid.
- **Tape contribution**: Polypropylene packing tape has a tensile strength of approximately 30-40 N per cm of width. A 48mm-wide tape wrapped around structural members adds significant tensile reinforcement. The tape "skin" acts as a tension flange in bending, dramatically increasing the beam's resistance to bending failure. Valid.
- **Twine cross-bracing**: Jute twine at 30 kg tensile strength per strand provides diagonal bracing that converts the parallel beams into a truss. Triangulated trusses resist shear and bending far more effectively than parallel beams alone. Valid.
- **Span calculation**: 1.4m effective span (after 0.3m overlap on each side) is well within the capability of this composite structure under distributed loading. Valid.
- **Weight distribution**: Crawling on hands and knees distributes 75 kg across 4 contact points spanning ~0.5m of bridge length. Peak bending moment is significantly lower than a single point load at midspan. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Newspaper is not weak -- flat newspaper is weak.** The transformation from flat sheet to rolled tube changes the geometry from a surface (zero structural depth) to a cylinder (structural depth in all directions). This is the same principle behind corrugated cardboard and structural paper tubes.
2. **Tape is not just adhesive -- it is a structural material.** Polypropylene packing tape has significant tensile strength. When used as a wrapping skin, it acts as a tension member that prevents the paper tubes from separating under load.
3. **Triangulation is the key to structural rigidity.** The twine cross-bracing transforms two flexible beams into a rigid truss. Without triangulation, the beams would slide laterally and the bridge would collapse.
4. **Distributed loading matters.** Crossing on hands and knees (not walking upright) distributes the load and reduces peak bending stress by approximately 50% compared to standing point loads.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The twine** appears to be the "obvious" rope solution. Models may attempt to use it as a tightrope or a swing rope. Both fail: 3mm jute twine is too thin for balance and too short for a meaningful pendulum. Its real role is as structural cross-bracing.
- **The gap width (2m)** is deliberately close to human jumping range to tempt models into proposing a risky jump. The 0.4m shortfall is enough to make it physically unreliable.
- **The 3m drop** is in the "dangerous but not certain death" range, tempting models to propose climbing down. The smooth walls eliminate this option.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests whether the model can overcome material prejudice -- the assumption that "paper is weak." The solution requires understanding that structural strength comes from geometry, not just material properties. A cylinder is strong; a flat sheet is weak. Same material, different geometry, vastly different capability.

The scenario also tests whether the model can synthesize a composite structure from multiple simple materials (paper for compression, tape for tension, twine for shear bracing) -- a fundamental engineering principle.
