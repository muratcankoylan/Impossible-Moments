# IM-0148: The Bridge of Boats

**Category**: The Cascade (multiple interdependent failures requiring sequenced solutions)
**Difficulty**: FRACTURE (Tier 2 -- Medium)
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: SEQUENCE: ANCHOR B FIRST, THEN POSITION A ALONGSIDE, THEN USE C AS A SHUTTLE

---

## Scenario

You need to cross a river. The river is 25 meters wide, 2-3 meters deep at center, with a moderate current (1.5 m/s). The water is cold (8 degrees C) -- swimming is possible for a strong swimmer but hypothermia risk is real within 10-15 minutes. You are not a strong swimmer. The nearest bridge is 18 km upstream. You need to cross today with approximately 200 kg of supplies (tools, food, camping equipment).

On your side of the river, beached on the bank, are three abandoned boats. Each has a unique problem that prevents it from being used alone as a simple ferry. But together, they can form a pontoon bridge or be used in a coordinated system to get you and your supplies across.

### Environment

- **River**: 25m wide bank-to-bank. Depth: 0.5m at edges, 2-3m at center. Current: 1.5 m/s, flowing west to east. Banks are firm clay with gentle slopes (15-degree grade). The downstream area for 200m is clear (no rapids, falls, or obstructions).
- **Your bank**: South side. Firm, level ground extending back 10m from water's edge. Three boats beached here.
- **Far bank**: North side. Similar terrain. A cleared path leads inland -- your destination.
- **Weather**: Overcast, 12 degrees C air temperature, light wind. No rain. 6 hours of daylight remaining.

## Why This Looks Impossible

No single boat can serve as a straightforward ferry:
- **Boat A** leaks. You could paddle it across (5.5m punt with a broken paddle is awkward), but it takes on 20 L/min. A crossing would take 3-5 minutes of hard paddling against current, during which 60-100 liters of water enters the hull. With 280 kg aboard (you + supplies), the additional water weight (60-100 kg) risks swamping before you reach the far bank.
- **Boat B** is watertight and has an anchor, but is only 3.0m long and has one oar. It can ferry small loads (you alone, or you + one bag) across with difficulty (sweep rowing against current), but carrying all 200 kg of supplies would require 3-4 trips. Each trip against 1.5 m/s current with a single oar is exhausting and slow. It works, but barely.
- **Boat C** is light and maneuverable with two paddles, but has no anchor. In 1.5 m/s current, you must paddle continuously to maintain position. It cannot be fixed in place as a bridge section or staging platform.

The three problems (leak, insufficient length, no anchor) seem to compound: each boat's weakness is different, and combining boats in water with current is complex.

Most models will either:
- Try to use Boat A as a leaky ferry (risky -- may swamp)
- Make multiple trips with Boat B (works but inefficient and exhausting -- and may not recognize the elegant combined solution)
- Attempt to lash boats together as a continuous bridge (the boats' combined length is 11.3m, far short of the 25m river width)
- Say IMPOSSIBLE (wrong: a coordinated system works)

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Each Boat Solves Another Boat's Problem.

The boats are not meant to be used independently or as a continuous bridge. They form a **relay system**:

- **Boat B** (watertight + anchor) serves as a **fixed midstream platform**. Anchor it at the halfway point (~12.5m from each bank). The anchor holds it in position against the current. It becomes a stable staging point.
- **Boat A** (long + leaky) is **moored to the near bank** as a **loading platform and short-distance shuttle to Boat B**. Its length (5.5m) means it only needs to span ~7m from the bank to reach Boat B's position (12.5m out minus Boat B's 1.5m reach = ~11m, and Boat A extending 5.5m from bank reaches ~5.5m, leaving a ~6m gap). With the 30m rope, Boat A can be tethered to both the bank and Boat B, creating a guided ferry for the first half. The leak doesn't matter for short trips (6m crossing takes <1 minute; only 20L water enters).
- **Boat C** (maneuverable + no anchor) serves as the **shuttle between Boat B and the far bank**. Paddle the remaining ~12.5m from Boat B's position to the north bank. With two paddles and the shorter distance, this is a manageable crossing even in 1.5 m/s current.

But the most efficient approach is even simpler:

### Refined Solution: Boat B as Fixed Anchor, Boat C as Active Ferry

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Rig a rope ferry line.** Tie one end of the 30m rope to a tree or stake on the south bank. Load the rope coil into Boat C. Paddle Boat C across the river (25m crossing with 1.5 m/s current -- you will drift downstream approximately 15-20m during crossing, which is fine since the downstream is clear). Tie the other end of the rope to a tree on the north bank. You now have a continuous rope spanning the river. | 10 min | 10 min |
| 2 | **Paddle Boat C back to the south bank**, following the rope upstream. Use the rope as a hand-over-hand guide to fight the current on the return trip. | 5 min | 15 min |
| 3 | **Position Boat B as midstream anchor.** Row Boat B out to the midpoint of the rope (~12.5m). Drop the mushroom anchor. Tie Boat B to the ferry rope with a loop knot that allows it to slide along the rope but also holds position when loaded. Boat B is now a stable midstream platform anchored to both the river bottom and the spanning rope. | 8 min | 23 min |
| 4 | **Use Boat A as near-bank loading platform.** Float Boat A in the shallows at the south bank. Its length (5.5m) and flat bottom make it a stable platform for loading supplies. The leak is irrelevant -- it stays in shallow water (<0.5m) where it cannot swamp even while leaking. Tie Boat A to the bank with a short line. | 5 min | 28 min |
| 5 | **Ferry supplies across using Boat C and the rope system.** Load 2 dry bags (100 kg) into Boat C at the south bank loading area (Boat A). Clip or loop Boat C onto the ferry rope. Pull hand-over-hand along the rope to cross to the north bank. The rope prevents drift; the paddles provide propulsion. Unload on the north bank. | 8 min per trip | 36 min (trip 1) |
| 6 | **Return and repeat.** Paddle/pull Boat C back along the rope. Load remaining 2 dry bags (100 kg). Cross again. | 12 min | 48 min (trip 2) |
| 7 | **Final crossing.** Return Boat C to the south bank one more time. Retrieve Boat B's anchor (or leave it). Cross to the north bank for the last time. | 10 min | 58 min |

**Total time: ~1 hour. Well within the 6-hour daylight window.**

### Physics Validation

- **Rope ferry (reaction ferry) principle**: A rope spanning a river with a boat attached allows hand-over-hand crossing. The rope bears the lateral force of the current, and the paddler only needs to provide forward motion. This reduces the effort of crossing from fighting the full current force to simply pulling along a fixed line. This is a well-established river crossing technique used historically and by modern wilderness travelers. Valid.

- **Current drift during initial crossing**: Boat C crossing 25m in 1.5 m/s current. Paddling speed in an inflatable with two paddles: approximately 2-3 km/h (0.55-0.83 m/s) perpendicular to current. Crossing time: 25m / 0.7 m/s (average) = ~36 seconds. Downstream drift: 1.5 m/s x 36s = ~54m. This is significant but within the 200m of clear downstream area. The paddler angles upstream to compensate, reducing net drift to ~15-20m. Valid.

- **Mushroom anchor holding in current**: A 5 kg mushroom anchor in a clay/silt river bottom provides approximately 25-50 kg of holding force once set. The lateral force on Boat B (3.0m x 0.3m underwater cross-section) from 1.5 m/s current is: F = 0.5 x Cd x rho x A x v^2 = 0.5 x 1.0 x 1000 x 0.9 x 2.25 = ~1,013 N (~103 kg-force). This exceeds the anchor's holding force. **The anchor alone may not hold.** The spanning rope, tensioned between two banks, provides the primary lateral restraint. The anchor prevents Boat B from sliding along the rope. The combined system holds. Valid with the rope system; invalid with anchor alone.

- **Boat A leak rate in shallows**: At 20 L/min leak rate in 0.5m of water, the hydrostatic pressure is lower than at full draft, reducing the leak to approximately 10-15 L/min. In shallows, the boat rests partially on the bottom, further reducing water ingress. As a stationary loading platform, the leak is manageable with occasional bailing. Valid.

- **Boat C payload**: 200 kg capacity. Two dry bags (100 kg) plus paddler (80 kg) = 180 kg. Within capacity. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **A spanning rope transforms the problem.** Without a rope, each boat must independently fight the current. With a rope between banks, boats can travel along it using hand-over-hand pulling, eliminating the main challenge (current). Getting the first rope across is the critical enabling step.

2. **Boat C is the initial pathfinder.** It is the only boat that can cross the river under its own power (two paddles, light, maneuverable). It crosses first, carrying the rope. After the rope is established, Boat C becomes a ferry running along the rope.

3. **Boat B's anchor stabilizes the midpoint.** The midstream platform reduces each leg of the ferry to ~12.5m, making each crossing more manageable. But more importantly, Boat B is the only boat with anchoring equipment, making it the natural midstream station.

4. **Boat A's leak is irrelevant in its assigned role.** As a near-bank loading platform in shallow water, the leak rate is reduced and the consequence of leaking is negligible (boat sits on the bottom in 0.5m of water). Assigning Boat A to a role where its weakness does not matter is the key insight.

5. **Each boat's strength compensates for another's weakness.** Boat C has mobility (paddles) but no anchor -- it is the ferry. Boat B has an anchor but limited mobility (one oar) -- it is the fixed station. Boat A has capacity and length but leaks -- it is the near-bank platform. The cascade of problems becomes a cascade of solutions when the right boat is assigned the right role.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Boat A's length (5.5m)**: Tempts the solver into bridge-building thinking. At 5.5m, it seems like "the big boat" that should span as much distance as possible. But its leak makes open-water use risky, and its length is most useful as a stable loading platform at the bank.
- **The single oar in Boat B**: Suggests Boat B should be rowed across. Sweep rowing a 3.0m aluminum dinghy in 1.5 m/s current with a single oar is exhausting. Boat B's real value is its watertight hull and anchor, not its rowing capability.
- **The broken paddle**: Tempts the solver to "repair" it or find a creative use. Its actual utility is marginal -- a 40cm paddle blade is usable as a short punt pole in shallows or a supplemental paddle, but it is not critical to the solution.
- **The "bridge" concept**: The scenario title ("Bridge of Boats") primes the solver to think about pontoon bridges. But the boats are too short to bridge the river. The solution is a ferry system, not a bridge.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests **cascade reasoning** -- the ability to see how multiple flawed components can be combined into a functional system. Each boat is individually inadequate. The solver must match each boat's strength to the system's needs and each boat's weakness to a role where that weakness is irrelevant.

The scenario title "Bridge of Boats" is a deliberate misdirection. It primes "bridge" thinking (static structure spanning the gap), but the solution is a "ferry" system (dynamic movement along a guide rope). Models that fixate on the bridge concept will struggle with the math (11.3m total length vs 25m width) and may conclude impossibility.

The rope ferry is a historically significant technique -- ferries operated on spanning ropes were common on rivers worldwide before modern bridges. The solution draws on practical engineering knowledge that predates industrial technology.
