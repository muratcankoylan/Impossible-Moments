# IM-0161: The Smoke Relay

**Category**: The Babel Problem
**Tier**: FRACTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: RELAY SMOKE SIGNALS THROUGH INTERMEDIATE CAMPS

---

## Scenario

You are the leader of a wilderness expedition in the highlands of central Kenya, operating in an area with no cellular coverage, no satellite phones, and no radio equipment. Your base camp is located at the southeast end of the Great Rift Valley escarpment. A supply depot is located 50 km to the northwest, at the top of the escarpment.

A medical emergency has occurred at base camp: one of your team members has been bitten by a puff adder (*Bitis arietans*). The antivenom is at the supply depot. Without antivenom, the patient will develop severe tissue necrosis within 6-8 hours and potentially fatal coagulopathy within 12-24 hours. Treatment is most effective within the first 4 hours.

You need to send a message to the supply depot requesting that antivenom be dispatched immediately by runner. The message is simple: "SEND ANTIVENOM NOW."

### The Distance Problem

The supply depot is 50 km away. At that distance:

- **Voice**: Human shouting is inaudible beyond ~400m in open terrain with wind. Voice communication across 50 km is physically impossible without electronic amplification.
- **Visual signals (direct)**: A bonfire is visible at approximately 10-15 km on a clear night in flat terrain. But the terrain is NOT flat -- the escarpment has ridges, valleys, and vegetation that block line-of-sight. Maximum line-of-sight between base camp and the depot is approximately 0 km (no direct visibility due to intervening ridges).
- **Running a messenger**: A fast runner covers 50 km in approximately 5-6 hours on trail, 8-10 hours on rough terrain. This is too slow -- antivenom must arrive within 4 hours of the bite, meaning the message must be sent NOW and the antivenom must travel back by runner (another 5-6 hours for the return trip). Total round trip: 10-12 hours. The patient needs antivenom within 4 hours. A single runner cannot make it in time.
- **Electronic communication**: No phone, no radio, no satellite device. Not available.

### The Route Between Base Camp and Supply Depot

The 50 km route between base camp and the supply depot passes through several intermediate locations where other expedition teams and friendly local communities are stationed:

| Location | Distance from Base Camp | Distance to Next Point | Elevation | Line of Sight |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base Camp | 0 km | 6 km to Camp 2 | 1,800 m | Can see Camp 2 (6 km, same valley) |
| Camp 2 (survey team) | 6 km | 5 km to Village A | 1,950 m | Can see Village A (5 km, on opposite ridge) |
| Village A (Maasai community) | 11 km | 4 km to Camp 3 | 2,100 m | Can see Camp 3 (4 km, across gorge) |
| Camp 3 (geology team) | 15 km | 7 km to Lookout | 2,200 m | Can see Lookout (7 km, ridge-to-ridge) |
| Lookout (unmanned cairn) | 22 km | 6 km to Village B | 2,400 m (ridgeline) | Can see Village B (6 km, below in valley) |
| Village B (Maasai community) | 28 km | 5 km to Camp 4 | 2,050 m | Can see Camp 4 (5 km, on hillside) |
| Camp 4 (resupply team) | 33 km | 8 km to Village C | 2,300 m | Can see Village C (8 km, across plateau) |
| Village C (Maasai community) | 41 km | 9 km to Supply Depot | 2,500 m | Can see Supply Depot (9 km, on escarpment top) |
| Supply Depot | 50 km | -- | 2,700 m | Destination |

**Key**: Each point can see the NEXT point (4-9 km line-of-sight), but NO point can see more than one hop ahead due to terrain obstructions. The chain must be unbroken.

### Available Resources

| Resource | Available at Each Location |
|---|---|
| Fire-making materials | Firewood, matches/lighter, dry grass for kindling -- available at ALL manned locations (camps and villages). The Lookout (unmanned) has a pre-built fire ring with a small cache of firewood and matches (maintained by the expedition). |
| Green vegetation | Abundant. Green branches, damp leaves, and fresh grass are available everywhere for producing thick white/gray smoke. |
| People | Each manned location has 2-6 people. All have been briefed on the expedition's emergency protocols, including smoke signaling. |
| Pre-arranged signals | The expedition has a simple pre-arranged smoke signal protocol: **One smoke column = attention/acknowledge. Two smoke columns = emergency/send help. Three smoke columns = all clear.** |
| Antivenom | Available ONLY at the Supply Depot. One dose. |

### Human Capabilities (assumed)

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Time to spot a smoke signal (once it's burning) | 1-5 minutes (depends on whether the observer is actively watching or must be alerted) |
| Time to build and light a signal fire | 3-8 minutes (fire-making materials are readily available; green vegetation for smoke is nearby) |
| Smoke visibility range | 4-10 km in clear air at this altitude and terrain |
| Wind conditions | Light (10-15 km/h), generally westward. Smoke columns remain coherent for 30-60 seconds before dispersing. |

### Constraints

| Constraint | Details |
|---|---|
| No electronic communication | No phone, radio, or satellite device at any location |
| No direct line of sight to depot | 50 km, multiple ridges, no direct visibility |
| Time constraint | Antivenom must be dispatched within ~1 hour for the runner to deliver it within 4 hours (3 hours of running from depot at 50/3 = ~17 km/hr... actually at 6-8 km/h on trail, the runner needs ~6 hours from the depot. This means the message must be sent ASAP to minimize total response time.) |
| Message complexity | Simple: "emergency, send help" (encoded as two simultaneous smoke columns) |
| Chain reliability | Each link depends on observers at the next station being alert and responsive |

## Why This Looks Impossible

50 km with no electronic communication and no direct line of sight. Voice does not carry. A single visual signal cannot span the distance. A runner is too slow for the round trip. The scenario appears to require either electronic communication or superhuman running speed.

The instinct is to focus on the endpoints: "how do I get a message from HERE to THERE?" This framing makes the problem seem like a single impossible communication link.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Send a runner to the supply depot" | A runner covering 50 km of rough terrain takes 6-8 hours one way. The antivenom must arrive within 4 hours of the bite. By the time the message arrives and the antivenom runner returns, the patient has been waiting 12-16 hours. Too slow. |
| "Build a very large fire visible at 50 km" | No fire is visible at 50 km through intervening ridges. Line-of-sight is blocked by terrain. Even on flat terrain, atmospheric conditions limit fire visibility to ~15-20 km. |
| "Use mirror signals" | Mirror signals (heliograph) require direct line of sight. Same problem: terrain blocks the path. A mirror signal cannot curve around ridges. |
| "Send a written message with a fast runner" | Same speed problem as running. A fast runner (marathon pace, 12-15 km/h on trails) still takes 3.5-4 hours for 50 km on rough terrain. Total round trip: 7-8 hours minimum. Too slow. |
| "Sound a horn or drum" | Sound attenuates to inaudibility beyond 1-3 km in open mountainous terrain with wind. Cannot span 50 km. |
| "Fly a kite or balloon with a visible marker" | No kite or balloon materials available. Even if available, wind direction and terrain make this impractical. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Break the 50 km into 8 Short Relay Segments

The 50 km distance is impossible for a single communication link. But it is trivially easy for a series of 4-9 km links. Each intermediate camp and village can see the next one. Each has fire-making materials and people who know the smoke signal protocol.

The message propagates as a relay: each station spots the signal from the previous station, builds its own signal, and passes the message to the next station. The information travels at the speed of human response (spot + build + signal), not the speed of physical transport.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Station | Action | Time | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Base Camp** | Build TWO simultaneous fires. Add green vegetation for thick white smoke. Two columns = emergency/send help. | 5-8 min | 5-8 min |
| 2 | **Camp 2** (6 km) | Observers spot two smoke columns from Base Camp. Recognize emergency signal. Build TWO fires with green vegetation, directing smoke skyward. | 3-8 min to spot + 5-8 min to build | 13-24 min |
| 3 | **Village A** (11 km) | Maasai community members spot two smoke columns from Camp 2. They have been briefed on the protocol. Build TWO fires. | 3-8 min + 5-8 min | 21-40 min |
| 4 | **Camp 3** (15 km) | Geology team spots signal from Village A. Build TWO fires. | 3-8 min + 5-8 min | 29-56 min |
| 5 | **Lookout** (22 km) | **UNMANNED**. No one is present to relay. | -- | -- |

**Problem**: The Lookout is unmanned. The relay chain breaks here.

**Solution**: Camp 3 must send a runner to the Lookout (7 km, approximately 1-1.5 hours on mountain terrain) to build the signal fire there. Alternatively, if the Lookout has a pre-built fire ring with cached firewood and matches, Camp 3 sends a fast runner who lights the pre-staged fire upon arrival.

**Revised plan**: Since the Lookout is unmanned, the relay chain has a gap. But the pre-staged fire ring with cached supplies means a runner from Camp 3 (7 km) can reach the Lookout and light the signal in approximately 1-1.5 hours. This adds significant delay.

**Better revised plan**: Skip the Lookout. Can Camp 3 (elevation 2,200m) see Village B (elevation 2,050m) directly? The distance is 13 km (7 + 6), and there is a ridgeline (the Lookout ridge at 2,400m) between them. Answer: NO. The ridge blocks the line of sight. The Lookout is necessary.

**Practical revised plan**: The expedition protocol should have a standing watch at the Lookout during daylight hours. Assuming the Lookout is manned (or that a runner from Camp 3 reaches it in 1-1.5 hours):

| Step | Station | Time from Last | Cumulative (best case, all manned) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Base Camp signals | 5-8 min | 5-8 min |
| 2 | Camp 2 relays | 6-12 min | 11-20 min |
| 3 | Village A relays | 6-12 min | 17-32 min |
| 4 | Camp 3 relays | 6-12 min | 23-44 min |
| 5 | Lookout relays (if manned) | 6-12 min | 29-56 min |
| 6 | Village B relays | 6-12 min | 35-68 min |
| 7 | Camp 4 relays | 6-12 min | 41-80 min |
| 8 | Village C relays | 6-12 min | 47-92 min |
| 9 | Supply Depot receives | 3-8 min (spot only) | 50-100 min |

**Best case: message arrives at the supply depot in approximately 50 minutes.**
**Worst case: approximately 100 minutes (1 hour 40 minutes).**
**Average estimate: approximately 70 minutes.**

### Antivenom Delivery Timeline

| Event | Time from Snakebite |
|---|---|
| Smoke relay begins | t+10 min (assessment, first aid, decision to signal) |
| Message arrives at supply depot | t+60 to t+110 min (relay time) |
| Antivenom dispatched by runner from depot | t+70 to t+120 min |
| Runner arrives at base camp (50 km, fresh runner at 8-10 km/h on trail) | t+370 to t+620 min (5-8.3 hours of running) |

**Total time from bite to antivenom: approximately 6-10 hours.**

This is marginally within the treatment window (antivenom is most effective within 4 hours but remains beneficial for up to 24 hours for puff adder envenomation). The relay drastically reduces the response time compared to sending a single runner (which would require 6-8 hours for the message alone, plus 6-8 hours for the return trip = 12-16 hours).

**Optimization**: The antivenom runner can meet a second runner coming from base camp at a midpoint, cutting the return time in half. Or the supply depot can dispatch the antivenom to the nearest point reachable by vehicle or animal (if available at the depot).

### Physics Validation

- **Smoke visibility at 4-9 km**: Thick white smoke from green vegetation (producing water vapor and fine particulates) creates a column visible at 5-15 km in clear mountain air at altitude. At 2,000+ m elevation, visibility is typically excellent (>30 km for large features). Two distinct smoke columns are distinguishable at up to 8-10 km. The relay distances (4-9 km) are well within this range. Valid.

- **Signal relay latency**: The time for each relay hop includes: observation latency (1-5 min for someone actively watching; up to 10 min if the observer is occupied and notices the smoke peripherally) + fire building time (3-8 min with prepared materials and tinder). Total per hop: 5-15 minutes. With 8 relay hops, total relay time is 40-120 minutes. Valid.

- **Historical signal relay networks**: The Roman Empire maintained signal fire networks (speculae) across its frontiers that could relay alerts over hundreds of kilometers in hours. The Chinese Great Wall had beacon towers every 5-10 km for exactly this purpose. The Maasai people of East Africa have a traditional smoke signaling system used for inter-community communication across the Rift Valley. This is documented anthropology. Valid.

- **Puff adder envenomation timeline**: *Bitis arietans* venom is primarily cytotoxic (tissue-destroying) with secondary hemotoxic (blood-clotting-disrupting) effects. Local tissue necrosis develops within 6-12 hours. Coagulopathy (inability to clot blood) may develop within 12-24 hours in severe envenomation. Antivenom is most effective within 4 hours but remains beneficial for up to 24 hours. The 6-10 hour delivery timeline via smoke relay is clinically relevant -- late but potentially life-saving. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Relay transforms an impossible distance into trivial segments.** 50 km is impossible for any single communication method available to the expedition. But eight segments of 4-9 km each are trivially easy for smoke signals. The relay principle converts an impossible single link into a chain of easy links.

2. **Information travels faster than matter.** A runner covering 50 km takes 6-8 hours. A relay signal covering 50 km takes ~1 hour. The information (the message) arrives 6-7x faster than a physical messenger could carry it. This is the fundamental advantage of relay communication over physical transport.

3. **Pre-arranged protocols are essential.** The relay works because all stations share a common signal code (two columns = emergency). Without this pre-arrangement, the smoke would be ambiguous. The protocol transforms raw "something is happening" into actionable "send help." This is the Babel Problem dimension: even simple communication requires minimal shared encoding.

4. **The chain is only as fast as its slowest link.** The unmanned Lookout creates a gap that could delay the relay by 1-1.5 hours. A strong expedition plan would ensure all relay points are manned during operational hours. The chain's reliability depends on every link being active.

5. **This is how ancient civilizations actually communicated.** Smoke relays, beacon fires, and signal towers are among the oldest long-distance communication technologies. They predate the telegraph by millennia. The scenario tests whether models can re-derive a technology that was obvious to ancient engineers but has been rendered invisible by modern telecommunications.

## Distractor Analysis

- **"Send a runner"** is the primary distractor. It is the most intuitive response to "send a message over 50 km." But running takes 6-8 hours for the message alone, and the critical constraint (antivenom within 4 hours) makes a single-runner solution too slow.
- **"Build a bigger fire"** is a secondary distractor. The instinct to overcome distance with amplitude (a bigger signal) ignores the terrain constraint: ridges block line-of-sight regardless of signal intensity. A bonfire visible at 15 km is useless if a ridge blocks the path at 7 km.
- **Mirror/heliograph signals** are a plausible-sounding alternative that fails for the same terrain reason. Mirrors require direct line-of-sight.
- **"The message is too simple for smoke signals"** is a reverse distractor -- the message is actually simple ENOUGH for smoke signals. Two columns = emergency. No complex encoding needed.
- **The unmanned Lookout** is a complication distractor that may cause models to abandon the relay approach entirely. The correct response is to plan for this gap (send a runner to man it) rather than discarding the relay concept.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to decompose an impossible point-to-point communication problem into a chain of feasible relay segments. The Babel Problem category tests communication under constraints, and this scenario specifically tests the relay principle: when you cannot communicate directly over a long distance, use intermediate stations to relay the message in hops.

The FRACTURE classification reflects that the core insight (relay the signal) requires one conceptual step beyond the naive approach (send a direct signal or runner), plus the practical engineering of managing the relay chain (timing, unmanned station, protocol).
