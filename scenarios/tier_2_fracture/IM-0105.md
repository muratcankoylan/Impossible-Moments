# IM-0105: The Matchless Furnace

**Category**: Last Ingredient

## Scenario

You are the owner of a small woodworking workshop in a rural area. It is 2:00 AM on a January night. The temperature outside is -18 degrees C (0 degrees F) and dropping. A winter storm has knocked out power to the area -- the utility company estimates restoration in 18-24 hours. Your workshop is heated by an electric forced-air system that is now dead. There is no backup generator.

The workshop contains your livelihood: $40,000 worth of woodworking equipment, a $15,000 inventory of premium hardwood lumber (walnut, cherry, maple), and an active commission -- a half-completed dining table for a client. More critically, the workshop's plumbing (a utility sink and a toilet for the attached half-bathroom) has copper water supply lines running along the exterior wall. These pipes are not insulated. At the current rate of cooling, the interior temperature will drop below 0 degrees C within approximately 3 hours. When the pipes freeze, the ice expansion (water expands 9% by volume when freezing) will burst the copper pipes. When power is restored and the pipes thaw, the resulting flood will destroy the lumber inventory and damage the equipment.

You need to heat the workshop -- or at least keep the pipe area above freezing -- for the next 18-24 hours. You have no heating fuel (no propane, no kerosene, no natural gas -- the workshop is all-electric), no wood stove, no fireplace, and no way to safely burn wood indoors (no chimney, no ventilation for combustion exhaust, risk of carbon monoxide poisoning and fire in a wood-filled shop).

### Environment

- **Workshop**: Single-story concrete block building, 12m x 8m x 3m ceiling height (288 m-cubed volume). Insulated walls (R-13 fiberglass in block cavities), insulated roof (R-30), concrete slab floor (uninsulated, on grade). One insulated steel overhead door (3m x 3m, R-8), one insulated entry door, four double-pane windows (each 1.2m x 0.8m). The building is reasonably well-sealed but not airtight.
- **Current interior temperature**: 12 degrees C (53.6 degrees F) and falling. The workshop was at 18 degrees C when the power went out at 10:00 PM (4 hours ago). Cooling rate: approximately 1.5 degrees C per hour.
- **Outside temperature**: -18 degrees C and steady (expected to stay between -18 and -22 degrees C through the night and following day).
- **Pipe location**: Copper water supply lines (15mm diameter, 3m total exposed length) run along the interior face of the north exterior wall, approximately 2m above floor level. The wall has R-13 insulation between the pipes and the outside, but the pipes are exposed to the interior air. Pipe freezing occurs when the air temperature around the pipes drops to approximately -1 to -2 degrees C (slightly below 0 due to the heat capacity of the water and the slow cooling rate).
- **Wind**: 30 km/h from the northwest, creating wind chill of -32 degrees C on the exterior walls.

### Threat

- **Pipe freezing timeline**: At the current cooling rate (1.5 degrees C/hour), the interior will reach 0 degrees C in approximately 8 hours (10:00 AM). However, the north wall area (closest to the exterior, least insulated from the wind-driven cold) will reach freezing approximately 2 hours sooner -- around 8:00 AM. After that, ice formation in the pipes takes approximately 1-2 hours. Pipe burst expected by 9:00-10:00 AM if no heat is provided.
- **Flood damage potential**: When power returns and the system pressurizes, burst pipes will discharge water at municipal pressure (approximately 400 kPa, 60 PSI). Flow rate through a burst 15mm copper pipe: approximately 20-30 liters per minute. Within 30 minutes, 600-900 liters of water will flood the workshop floor. Hardwood lumber (especially walnut and cherry) absorbs water rapidly through end grain, causing irreversible warping, staining, and degrade. Estimated damage: $15,000+ in lumber, $5,000-$10,000 in equipment repair/replacement, plus the lost commission.
- **Carbon monoxide risk**: Burning wood, charcoal, or any hydrocarbon fuel indoors without ventilation produces CO. Carbon monoxide concentration of 400 ppm causes headache within 1-2 hours; 800 ppm causes dizziness and nausea; 12,800 ppm causes death within 1-3 minutes. A small wood fire in a 288 m-cubed space would produce dangerous CO levels within 30-60 minutes. This rules out any open combustion as a heating solution.

### Your Position

- In the workshop, wearing heavy winter clothing. You have a battery-powered LED headlamp (12 hours of light remaining). You have your phone (40% battery, no signal -- tower is out).

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need heat, but every obvious heat source is unavailable:

1. **No electricity**: The forced-air heater is dead. No space heaters, no heat lamps, no electric radiators.
2. **No combustion fuel**: No propane, no kerosene, no natural gas. The workshop has no gas lines or fuel storage.
3. **No safe way to burn wood**: You have 50 kg of scrap wood and 30 kg of sawdust -- excellent fuel. But burning it indoors in a sealed workshop with no chimney or ventilation would produce lethal carbon monoxide levels within 30-60 minutes. Opening a door or window for ventilation would let in -18 degrees C air, negating the heating benefit and accelerating pipe freezing.
4. **No external help**: No phone signal, storm conditions, rural area. No one is coming until the power is restored.

The scenario has heat-generating material (wood) but no way to use it safely, and no other obvious fuel source. Every path seems blocked.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Calcium Oxide + Water = Exothermic Reaction

The 150 kg of quicklime (calcium oxide, CaO) in the workshop is a chemical heat source. When CaO reacts with water, it produces calcium hydroxide (slaked lime, Ca(OH)2) and releases significant heat:

**CaO + H2O -> Ca(OH)2 + 65.2 kJ/mol**

This is the same reaction used in self-heating food cans (MRE heaters), self-heating beverage cans, and historically in military ration heating. The reaction is vigorous, exothermic, and requires no ignition -- just the addition of water to the dry quicklime.

**Detailed heat loss calculation**:
- Walls: 2(12x3) + 2(8x3) = 120 m-squared. R-13 = RSI 2.3 m-squared-K/W. U = 0.435 W/m-squared-K. Q = 0.435 x 120 x 20 = 1044 W.
- Roof: 12 x 8 = 96 m-squared. R-30 = RSI 5.3. U = 0.189. Q = 0.189 x 96 x 20 = 363 W.
- Floor: 96 m-squared. Slab on grade, uninsulated. Effective U ~ 0.5-1.0 W/m-squared-K for perimeter losses. Q ~ 200-400 W.
- Windows: 4 x 1.2 x 0.8 = 3.84 m-squared. Double-pane U ~ 2.8 W/m-squared-K. Q = 2.8 x 3.84 x 20 = 215 W.
- Doors: overhead 9 m-squared at U = 0.71 (R-8). Q = 0.71 x 9 x 20 = 128 W. Entry door ~2 m-squared at U = 0.5. Q = 20 W.
- Infiltration: 0.5 ACH x 288 m-cubed x 1.2 kg/m-cubed x 1005 J/kg-K x 20 / 3600 = 965 W.
- **Total: approximately 1044 + 363 + 300 + 215 + 148 + 965 = 3035 W at delta-T = 20.**

Revised: at delta-T = 20, the heat loss is approximately 3000 W. To maintain the room above freezing (2 degrees C) with outside at -18 degrees C (delta-T = 20), you need approximately 3000 W continuously.

- Total available energy: 174,345 kJ = 48.4 kWh.
- Duration at 3000 W: 48,400 / 3000 = 16.1 hours.

This is not quite enough for 18-24 hours at 3000W. However, you do not need to maintain the entire workshop at 2 degrees C. You only need to keep the PIPE AREA above freezing. By creating a localized warm zone around the pipes (using plastic sheeting and moving blankets as an insulated enclosure), you can dramatically reduce the volume and surface area you need to heat.

### Revised Strategy: Localized Heating

Instead of heating the entire 288 m-cubed workshop, create an insulated enclosure around the pipe area:

- Pipe run: 3m long, along the north wall, at 2m height.
- Enclosure: Use plastic sheeting and moving blankets to create an insulated "tent" around the pipe area. Dimensions: approximately 3.5m long x 0.5m deep x 0.5m tall = 0.875 m-cubed.
- Heat loss from this enclosure: The outer surface (exposed to the cooling workshop air) is approximately 3.5 m-squared. With 2 layers of moving blanket (R-value ~2 per blanket), U ~ 1.5 W/m-squared-K. At delta-T = 5 degrees C (keeping the enclosure at 5 degrees C while the shop drops to 0 degrees C), Q = 1.5 x 3.5 x 5 = 26 W.
- At 26 W, the 174 MJ of heat energy lasts: 174,345,000 / 26 = 6,705,577 seconds = 1,863 hours. This is vastly more than needed.

Even accounting for the fact that the workshop itself will cool to well below 0 degrees C (increasing the delta-T across the enclosure), the energy budget is more than sufficient for localized pipe protection.

### Physics Validation

- **CaO + H2O reaction enthalpy**: The standard enthalpy of reaction for CaO(s) + H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(s) is delta-H = -65.2 kJ/mol. This is a well-established thermochemical value. For 150 kg of CaO (2674 mol), total heat = 174.3 MJ = 48.4 kWh. Valid.
- **Reaction temperature**: The CaO hydration reaction in a bucket with a 0.5:1 water-to-CaO ratio reaches peak temperatures of 80-150 degrees C depending on the rate of water addition and insulation. With excess water (ratio > 0.6:1), the peak temperature is lower but the reaction mass is larger, providing a longer-lasting heat source. In practice, the bucket contents will stabilize at 70-100 degrees C and cool gradually over 2-4 hours. Valid.
- **HDPE bucket compatibility**: HDPE has a maximum continuous service temperature of approximately 80-100 degrees C (depending on grade). The CaO reaction in a bucket may temporarily exceed this at the contact surface, causing slight softening but not failure. HDPE does not dissolve in the highly alkaline Ca(OH)2 solution (pH 12.4). For safety, filling the bucket only 1/3 full ensures the hot material does not overflow and the bucket walls are not in prolonged contact with the hottest material. Valid.
- **Building heat loss calculation**: The detailed U-value and infiltration calculation yields approximately 3000 W at delta-T = 20 degrees C. This is consistent with the observed cooling rate of 1.5 degrees C/hour: thermal mass of the building (concrete block walls, slab floor, equipment) stores approximately 288 m-cubed x 1.2 kg/m-cubed x 1005 J/kg-K + concrete mass ~ 100,000 kJ/K (estimated). At 3000 W loss, cooling rate ~ 3000 / (thermal mass per degree C). The observed 1.5 degrees C/hour implies an effective thermal mass of 3000 x 3600 / 1.5 = 7.2 MJ/K, which is reasonable for a concrete block building with equipment. Valid.
- **Localized enclosure effectiveness**: A 0.875 m-cubed enclosure insulated with two layers of moving blankets (total R-value ~4, or RSI 0.7) with exposed surface area of 3.5 m-squared loses heat at approximately 1.5/0.7 x 3.5 x delta-T = 7.5 x delta-T watts. At delta-T = 20 degrees C (enclosure at 2 degrees C, shop at -18 degrees C worst case), Q = 150 W. At this rate, each 15 kg batch of CaO (17,440 kJ) lasts 17,440,000/150 = 116,267 seconds = 32 hours. The energy budget is more than sufficient. Valid.
- **Safety: Ca(OH)2 is caustic but not toxic by inhalation in solid form.** The main safety risks are: (a) skin burns from contact with wet CaO or Ca(OH)2 solution (pH 12.4), mitigated by nitrile gloves and goggles; (b) inhalation of dry CaO dust, mitigated by the P100 respirator; (c) steam burns during the reaction, mitigated by adding water slowly and not leaning over the bucket. The reaction does not produce toxic gases -- the only emissions are steam and heat. This is why CaO is used in MRE heaters (sealed in a food package). Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The Last Ingredient is already in the workshop.** The 150 kg of quicklime, purchased for an unrelated purpose (lime washing), is a chemical heating fuel. It does not burn, does not produce carbon monoxide, does not require a chimney, and does not need ignition. It just needs water.
2. **Chemical energy replaces combustion energy.** The CaO hydration reaction releases 65.2 kJ/mol without combustion. There is no flame, no CO, no smoke, no fire risk. This makes it safe for indoor use in a sealed space -- the exact conditions where burning wood is lethal.
3. **Localized heating beats whole-room heating.** Heating 288 m-cubed to above freezing requires approximately 3000 W -- feasible but energy-intensive. Heating a 0.875 m-cubed enclosure around the pipes requires approximately 26-150 W -- an order of magnitude less. The insulated enclosure strategy stretches the available chemical energy from "barely enough" to "vastly more than enough."
4. **The scrap wood is the trap.** 50 kg of excellent fuel sitting right there, screaming "burn me." But burning it indoors is lethal (CO poisoning). The scrap wood is the scenario's primary misdirection -- it draws attention toward combustion thinking and away from chemical reaction thinking.
5. **Safety requires respect for the chemistry.** CaO is not benign. It causes severe chemical burns, and the reaction produces dangerously hot material and steam. The safety gear (gloves, goggles, respirator) is essential. A solution that suggests "dump quicklime and water together" without mentioning safety precautions is dangerously incomplete.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The scrap wood and sawdust** are the primary distractors. They are obvious, abundant fuel -- if only you could burn them safely. Every combustion-based solution (wood fire, sawdust fire, charcoal kiln) fails due to the CO risk in a sealed space. The wood is a trap.
- **The moving blankets** suggest an insulation-only approach (wrap the pipes, insulate the building). Insulation delays freezing but cannot prevent it during an 18-24 hour outage at -18 degrees C. However, blankets ARE essential as a supplementary measure for the insulated enclosure.
- **The plastic sheeting** has a dual-use quality (insulation tent construction + covering lumber), but on its own it provides minimal insulation. Its value is as a vapor barrier and structural component of the pipe enclosure.
- **Draining the pipes** is a legitimate emergency plumbing response that earns partial credit. However, it does not address the broader workshop protection (lumber, equipment, glue joints all suffer in below-freezing conditions), and most people will not think of it because the scenario primes them to think about "heating."
- **The "open a window and burn wood" approach** is the most dangerous wrong answer. It attempts to balance combustion exhaust with cold air intake, but the arithmetic does not work: the ventilation needed for safe combustion (approximately 10 m-cubed/minute for a small wood fire) overwhelms the fire's heating capacity in -18 degrees C conditions.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests whether the model can identify a non-obvious chemical energy source when all conventional heating methods are unavailable. The Last Ingredient category means the solution requires recognizing an available material (quicklime) as a heat source -- a use that is not its primary purpose (it was bought for lime washing).

The scenario deliberately creates a strong attractor toward combustion thinking: scrap wood, sawdust, and the intuitive association between "heating" and "fire." The model must recognize that fire is unavailable (CO risk) and pivot to chemical exothermic reactions, which provide heat without combustion.

The energy calculation is a critical element. A model that identifies the CaO reaction but does not estimate the available energy cannot confirm that the solution is physically sufficient. The calculation reveals that 150 kg of CaO provides ample energy for localized pipe heating but is marginal for whole-workshop heating -- this distinction between localized and whole-room strategies is the secondary insight.
