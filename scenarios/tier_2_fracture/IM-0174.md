# IM-0174: The Pipe Chase

**Category**: Locked Room
**Tier**: FRACTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: REMOVE THE PIPE CHASE ACCESS PANEL AND CLIMB THROUGH THE VERTICAL SHAFT TO THE NEXT FLOOR

---

## Scenario

You are locked in a mechanical room in the basement of a four-story commercial building. You were sent down by your supervisor to read the water meter for a monthly report. The mechanical room door -- a heavy steel fire door -- closed behind you and the lock engaged. This door requires a key from both sides (a code violation, but the building is old and the lock was never updated). Your key ring, which you set on a shelf outside the door while you squeezed through with your clipboard, is on the other side.

Your phone has no signal in the basement. The building is mostly empty -- it is Saturday afternoon, and only a security guard is on duty somewhere on the upper floors. You shouted, but the mechanical room has concrete block walls and a concrete ceiling. The sound does not carry.

The mechanical room contains the building's water heater, water softener, main water shutoff, and the associated piping. Pipe chases -- vertical shafts where water, sewer, and gas pipes run between floors -- penetrate the ceiling and continue upward through all four floors.

### Environment

- **Room**: 4m x 3m, ceiling height 2.7m. Concrete block walls, poured concrete floor and ceiling. The room is dry, well-lit (fluorescent ceiling lights on a timer -- they will turn off in approximately 2 hours but that is not the primary concern), and temperature-controlled (approximately 20 degrees C).
- **Door**: Steel fire door, 0.9m x 2.1m. Keyed lock (no interior handle mechanism -- requires key from both sides). Hinges are on the outside. The door fits tightly in a steel frame with no gap large enough to manipulate the lock from inside.
- **Pipe chases**: Two vertical pipe chases penetrate the ceiling.
  - **Chase A** (east wall): Contains the main water supply riser (75mm copper pipe), a hot water return pipe (25mm copper), and associated fittings. The chase is a rectangular shaft, 60cm x 60cm in cross-section, running from the basement to the roof. It is enclosed on each floor by drywall (in the occupied spaces) but is open on the mechanical room side via an access panel.
  - **Chase B** (north wall): Contains the main sewer stack (100mm cast iron pipe) and a gas pipe (25mm black iron). Same 60cm x 60cm cross-section. Has a similar access panel.
  - **Access panels**: Each pipe chase has a removable access panel at the mechanical room level. The panels are 60cm x 60cm, made of 16-gauge sheet metal (1.5mm thick steel), and secured by four thumbscrews (hand-tightened, no tools required). The panels are at floor level, with the bottom edge of the panel at 20cm above the floor.
- **Above the mechanical room**: The first floor (ground level) contains a janitor's closet directly above Chase A. The janitor's closet has a similar 60cm x 60cm access panel on its side of the pipe chase. The closet door opens into a hallway.

### Threat

- **Confinement**: You are locked in a basement room with no phone signal and no one who can hear you. The building is mostly empty.
- **Time**: The security guard makes rounds every 4 hours. If you are not discovered, you could be in this room until Monday morning (approximately 40 hours). While not immediately life-threatening (there is water from the plumbing), 40 hours in a locked basement room is an unacceptable situation.
- **Lighting**: The fluorescent lights are on a 2-hour timer. After they turn off, you will be in complete darkness unless you can find the light switch (which may or may not be inside the room).

### Your Position

- Standing in the mechanical room, facing the locked door. You have just confirmed the door is locked and your phone has no signal.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are in a locked room with concrete walls, a steel door (keyed from both sides), no phone signal, and no one to hear you. The room is in a basement. There are no windows. The door cannot be opened without a key. The concrete block walls cannot be breached by hand.

Most people mentally model a room as a six-sided box: four walls, a floor, and a ceiling. If the door is locked and the walls are solid, the box is sealed. But this room is not a sealed box. The pipe chases are openings in the ceiling that connect to every floor above.

The pipe chases are "invisible" as escape routes because:
1. They are covered by access panels (they look like wall, not openings).
2. Pipe chases are infrastructure, not architecture -- people do not think of utility shafts as passages.
3. The idea of climbing a 60cm x 60cm shaft feels dangerous and unusual, even though it is physically feasible.

### Common Wrong Answers

| Wrong Answer | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "Force the door open" | Steel fire door in a steel frame, keyed on both sides. A human cannot force a properly installed fire door. It is designed to resist exactly this. |
| "Pick the lock with the pen" | The lock is a keyed cylinder, not a simple latch. Lock picking requires specialized tools and skill. A ballpoint pen cannot be used to pick a commercial lock cylinder. |
| "Remove the door hinges" | The hinges are on the outside of the door (standard for fire doors -- they swing outward for egress). You cannot access the hinge pins from inside. |
| "Break through the concrete wall" | Concrete block walls (standard 20cm CMU, grouted and reinforced) cannot be breached by hand or with a clipboard. Even with tools, it would take hours. |
| "Wait for the security guard" | Possible but the guard makes rounds every 4 hours and may not check the basement. Worst case: 40 hours until Monday morning. Not acceptable when an escape route exists. |
| "Shout louder" | Concrete walls and a steel door provide approximately 50-60 dB of sound reduction. Even shouting at 90 dB, the sound in the hallway outside is approximately 30-40 dB -- barely audible and easily masked by building HVAC noise. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Pipe Chases Are Passages Between Floors. Remove the Access Panel and Climb Up.

Pipe chases in commercial buildings are vertical shafts, typically 45-90 cm in cross-section, that carry plumbing, electrical, and HVAC services between floors. They are enclosed by access panels on each floor for maintenance. In this building, the pipe chases are 60cm x 60cm -- large enough for an adult to enter and climb.

### Physics Validation

- **Pipe chase dimensions**: 60cm x 60cm (24 inches x 24 inches) is a standard pipe chase size in commercial buildings for main risers. Building codes (IPC, UPC) require pipe chases to be accessible for maintenance, which is why access panels exist. A 60 cm shaft accommodates a person with 48 cm shoulder width. Valid.
- **Thumbscrew access panels**: Removable access panels secured by thumbscrews or quarter-turn fasteners are standard in commercial plumbing installations. They are specifically designed for tool-free removal. Valid.
- **Climbing feasibility**: Chimney-climbing (bridging) technique works in shafts of 45-90 cm width. At 60 cm, the climber can brace back against one wall and feet against the opposite wall, creating friction holds. Combined with pipe clamps as footholds every 1.5 meters, a 3-meter vertical climb is physically demanding but achievable for a reasonably fit adult. Rock climbers routinely chimney-climb cracks of this width. Valid.
- **Copper pipe load-bearing**: A 75mm Type L copper pipe (wall thickness ~1.65 mm) supported by clamps every 1.5 meters can support approximately 100-200 kg of lateral load before deforming. An 80 kg person gripping the pipe distributes load across multiple clamp points. The pipe may deform slightly but will not fail catastrophically under a brief dynamic load during climbing. Valid.
- **Pipe clamp as foothold**: Standard pipe clamps (U-bolt or split ring type) protrude 3-5 cm from the wall, providing a small but usable ledge for a boot toe. At 1.5-meter intervals, these serve as intermediate rest points during the climb. Valid.
- **Sound attenuation through concrete**: Concrete block walls provide approximately 45-55 dB of sound transmission loss (STC rating for 20cm CMU: STC 46-52). A steel door adds approximately 30-40 dB. Combined attenuation from room to hallway: 50-60 dB. A shout at 90 dB reduces to 30-40 dB in the hallway -- below conversational speech level. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Pipe chases are vertical passages.** They are designed as maintenance access corridors for plumbing and electrical services. They connect every floor of a building. In an emergency, they are alternative routes between floors.
2. **Access panels are removable by design.** The thumbscrews are there specifically so that maintenance workers can open the panels without tools. This is not "breaking into" the pipe chase -- it is using the designed access mechanism.
3. **A room with pipe chases is not a sealed box.** The mental model of "four walls, ceiling, floor = sealed" breaks down when utility shafts penetrate the boundary. The pipe chases are holes in the ceiling that are covered, not sealed.
4. **Building infrastructure is invisible until you need it.** Pipe chases, duct shafts, cable trays, and service corridors are the hidden nervous system of buildings. Most people are unaware they exist because they are covered by access panels and hidden behind walls. In an emergency, this infrastructure becomes a potential escape route.
5. **The climb is physically demanding but not dangerous.** A 3-meter vertical climb in a 60 cm shaft with handholds every 1.5 meters is strenuous but well within the capability of an average adult. It feels risky because climbing pipe chases is unusual, not because it is actually hazardous.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The locked door** dominates attention. It is the obvious barrier. Every instinct says "get the door open." But the door is a steel fire door with a keyed lock on both sides -- it is designed to resist exactly this. Fighting the door wastes time.
- **The clipboard and pen** suggest communication (write a note, slip it under the door) or lock manipulation. A note under the door requires someone to walk past and notice it. Lock manipulation with a pen is not viable on a commercial cylinder lock.
- **Chase B (sewer stack)** is a viable alternative but less pleasant. The sewer stack may have waste odor, and cast iron is rougher to grip. Chase A (water supply) is preferable for comfort but both are viable.
- **The fluorescent light timer** creates false urgency. The lights going off in 2 hours feels threatening, but the pipe chase escape takes only 13 minutes. The timer is irrelevant.
- **The water heater and plumbing equipment** fill the room with mechanical complexity that distracts from the simple observation that the pipe chases are passages, not just equipment housings.
- **Shouting** feels productive but is futile through 50-60 dB of concrete and steel attenuation.
