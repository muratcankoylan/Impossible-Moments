# IM-0122: The Ground Wave

**Category**: Babel Problem
**Tier**: FRACTURE
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: COMMUNICATE BY STRIKING THE ROCKY GROUND WITH HAMMERS -- SEISMIC WAVES TRAVEL THROUGH ROCK AND CAN BE FELT BY THE OTHER PERSON

---

## Scenario

Two people -- you and a partner -- are separated by a rocky hill in a remote wilderness area. You are on the north side; your partner is on the south side. The hill is approximately 200m wide at the base and 80m tall, composed of solid granite. There is no line of sight between you (the hill completely blocks visual contact). There is no path over the hill that can be traversed in less than 45 minutes (steep, dense vegetation on the slopes).

You need to communicate a simple numerical message to your partner: a 4-digit grid reference (e.g., "4217") that indicates a rendezvous point on a shared map. You must transmit this number so your partner can read the map and meet you at the correct location.

You have no radio, no cell phone, no signal mirror (no line of sight anyway), no writing materials that could be sent. You cannot shout -- the hill absorbs and blocks sound; even at maximum human vocal output (~100 dB at 1m), the sound would attenuate below detection threshold well before reaching the far side of a 200m solid rock mass.

### Environment

- **Terrain**: Granite hill, approximately 200m wide at the base, 80m tall. The rock is solid, competent granite with minimal fracturing. Exposed rock faces on both sides. Both you and your partner are standing on exposed rock surfaces (not on soil or vegetation).
- **Distance through rock**: Approximately 200m of solid granite between your position and your partner's position (straight-line through the hill).
- **Weather**: Clear, calm. No wind. Ambient noise is very low (~25 dB -- remote wilderness).
- **Time of day**: Midday. Good visibility (not relevant due to no line of sight).
- **Vegetation**: Dense brush and trees on the hill slopes. No tall trees on the exposed rock surfaces where you are standing.

### Threat

- **Time constraint**: You must transmit the 4-digit number within 30 minutes. After that, your partner will proceed to a default rendezvous point that is suboptimal (farther, more difficult terrain).
- **Coordination failure**: If your partner cannot receive the message, they go to the wrong location, and you lose 4-6 hours of daylight re-coordinating.

### Your Position

- Standing on exposed granite bedrock on the north side of the hill. Your partner is on exposed granite bedrock on the south side, approximately 200m away through solid rock.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need to send a 4-digit number to someone you cannot see, hear, or physically reach. No electronic communication is available. No line of sight for visual signaling. No air path for sound (the hill blocks acoustic transmission). You have only a hammer and a rock.

The scenario seems to reduce to: two people separated by a solid wall of rock with no communication channel.

The apparent impossibility stems from thinking of communication as requiring air-borne or electromagnetic transmission. We naturally think of talking, shouting, visual signals, or radio. All of these are blocked by the hill.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Seismic Waves Travel Through Solid Rock. Strike the Rock with the Hammer -- the Vibrations Will Travel Through the Granite Hill to the Other Side.

Rock is an excellent conductor of mechanical (seismic) waves. Granite transmits compressional waves (P-waves) at approximately 5000-6000 m/s. A hammer strike on exposed bedrock generates a seismic pulse that travels through the rock mass at this speed and can be detected on the far side. At 200m through granite, the travel time is approximately 200/5500 = 0.036 seconds (36 milliseconds) -- essentially instantaneous.

The receiver detects the vibration by pressing an ear to the rock surface or by feeling the vibration through their feet or hands placed on the rock. The human ear pressed against rock can detect vibrations with particle velocities as low as 0.01 mm/s. A hammer strike on granite at 200m produces particle velocities on the order of 0.01-0.1 mm/s -- detectable but faint. Pressing the ear to the rock significantly improves detection compared to standing.

### Communication Protocol

Since no code was pre-arranged, both people must converge on a simple encoding. The most natural approach for transmitting digits is a unary (count-based) code:

- Each digit is transmitted as a series of strikes equal to the digit value.
- Zero is encoded as 10 strikes (since 0 strikes would be indistinguishable from silence).
- Digits are separated by a pause (3-5 seconds of silence).
- The message boundary (start/end of the 4-digit number) is marked by a distinctive pattern: a rapid burst of strikes (e.g., 5 rapid strikes in 1 second).

**Example: Transmitting "4217"**

| Phase | Action | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Start signal | 5 rapid strikes (tap-tap-tap-tap-tap in 1 second) | 1 s |
| Pause | Silence | 3 s |
| Digit 1: "4" | 4 evenly spaced strikes (1 per second) | 4 s |
| Pause | Silence | 5 s |
| Digit 2: "2" | 2 evenly spaced strikes | 2 s |
| Pause | Silence | 5 s |
| Digit 3: "1" | 1 strike | 1 s |
| Pause | Silence | 5 s |
| Digit 4: "7" | 7 evenly spaced strikes | 7 s |
| Pause | Silence | 3 s |
| End signal | 5 rapid strikes | 1 s |
| | **Total: approximately 37 seconds** | |

Repeat the full message 3-5 times with 30-second pauses between repetitions to ensure the partner has time to detect and decode. Total transmission time: approximately 5-8 minutes.

### Physics Validation

- **P-wave velocity in granite**: Compressional wave velocity in intact granite ranges from 4500-6500 m/s, with typical values around 5000-5500 m/s. At 200m distance, travel time is 200/5500 = 36 ms. Valid.
- **Attenuation in granite**: Seismic wave attenuation in granite is very low. The quality factor Q for granite is typically 200-1000 (higher Q = lower attenuation). At 200m distance, the amplitude reduction from attenuation is minimal -- perhaps 10-30% for a single passage. The dominant amplitude reduction comes from geometric spreading (1/r for body waves), which reduces amplitude by a factor of ~200 over 200m distance. For a hammer strike generating ~0.1 mm/s particle velocity at 1m distance, the particle velocity at 200m is approximately 0.1/200 = 0.0005 mm/s. This is below the threshold for detection through feet (~0.1 mm/s) but detectable with an ear pressed to rock (~0.01 mm/s threshold) if the initial strike is stronger. Increasing hammer force and striking a more competent surface can increase the source amplitude. A strong geological hammer strike on competent granite can generate 1-10 mm/s at 1m, yielding 0.005-0.05 mm/s at 200m -- within the ear-on-rock detection range. Valid with margin.
- **Human seismic detection**: Humans can detect vibrations through the body at particle velocities as low as 0.1 mm/s (through feet). With the ear pressed to a solid surface, the threshold drops to approximately 0.01 mm/s due to the ear's sensitivity to mechanical vibration in contact mode. Miners have historically communicated through rock by tapping for centuries. This is a well-documented capability. Valid.
- **Ambient noise**: In a remote wilderness with low wind, seismic ambient noise on bedrock is extremely low (microseismic noise is typically 0.001-0.01 mm/s in the 0.1-1 Hz band). Hammer strikes produce signals in the 10-100 Hz range, where ambient noise is even lower. The signal-to-noise ratio for hammer strikes through 200m of granite is favorable. Valid.
- **Frequency content**: A hammer strike on rock produces a broad-spectrum pulse with peak energy in the 50-500 Hz range. This is well within the human audible range (20-20,000 Hz) when detected through bone/rock conduction. The strikes will sound like sharp taps or clicks when heard with ear to rock. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Rock is a communication medium.** We think of communication as traveling through air (sound) or through electromagnetic spectrum (light, radio). But solid rock transmits mechanical waves faster and with less attenuation than air over this distance. The hill is not a barrier -- it IS the communication channel.
2. **Seismic communication is ancient.** Miners, quarry workers, and tunnel builders have communicated through rock by tapping for centuries. Aboriginal Australians used ground vibrations for long-distance signaling. This is not a novel concept -- but it is one that modern, technology-dependent thinking overlooks.
3. **The ear is a seismic sensor.** Pressing the ear to rock transforms it from an air-pressure sensor (microphone) to a vibration sensor (geophone). The detection threshold drops by an order of magnitude. This technique is instinctive in many cultures ("put your ear to the ground to hear distant horses").
4. **Simple encoding suffices.** A 4-digit number requires only counted strikes with pauses. No pre-arranged Morse code, no binary encoding, no complex protocol. The most natural encoding (count the strikes) is the most robust because it requires the least prior agreement.
5. **Repetition provides error correction.** Repeating the message 3-5 times and requesting echo-back confirmation is a primitive but effective error-correction protocol, analogous to checksums in digital communication.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The compass** is a mild distractor. It has no communication function. A model might attempt to use its metallic components for signaling, but without line of sight, this is useless.
- **The water bottles** are irrelevant to communication. A model might suggest using the bottle as a sound amplifier or resonator, but this does not help with the fundamental problem of getting sound through/around the hill.
- **The backpack** contains no useful communication tools by specification. A model that invents contents for the backpack is hallucinating.
- **The topographic map** is the MESSAGE, not the MEDIUM. It is essential for understanding WHAT to communicate (the grid reference) but does not help with HOW to communicate.
- **Walking around the hill** is a valid but time-exceeding option. It is the "obvious" solution that the time constraint is designed to eliminate.
- **Shouting** is the most natural communication instinct and the most tempting wrong answer. The physics of acoustic attenuation through/around a solid rock mass makes shouting futile at this distance.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to recognize unconventional communication channels. The "Babel Problem" framing emphasizes that two people who share a language but have no communication medium must find one. The solution requires reframing the hill from "obstacle" to "medium" -- the very object that prevents conventional communication IS the channel for seismic communication.

The scenario is calibrated so that the physics works but is not trivially obvious. The attenuation calculation shows that detection is feasible but marginal -- strong strikes and ear-to-rock detection are both necessary. A model that gets the concept right but does not consider whether the signal is actually detectable at 200m shows incomplete reasoning.
