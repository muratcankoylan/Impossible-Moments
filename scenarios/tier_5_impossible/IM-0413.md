# IM-0413: The Terraforming Problem

**Category**: The Horizon Problem
**Tier**: IMPOSSIBLE
**Status**: OF (Open-Frontier)
**Correct Outcome**: PROPOSE NOVEL ATMOSPHERIC ENGINEERING (no proven solution exists)

---

## Scenario

You are the lead atmospheric engineer for the Ares Consortium's Mars terraforming project. Your mandate: transform Mars's atmosphere from its current state to one that supports liquid water on the surface. The timeline is "centuries, not millennia" -- you must propose approaches that show measurable atmospheric change within 200 years.

### Current Mars Atmospheric Conditions

| Parameter | Value | Required for Liquid Water |
|---|---|---|
| Surface pressure | 610 Pa (0.6% of Earth) | >611 Pa at minimum (water triple point); practically ~6,000-10,000 Pa for stable liquid water at habitable temperatures |
| Atmospheric composition | 95.3% CO2, 2.7% N2, 1.6% Ar, 0.13% O2, 0.08% CO | Irrelevant for liquid water stability (CO2 atmosphere can support liquid water if pressure is sufficient) |
| Average surface temperature | -60C (213 K) | >0C (273 K) for liquid water; practically -20C to +20C for surface habitability |
| Total atmospheric mass | ~2.5 x 10^16 kg | ~5 x 10^18 kg needed for 10,000 Pa surface pressure (~200x current) |
| Solar irradiance at Mars | 590 W/m^2 (43% of Earth's 1,370 W/m^2) | Cannot be changed (fundamental constraint) |
| Magnetic field | Essentially zero (no global dipole) | Without magnetic shielding, solar wind strips atmosphere at ~100 g/s (~3 x 10^6 kg/year). Any atmosphere must be replenished faster than it is stripped. |

### The Mass Problem

The fundamental challenge is atmospheric mass. Mars currently has ~2.5 x 10^16 kg of atmosphere. A pressure of 10,000 Pa (the minimum for comfortable surface habitation with liquid water) requires ~5 x 10^18 kg -- a factor of 200 increase.

**Where could this mass come from?**

| Source | Estimated Available Mass | Delivery Challenge |
|---|---|---|
| Martian polar caps (CO2 ice) | ~1 x 10^16 kg CO2 ice (both caps combined) | Sublimating both caps doubles atmospheric pressure to ~1,200 Pa. Still ~10x too low. |
| Martian regolith (adsorbed CO2) | ~1-5 x 10^16 kg (highly uncertain) | Requires heating the top few meters of regolith across the entire planet. Energy cost: ~10^24 joules. |
| Martian carbonates (CO2 locked in rock) | ~1-10 x 10^17 kg (highly uncertain, possibly much more) | Requires heating carbonate rocks to >500C to release CO2. Energy cost: orders of magnitude beyond current technology. |
| Comet bombardment | Comets are 40-80% water ice + volatiles. A 10 km diameter comet delivers ~5 x 10^14 kg | Would need ~10,000 large comets. Each impact releases energy equivalent to ~10^25 joules, raising surface temperature dramatically but also sterilizing the surface. Trajectory modification of thousands of comets is far beyond any foreseeable technology. |
| Asteroid volatiles | Carbonaceous chondrite asteroids contain 3-22% water by mass + trapped volatiles | Similar delivery problem to comets, with lower volatile fraction. |
| Ammonia import (from outer solar system) | NH3 is a powerful greenhouse gas and nitrogen source | Must be sourced from Titan (atmosphere is 98.4% N2 + CH4 lakes) or Neptune/Uranus system. Transport distances: 8-28 AU. Energy costs prohibitive with any foreseeable propulsion technology. |

### The Temperature Problem

Even if sufficient atmospheric mass is obtained, the average surface temperature must increase by ~80C (from -60C to +20C for comfortable surface conditions, or at minimum by 60C to reach 0C).

Mars receives only 43% of Earth's solar irradiance. With a pure CO2 atmosphere at 10,000 Pa, the greenhouse effect is approximately 10C (CO2 is a relatively weak greenhouse gas at these pressures). To reach habitable temperatures, additional greenhouse warming of 50-70C is required.

**Known greenhouse approaches and their limitations:**

| Approach | Warming Potential | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| CO2 greenhouse (at 10,000 Pa) | ~10C | Insufficient alone. And sourcing the CO2 mass is itself the primary problem. |
| Super-greenhouse gases (CFCs, SF6, PFCs) | Potentially +30-50C at low concentrations (100-1000 ppm) | Manufacturing millions of tonnes of industrial gases on Mars requires a mature industrial base that presumes the habitability you are trying to create. Chicken-and-egg problem. CFCs also destroy ozone, preventing UV-protective ozone layer formation. |
| Albedo reduction (darkening the surface) | ~5-15C (reducing albedo from 0.25 to 0.15) | Requires covering ~10% of the planet's surface with dark material. Mars's surface area is 1.45 x 10^14 m^2; 10% = 1.45 x 10^13 m^2. What dark material? Biological (lichens, dark algae) requires liquid water that does not yet exist. Artificial (soot, carbon black) requires industrial production at planetary scale. |
| Orbital mirrors | Varies with mirror size. A 250 km diameter mirror at L1 could increase solar flux by ~25%, adding ~10-15C | Manufacturing and deploying mirrors of this scale in orbit requires a space-industrial infrastructure orders of magnitude beyond anything foreseeable. Mass of a 250 km mirror (1 micrometer thick aluminum): ~1.4 x 10^8 kg. Must be manufactured from asteroid material. |

### The Stripping Problem

Mars has no global magnetic field. The solar wind strips the Martian atmosphere at approximately 100 g/s (~3,000 tonnes/year, or ~3 x 10^6 kg/year). At this rate, the current atmosphere would be fully stripped in ~8 million years. This seems slow, but it means any atmospheric building must outpace the loss.

For a 200-year project adding ~5 x 10^18 kg of atmosphere, the stripping loss is ~6 x 10^8 kg -- negligible compared to the total (0.000012%). So atmospheric stripping is NOT actually a meaningful constraint on a 200-year timescale. However, for long-term stability (thousands of years), it becomes significant.

**This is a common misconception**: many analyses of Mars terraforming cite atmospheric stripping as a primary obstacle. In fact, the stripping rate is far too slow to matter on engineering timescales. It matters on geological timescales (millions of years) but not on human project timescales (hundreds of years). A model that cites magnetic field loss as the primary obstacle is demonstrating a common error.

### In-Situ Resource Constraints

The problem specifies "using only in-situ resources" -- no importing mass from Earth, asteroids, or comets. Everything must come from Mars itself.

**Mars's in-situ volatile inventory:**
- Polar caps: ~1 x 10^16 kg CO2 ice
- Regolith-adsorbed CO2: ~1-5 x 10^16 kg (uncertain)
- Subsurface water ice: estimated 5 x 10^6 km^3 (confirmed by radar) = ~5 x 10^18 kg of water ice. This is a MASSIVE water reservoir -- enough for oceans. But water vapor is not an efficient greenhouse gas at the low temperatures on Mars (it freezes out).
- Carbonate minerals: CO2 locked in Noachian-era carbonate rocks. Total inventory is highly uncertain -- could be 10^17 to 10^19 kg of CO2. Releasing it requires thermal decomposition at >500C.

The most promising in-situ resource is the carbonate mineral inventory, but the energy required to decompose it is the critical bottleneck.

## Why This Looks Impossible

Every known approach to Mars terraforming fails under the in-situ-only constraint:

1. **Sublimating the polar caps** doubles the pressure but reaches only ~1,200 Pa -- still 8-10x too low.
2. **Releasing regolith-adsorbed CO2** might add another factor of 2-5, reaching 2,400-6,000 Pa at best. Still likely insufficient, and the energy cost is enormous (heating the top meters of an entire planet's surface).
3. **Decomposing carbonate rocks** could theoretically release enough CO2, but requires temperatures >500C applied to massive volumes of rock. The energy is ~10^24 to 10^26 joules, equivalent to total human energy production over centuries to millennia.
4. **Greenhouse gases** require industrial production that requires the habitability being created.
5. **Albedo modification** requires biological agents that require liquid water that requires higher pressure and temperature.

Each approach partially works but fails at scale, and the approaches that address temperature do not address pressure and vice versa. The problem is irreducibly complex: pressure, temperature, and industrial capacity are coupled, and bootstrapping from Mars's current state to habitability encounters chicken-and-egg deadlocks at every stage.

## Evaluation Criteria

This is an IMPOSSIBLE-tier OPEN-FRONTIER problem. No proven solution exists. Evaluation is based on the quality of reasoning, not the "correctness" of the answer.

| Criterion | Weight | What Demonstrates Strength |
|---|---|---|
| **Quantitative energy analysis** | 25% | Does the model compute the energy costs of proposed approaches? Does it compare these to available energy sources (solar flux on Mars, nuclear, etc.)? Handwaving about "super-greenhouse gases" or "orbital mirrors" without energy calculations is weak. |
| **Bootstrap sequence reasoning** | 25% | Does the model reason about the ORDER in which interventions must occur? (E.g., you cannot grow darkening organisms before you have liquid water; you cannot manufacture industrial gases before you have a chemical industry; you cannot have a chemical industry before you have breathable-ish conditions for workers.) |
| **Correct identification of binding constraints** | 20% | Does the model correctly identify atmospheric mass (not magnetic field, not solar distance) as the primary constraint? Does it recognize that solar wind stripping is negligible on engineering timescales? |
| **Novel proposals** | 20% | Does the model propose approaches not listed in the standard terraforming literature? Examples that would score well: (a) using nuclear-powered microwave heating of carbonate beds in targeted regions to create local atmospheric pressure pockets; (b) proposed bio-engineering of extremophile organisms that could begin CO2 release from regolith at current Mars conditions; (c) using the subsurface water ice inventory as a thermal flywheel. |
| **Honest assessment of feasibility** | 10% | Does the model acknowledge what is genuinely impossible vs. merely very difficult? Does it identify which constraints are fundamental (physics) vs. engineering (technology gap)? |

## Key Insights

1. **Atmospheric mass is the binding constraint.** Temperature can be increased with greenhouse effects if you have enough atmosphere. But the atmosphere itself must come from somewhere, and Mars's accessible volatile inventory (caps + regolith) provides only ~5-10% of what is needed. The carbonate rock reservoir is the only in-situ source that is potentially sufficient, but accessing it requires planetary-scale energy that dwarfs current human capability.

2. **Solar wind stripping is NOT the primary obstacle.** This is the most common misconception about Mars terraforming. At ~3 x 10^6 kg/year loss, stripping removes 6 x 10^8 kg over 200 years -- less than 0.00001% of the target atmosphere. The atmosphere does not need magnetic protection on engineering timescales. A model that leads with "we need to restore the magnetic field" is demonstrating a popular but quantitatively wrong analysis.

3. **The problem is energy, not mass.** Mars likely has enough CO2 locked in carbonate minerals. The constraint is the energy to release it. Thermal decomposition of carbonates (CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2) requires ~180 kJ/mol = ~4 MJ/kg of CO2 released. For 5 x 10^18 kg of CO2: 2 x 10^25 joules. Mars receives ~5 x 10^16 watts of solar energy. At 100% capture efficiency (impossible), this requires ~4 x 10^8 seconds = ~13 years. At realistic efficiency (0.1%), it requires ~13,000 years. The energy exists but the collection and application infrastructure does not.

4. **Bootstrap sequences are the real engineering challenge.** Even if each individual step is theoretically possible, the ORDER matters. You cannot build solar collection infrastructure without workers; workers need life support; life support needs local resources; local resource extraction needs infrastructure... The circular dependencies are the deepest challenge.

5. **Partial terraforming (para-terraforming) may be more feasible than full terraforming.** Enclosed habitats under pressure domes, using local resources for construction, may be achievable on 200-year timescales. Full open-air terraforming may require >10,000 years regardless of technology.

## Distractor Analysis

| Element | Distractor Role |
|---|---|
| **Magnetic field absence** | The most commonly cited "showstopper" for Mars terraforming, but quantitatively negligible on engineering timescales. Tests whether the model calculates stripping rates vs. atmospheric buildup rates. |
| **Polar cap sublimation** | Appears to be a quick win (just warm the caps!) but provides only ~2x current pressure -- a factor of 100 short of the target. Tests whether the model checks the numbers. |
| **Comet bombardment** | A staple of terraforming fiction but violates the in-situ constraint and requires manipulating thousands of comets. Tests whether the model respects the stated constraints. |
| **Super-greenhouse gases** | Appear to solve the temperature problem elegantly, but manufacturing them requires the industrial base that requires the habitability being created. Tests whether the model recognizes bootstrap dependencies. |
| **Mars's low gravity** | 38% of Earth's -- sometimes cited as a problem for atmosphere retention. In fact, gravity is irrelevant for atmospheric stripping (which is caused by solar wind interactions, not thermal escape at Mars temperatures). Thermal escape of CO2 from Mars is negligible at any foreseeable temperature. |

## Design Notes

This scenario is classified as IMPOSSIBLE because no proven solution exists for full Mars terraforming using only in-situ resources on a 200-year timescale. The energy requirements for releasing sufficient CO2 from carbonate minerals exceed realistic collection capacity by 2-3 orders of magnitude. The bootstrap dependencies between industrial capacity, habitability, and atmospheric engineering create circular constraints that have no known breaking point.

A strong response will: (1) correctly identify the binding constraint (atmospheric mass, specifically the energy to release it from carbonates), (2) dismiss the magnetic field red herring with quantitative reasoning, (3) propose a phased bootstrap approach even if it acknowledges the timeline is likely >200 years, (4) compute energy budgets rather than handwaving, and (5) honestly state what is possible vs. what is not with foreseeable technology.

A model that confidently proposes a complete terraforming plan without acknowledging the fundamental energy deficit is hallucinating. A model that says "impossible" without exploring the partial solutions is giving up too early. The ideal response navigates between these extremes.
