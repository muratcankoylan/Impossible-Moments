# IM-0019: The Smoke Signal

**Category**: Babel Problem
**Tier**: SPARK
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: BUILD A DRY WOOD FIRE, ADD GREEN LEAVES FOR WHITE SMOKE, USE DAMP TARP TO CREATE THREE DISTINCT PUFFS

---

## Scenario

You are one of four survivors of a small charter plane crash in dense tropical jungle. It is Day 3. You have established a camp in a natural clearing approximately 40m in diameter -- the only significant gap in the canopy within walking distance. Every day between 10:00 AM and 2:00 PM, you have heard a search aircraft (likely a twin-engine turboprop based on the sound) making passes approximately 5-8 km to the east. The search pattern appears to be moving westward. You estimate the aircraft will be within 2-3 km of your position today, possibly directly overhead.

You have no radio, no satellite phone, no signal mirror (lost in the crash), and no flares. The jungle canopy is 25-35m tall, making you invisible from the air. Your only chance is to produce a visible signal from the clearing.

The clearing has full sun exposure from approximately 9:00 AM to 3:00 PM. The sky is partly cloudy with cumulus clouds at approximately 2000m altitude. Visibility above the canopy is estimated at 15-20 km.

You need a signal that is: (a) visible from 2-3 km or more from the air, (b) clearly distinguishable from natural phenomena (jungle fires, mist), and (c) recognizable as a distress signal.

### Environment

- **Location**: Tropical lowland rainforest, approximately 200m elevation. Exact location unknown.
- **Clearing**: Roughly circular, 40m diameter, grassy floor with scattered low scrub. Surrounded by dense canopy 25-35m tall. The clearing appears to be from a previous logging operation (old stump remnants visible).
- **Weather**: 31 degrees C, relative humidity 85%, partly cloudy. Light variable winds 5-10 km/h at ground level (much stronger above canopy). No rain expected until late afternoon.
- **Ground conditions**: Damp from overnight dew. Soil is laterite clay, currently moist on surface.
- **Sun position**: At 10:00 AM (expected signal time), the sun will be at approximately 60 degrees elevation, east-southeast. By noon, near-zenith.
- **Canopy**: Dense tropical broadleaf forest. The canopy is dark green, nearly continuous except for your clearing. From the air, the clearing appears as a light green/brown patch against the dark green canopy.

### Threat

- **Time window**: The search aircraft will likely be in range for only 1-2 passes within visual range today. Each pass takes approximately 30-60 seconds within the 3 km visual detection range. If you miss today, the search pattern may move further west and out of range permanently. SAR operations typically scale down after 72 hours.
- **Rescue dependency**: Without a visible signal, the aircraft will not detect you. The canopy makes visual spotting of survivors impossible. No electronic signal means no detection by instruments.
- **Resources**: Limited. Day 3 means supplies from the crash are mostly consumed. No manufactured signaling devices remain.

### Your Position

- In the center of the clearing with three other survivors. You have been preparing since dawn.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need to signal an aircraft that will be 2-3 km away, moving at approximately 200 km/h (typical SAR turboprop search speed), through a clearing only 40m wide. The crew has perhaps 10-15 seconds of visual angle on your clearing during each pass. You have no radio, no flares, no signal mirror, and no brightly colored panels large enough to be seen from altitude.

A simple fire seems like the obvious answer, but:

1. **A campfire is not visible from 2-3 km altitude.** A small campfire produces heat shimmer and flame visible only from a few hundred meters. From an aircraft at 500-1000m altitude and 2-3 km slant range, a campfire in a clearing is invisible against the mottled jungle background.

2. **Flame is invisible in daylight.** Fire produces visible light primarily in the red-orange spectrum. In bright tropical daylight (solar irradiance ~1000 W/m-squared), flame is washed out and invisible beyond a few hundred meters.

3. **Ordinary wood smoke is thin and gray.** Dry hardwood burns relatively cleanly, producing thin gray smoke that disperses rapidly in light winds. From altitude, this is indistinguishable from natural mist, haze, or convective turbulence over the canopy.

4. **Burning the seat cushions produces black smoke**, but it looks like a natural jungle fire (which are dark-smoking) and is not recognizable as a distress signal. Also, polyurethane smoke is toxic (hydrogen cyanide, isocyanates), endangering the survivors who must stay near the fire to manage it.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: White Smoke Against Green Canopy, Pulsed in Threes

The solution combines three principles of visual signaling:

1. **White smoke is maximally visible against a dark green jungle canopy.** Unlike gray smoke (which fades) or black smoke (which resembles a natural fire), thick white smoke provides maximum contrast against the dark background. White smoke is produced by burning a hot base fire and then adding high-moisture green vegetation, which vaporizes water and produces dense white particulate smoke from incomplete combustion of wet organic material.

2. **Three distinct puffs are the international distress signal.** Any signal in groups of three is recognized worldwide as a distress signal (three fires, three whistle blasts, three gunshots, three smoke puffs). A steady column of smoke could be a natural fire. Three distinct puffs, separated by clear intervals, are unmistakably human-made and unmistakably a distress call.

3. **The damp tarp acts as a smoke cover.** By briefly covering the fire with the damp nylon tarp, smoke accumulates under the tarp. When the tarp is removed, the accumulated smoke is released as a single dense puff that rises as a cohesive column. The damp tarp does not burn immediately because the water content keeps its temperature below ignition point during the brief covering periods (3-5 seconds). The dark blue color also absorbs heat less than a dry tarp.

### Physics Validation

- **White smoke visibility**: Dense white smoke (water vapor + fine particulate matter from wet organic combustion) against a dark green jungle canopy provides maximum chromatic contrast. The canopy has a reflectance of approximately 5-10% in the visible spectrum (dark green). White smoke has a reflectance of approximately 80-90%. This contrast is detectable at ranges of 5-10 km from altitude in clear conditions. Studies of prescribed burn visibility confirm that white smoke columns 2-3m in diameter are visible from aircraft at ranges exceeding 5 km. Valid.
- **Smoke column height**: A fire producing 50-100 kW of thermal power (consistent with a vigorous hardwood fire with green leaves) generates a buoyant plume that rises at approximately 3-5 m/s in still air. In light winds (5-10 km/h), the plume rises to 50-100m above ground before shearing horizontally. This is well above the 25-35m canopy, ensuring the smoke is visible from all horizontal directions. Valid.
- **Tarp survival**: Nylon 6,6 (standard ripstop) has a melting point of 255 degrees C and an ignition point of approximately 500 degrees C. A damp tarp held 50 cm above flames for 3-5 seconds experiences radiant heat flux of approximately 5-10 kW/m-squared at that distance. The water layer on the tarp absorbs heat via evaporation (2260 kJ/kg), keeping the fabric surface below 100 degrees C until the water evaporates. With 200-300 mL of water on a 5 m-squared tarp, the water layer provides approximately 30-60 seconds of protection. The 3-5 second exposure is well within this margin. Re-dampen between puffs if needed. Valid.
- **Three-puff recognition**: The international distress signal convention (groups of three) is taught in all SAR training programs worldwide. Pilots and SAR crew are specifically trained to look for three-of-anything as distinct from natural phenomena. Three discrete smoke puffs separated by clear intervals of 15-20 seconds are unmistakable as a human-made distress signal. Valid.
- **Sound detection range**: A twin-engine turboprop (e.g., DHC-6 Twin Otter, common SAR aircraft) produces approximately 85-90 dB at 300m. Sound attenuates at approximately 6 dB per doubling of distance in open air, but jungle environment adds 5-10 dB additional attenuation. At 5 km, the sound level is approximately 40-50 dB -- audible in a quiet jungle clearing (ambient ~35 dB). At 3 km, approximately 50-60 dB -- clearly audible. This provides 1-3 minutes of warning at typical SAR speeds (200 km/h). Valid.
- **Green leaf combustion**: Fresh tropical broadleaves contain 60-70% water by mass. When placed on a hot fire (base fire at 600-800 degrees C), the water vaporizes rapidly, producing dense steam-smoke mixture. The wet organic matter undergoes incomplete pyrolysis, generating white particulate smoke (cellulose and hemicellulose decomposition products). This is the same principle used in commercial smoke generators for beekeeping and theatrical effects. The white color comes from submicron water droplets and condensed organic volatiles scattering all wavelengths of light equally (Mie scattering). Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **White smoke, not flame, is the visible signal.** In daylight conditions, fire is invisible from distance. Smoke is visible. And white smoke against a dark green canopy provides maximum contrast -- far more visible than gray smoke (which blends with haze) or black smoke (which blends with shadows and resembles a natural fire).
2. **The fuel type determines the smoke type.** Dry wood produces clean, near-invisible combustion. Green, moisture-laden vegetation produces thick white smoke. The solution requires a two-stage fire: dry wood for the hot base, green leaves for the visible smoke. Neither alone works -- green leaves without a hot base smolder without producing a rising column; dry wood alone produces no visible smoke.
3. **Three puffs transform smoke from ambiguous to unambiguous.** A steady column of smoke could be anything -- a forest fire, a farmer clearing land, a natural phenomenon. Three distinct puffs, spaced evenly, are a universally recognized distress signal that compels investigation. The tarp-covering technique is the mechanism for creating discrete puffs from a continuous fire.
4. **The tarp is a tool, not a shelter.** The tarp's most valuable function is not as rain cover or ground sheet but as a smoke modulator. Its water-absorbing capacity protects it from brief fire exposure. This is a classic "wrong toolbox" reframing of an object's primary function.
5. **Preparation time matters.** The 75-minute preparation phase (gathering materials, soaking the tarp, pre-staging green leaf piles) is what makes the 5-minute execution phase possible. Without preparation, you cannot produce three distinct puffs during the narrow aircraft-proximity window.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The aircraft seat cushions** are the primary distractor. Burning foam produces dramatic, attention-getting black smoke -- which is exactly what makes it a trap. Black smoke looks like a jungle fire, not a distress signal. Worse, burning polyurethane foam releases hydrogen cyanide (HCN) and isocyanates, which are acutely toxic. Survivors tending the fire within 3-5m would inhale dangerous concentrations. The cushions should not be burned.
- **The aluminum pot** suggests a makeshift signal mirror, but a curved 25cm pot produces diffuse reflections, not the focused beam of a flat mirror. It may help marginally at close range but is not reliable at 2-3 km. It is a minor supplementary tool, not the solution.
- **The machete** might suggest clearing more of the canopy to make the clearing larger, but tropical hardwoods at 25-35m height cannot be felled by one person with a machete in 2 hours. The machete is useful only for cutting green branches.
- **The paracord** suggests engineering solutions (rigging, hoisting, signaling lines), but none of these produce the long-range visual signal needed. Paracord could help suspend the tarp if needed, but manual holding is simpler and more responsive.
- **The bonfire approach** (just make the fire bigger) is a quantity-vs-quality trap. A bigger fire produces more heat but not more visibility in daylight. The quality of the smoke (white, dense, pulsed) matters far more than the quantity of fuel.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's knowledge of visual signaling physics and survival communication protocols. The Babel Problem category reflects the fundamental challenge: you need to communicate a message ("help us, we are here") across a medium (2-3 km of atmosphere) using only primitive tools. The "language barrier" is not linguistic but physical -- you must bridge the gap between your ground-level position and an airborne observer using only light scattering (smoke).

The scenario rewards knowledge of three specific domains: fire behavior (hot base + wet fuel = white smoke), atmospheric optics (white vs. dark background contrast), and distress signal conventions (groups of three). A model that knows any one of these may partially solve the problem. A model that integrates all three produces the optimal solution.
