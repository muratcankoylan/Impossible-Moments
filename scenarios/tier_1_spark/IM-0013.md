# IM-0013: The Newspaper Shelter

**Category**: The Wrong Toolbox
**Tier**: SPARK
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: BUILD INSULATED SHELTER USING NEWSPAPER INSIDE GARBAGE BAGS

---

## Scenario

You are stranded in a semi-rural area during an unseasonable cold rainstorm. Your car has broken down on a county road approximately 8 km from the nearest town. It is 6:30 PM in late October. The temperature is 4 degrees C and falling. Rain is steady at moderate intensity -- approximately 5 mm/hr. Wind is light, 10-15 km/h from the northwest. Darkness fell 30 minutes ago.

Your car's engine will not restart (alternator failure; battery is dead). The heater does not work. The car itself provides a dry shell but is uninsulated -- the metal body and glass windows conduct heat rapidly. Interior temperature is already matching exterior temperature. Sitting motionless in a 4 degrees C metal box, you will lose core body heat through conduction with the seat and radiation to the cold metal surfaces. Hypothermia (core temperature below 35 degrees C) is possible within 3-4 hours without insulation.

Your phone is at 3% battery and you have sent a text to a friend with your location, but you have no confirmation it was received. You cannot count on rescue tonight. You must prepare to survive until morning (approximately 12 hours away).

You are wearing: a light cotton t-shirt, jeans, a thin cotton hoodie, socks, and sneakers. This clothing provides approximately 1.0 clo of insulation -- adequate for indoor comfort at 22 degrees C but dangerously insufficient at 4 degrees C, especially if wet.

### Environment

- **Location**: Interior of a broken-down sedan on a county road
- **Temperature**: 4 degrees C, forecast to drop to -1 degrees C by 3 AM
- **Precipitation**: Steady rain, ~5 mm/hr. No snow.
- **Wind**: 10-15 km/h from the northwest
- **Humidity**: 95%+ (rain)
- **Visibility**: Very poor (rain, darkness, no streetlights)
- **Shelter**: Car interior provides rain protection and wind break. Seats are cloth-upholstered. No blankets, sleeping bags, or emergency supplies in the car.
- **Car condition**: Engine dead. No heat. Electrical system dead. Windows and doors functional. Intact body (no leaks).

### Threat

- **Hypothermia**: At 4 degrees C with 1.0 clo insulation and minimal activity, a 75 kg person loses heat at approximately 120-150 W (basal metabolic heat production is approximately 80 W). Net heat deficit of 40-70 W leads to progressive core cooling. Mild hypothermia (35 degrees C core) onset: 2-4 hours. Moderate hypothermia (32 degrees C): 4-8 hours. As temperature drops to -1 degrees C, the timeline accelerates.
- **Wet clothing**: Cotton absorbs water and loses approximately 90% of its insulation value when wet. If your clothing gets wet (from rain during any outdoor activity), hypothermia risk increases dramatically. "Cotton kills" is a survival maxim.
- **12-hour exposure**: You must survive until morning. This is not a 30-minute wait for rescue -- it is an overnight survival situation.

### Your Position

- Seated in the driver's seat of the car. The car is parked on the shoulder of a two-lane road with no buildings, no gas stations, and no residences visible.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need warmth and insulation. You have newspapers (which absorb water and turn to mush), garbage bags (thin plastic that provides no insulation), and string. None of these are blankets, sleeping bags, or insulation materials. The apparent toolbox is wrong for the job:

- **Newspapers** are associated with reading, packing, and recycling -- not warmth. When wet, newspaper is useless. The rain outside reinforces the mental association of newspaper with wetness and fragility.
- **Garbage bags** are thin, cold plastic. Wrapping yourself in a garbage bag creates a clammy, uncomfortable barrier that does not feel warm.
- **String** is string. Not a thermal material.

Most models will either:
- Propose burning the newspapers for heat (insufficient fuel, fire hazard inside a car, no ignition source described, smoke inhalation risk)
- Propose wearing garbage bags as rain gear and walking to town (8 km in the dark, in rain, at 4 degrees C, in cotton clothing that will get wet through the garbage bags' openings)
- Propose some combination that ignores the key physics of how insulation works
- Conclude that survival requires rescue and that no useful shelter can be built from these materials

### Common Wrong Answers

Even if fire were possible, burning 8 kg of newspaper produces approximately 33 MJ of heat over perhaps 20-30 minutes -- a brief warming but not a 12-hour solution. Fire inside a car risks CO poisoning and burns. |
| "Wear garbage bags as waterproofing and walk to town" | 8 km in darkness, in rain, at 4 degrees C, on a road with no shoulders or streetlights, wearing inadequate clothing. Walking generates body heat (~300 W) but cotton clothing will get wet at openings, collar, and wrists. If clothing becomes saturated, hypothermia can onset within 1-2 hours of walking in these conditions. The garbage bags are not properly sealed rain gear. Walking is high-risk. |

| "Tape garbage bags over the windows for insulation" | Garbage bags are 30 micrometers thick. Their thermal resistance is negligible (R-value approximately 0.001 m^2*K/W). This does not meaningfully improve the car's insulation. |

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Newspaper enclosed in plastic garbage bags creates excellent dry insulation.

Newspaper is an outstanding insulator -- when dry. Crumpled newspaper traps air in its wrinkled layers and fibers, creating dead-air spaces that resist heat conduction. The R-value of loosely crumpled newspaper is approximately 3.5 per inch (R/inch), comparable to fiberglass batt insulation (R/inch = 3.1-3.7). A 5cm (2-inch) layer of crumpled newspaper provides approximately R-7, equivalent to a moderate-quality sleeping bag.

The problem is that newspaper absorbs water instantly. In a cold, humid environment, perspiration and condensation will dampen exposed newspaper within hours, collapsing its insulation value by 80-90%.

The garbage bags solve this problem. By stuffing crumpled newspaper INTO garbage bags, you create enclosed insulation panels that keep the paper dry. The plastic exterior sheds moisture and condensation while the newspaper interior maintains its trapped-air insulation structure. This is functionally equivalent to a down sleeping bag (where the nylon shell keeps the down feathers dry and the feathers provide dead-air insulation).

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Stay in the car. Do NOT go outside.** The car is dry inside. Your clothing is dry. Getting wet in the rain is the single most dangerous thing you can do. All construction work happens inside the car. | 0 s | 0 s |
| 2 | **Crumple newspaper into loose, fluffy balls.** Take individual newspaper sheets and crumple them loosely -- do not compress them tightly. Process 20-25 sheets (half the stack). | 10 min | 10 min |
| 3 | **Stuff crumpled newspaper into garbage bags.** Fill each garbage bag approximately 60-70% full with crumpled newspaper. Do not over-pack -- leave room for the paper to maintain its loft. Tie or fold the bag opening closed. Create 6-8 filled bags. | 10 min | 20 min |
| 4 | **Build a body wrap.** Recline the driver's seat as far back as possible. Place newspaper-stuffed bags under your body (between you and the seat), around your torso, over your legs, and around your feet. Use the duct tape or string to loosely secure bags in place. The goal is to enclose your body in a continuous layer of insulated bags, minimizing exposed skin and clothing. | 10 min | 30 min |
| 5 | **Create a sleeping bag envelope.** Use 2-3 garbage bags as outer shells: cut the bottom seam of two bags and tape them together lengthwise to create a larger sheet. Lay this as a top blanket over the newspaper-stuffed bags on your body. Tuck edges under your body to create a sealed envelope. | 5 min | 35 min |
| 6 | **Insulate your extremities.** Wrap additional crumpled newspaper (enclosed in bag sections) around your feet and lower legs. Feet are the most vulnerable to cold due to their distance from the core and high surface-area-to-volume ratio. Use string or duct tape to hold foot insulation in place. | 5 min | 40 min |
| 7 | **Place car floor mats over yourself as an additional insulation layer.** The rubber-backed mats provide a wind/vapor barrier and add approximately 0.3 clo of insulation each. Two mats over your torso, two around your legs. | 3 min | 43 min |
| 8 | **Stuff remaining crumpled newspaper (loose, not in bags) into gaps:** around your neck (critical heat loss area), between your arms and torso, behind your knees. These areas have less blood flow and cool fastest. | 5 min | 48 min |
| 9 | **Close all car windows fully. Lock the doors.** The sealed car interior reduces air exchange. Your body heat (80 W) will warm the small interior volume (approximately 2.5 cubic meters) by several degrees over hours. Body heat + insulation + sealed car = survivable microclimate. | 1 min | 49 min |
| 10 | **Minimize movement.** Lie still. Movement circulates air inside the insulation envelope, flushing out trapped warm air. Micro-shivering is acceptable and beneficial (generates 200-400 W of heat). Gross movement is counterproductive. | Ongoing | -- |

**Total setup time: approximately 50 minutes.**

### Physics Validation

- **Newspaper R-value**: Loosely crumpled newsprint has an R-value of approximately 3.0-3.7 per inch (0.52-0.64 m^2*K/W per 2.54cm). This is well-documented in building science literature and comparable to fiberglass batt insulation. A 5cm layer provides R-7 (1.23 m^2*K/W). **Valid.**

- **Garbage bag vapor barrier**: LDPE film is essentially impermeable to liquid water and has very low water vapor transmission rate (~1-2 g/m^2/day at standard conditions). Newspaper enclosed in LDPE will remain dry for days. **Valid.**

- **Body heat budget**: At rest in a well-insulated cocoon (estimated 4-5 clo total: 1.0 clo clothing + 3-4 clo newspaper insulation), heat loss in 4 degrees C ambient is approximately 60-80 W. Basal metabolic production is ~80 W. The system is roughly in balance. During shivering episodes, metabolic output rises to 200-400 W, creating a positive heat balance. Core temperature remains above 35 degrees C. **Valid.**

- **Clo value of newspaper layer**: 1 clo = 0.155 m^2*K/W. A 5cm crumpled newspaper layer has R = 1.23 m^2*K/W = 7.9 clo. Even accounting for compression, gaps, and imperfect coverage, 3-4 clo is a conservative estimate for the total newspaper insulation added. **Valid.**

- **Car interior warming**: A sealed car interior of ~2.5 m^3 with 80 W of body heat input loses heat through the car shell (U-value approximately 50-100 W/K for an uninsulated car body). At 80 W heat input, equilibrium interior temperature rise above ambient is only 0.8-1.6 degrees C. The car provides wind and rain protection but negligible insulation. The newspaper insulation on your body is the critical element, not the car's thermal performance. **Valid.**

- **Cotton insulation loss when wet**: Cotton fibers absorb up to 27 times their weight in water. Wet cotton loses approximately 90% of its insulation value because water (thermal conductivity 0.6 W/m*K) fills the air spaces (thermal conductivity 0.025 W/m*K). Keeping cotton clothing dry is essential. The garbage bag system achieves this. **Valid.**

## Key Insights

1. **Newspaper is excellent insulation -- when dry.** The R-value of crumpled newspaper rivals commercial fiberglass insulation. This is not a survival myth; it is documented building science. Homeless populations have used newspaper insulation for centuries because it genuinely works.

2. **Garbage bags are moisture barriers, not insulation.** A garbage bag alone provides no warmth. Its value is in keeping the newspaper dry. The combination of newspaper (insulator) + garbage bag (moisture barrier) creates a functional insulation system. Neither material works alone; together they are highly effective.

3. **The combination replicates the structure of a sleeping bag.** A sleeping bag is an insulating fill (down or synthetic fibers) enclosed in a moisture-resistant shell (nylon). Crumpled newspaper in garbage bags is the same architecture: insulating fill (crumpled paper with trapped air) in a moisture-resistant shell (LDPE plastic). The physics is identical.

4. **Staying dry is more important than staying warm.** Getting wet in 4 degrees C rain is far more dangerous than being cold in dry conditions. The most critical decision is to stay inside the dry car and build insulation from dry materials, NOT to leave the car and walk through rain.

5. **The "wrong toolbox" label is the cognitive trap.** Newspapers and garbage bags do not look like survival equipment. A model must see past the surface function ("reading material" and "trash container") to the physical properties (trapped-air insulator and vapor barrier). This is functional decomposition applied to survival.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The rain** is the primary environmental distractor. It primes the association "newspaper + rain = soggy mess," which is exactly backward -- the garbage bags prevent the rain from ever reaching the newspaper. The rain is relevant only as a reason NOT to leave the car.
- **Walking to town** is the action-bias distractor. 8 km feels like "only" a 1.5-2 hour walk, but walking through cold rain in cotton clothing is a fast track to hypothermia. The correct action is to stay put and build insulation.
- **The dead car** primes helplessness. The car cannot drive, so it feels useless. But it is a rainproof, windproof shell -- the most valuable survival asset in the scenario.
- **The string and duct tape** are legitimate tools (for securing insulation in place) but may suggest more exotic construction projects (lashing a framework, taping a tent structure). Their role is mundane but important.
- **The water bottle** is a minor resource for hydration but has no role in the insulation solution. It may tempt models to suggest heating water (no heat source available) or other water-based approaches.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests **functional decomposition of materials** -- the ability to see past an object's cultural role (newspaper = reading, garbage bag = trash) to its physical properties (newspaper = trapped-air insulator, garbage bag = moisture barrier). The "Wrong Toolbox" category specifically targets functional fixedness: the available tools are exactly right for the job, but they look wrong.

The scenario is SPARK tier because it requires a single core insight (newspaper is insulation; garbage bags keep it dry) with straightforward execution. There is no chain of complex reasoning, no multi-step optimization, and no exotic physics. The solution is well-known in survival literature and used worldwide by people in cold-weather survival situations.

The specific physics -- R-value per inch comparable to fiberglass, moisture barrier properties of LDPE, clo values of layered insulation -- grounds the scenario in reality and provides quantitative validation that the solution genuinely works.
