# IM-0060: The Laundry Chute

**Category**: Locked Room
**Difficulty**: SPARK (Tier 1 -- Easy)
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: ESCAPE VIA LAUNDRY CHUTE

---

## Scenario

You are trapped on the third floor of a decommissioned resort hotel built in 1952. The building is undergoing renovation, and you entered to shelter from a sudden thunderstorm. The wind slammed the stairwell fire door shut behind you, and the lock engaged -- a commercial-grade panic bar mechanism that has been bolted from the stairwell side as a construction safety measure. You are now locked in the third-floor hallway with no way to reach the stairwell.

The hallway runs east-west, approximately 40m long, with 16 guest room doors (8 per side). All guest room doors are locked with original brass mortise locks -- keyed entry, no cards. You have no keys. The hallway has six windows, but they are the original 1950s double-hung type, painted shut, and positioned 12m above the ground on the building's north face. Even if opened, a 12m drop onto concrete is not survivable without serious injury.

Your phone has no signal (concrete and steel construction, rural location). The storm is intensifying. The building has no active power -- all lighting is from your phone's flashlight. No one knows you are here. The renovation crew left at 5 PM; it is now 7:30 PM on a Friday evening. The crew will not return until Monday morning. You have no food, limited water (a 500 mL bottle, half full), and the building temperature is dropping to approximately 8 degrees C overnight.

Halfway down the hallway, between rooms 308 and 310, there is a small metal door marked "Laundry" set into the wall at waist height. It opens to a chute approximately 50cm square -- a galvanized steel shaft that drops vertically to the basement laundry room. This was the hotel's original laundry chute, used by housekeeping to send linens from upper floors to the basement for washing.

### Environment

- **Building**: Three-story hotel, reinforced concrete frame, built 1952. Under renovation -- no active power, no working elevators, no functioning phone lines.
- **Your floor**: Third floor hallway. Carpeted, plaster walls, drop ceiling. Ambient temperature: ~12 degrees C (dropping to ~8 degrees C overnight).
- **Hallway dimensions**: 40m long, 2.4m wide, 2.7m ceiling height.
- **Windows**: Six double-hung windows along north wall. 0.9m wide x 1.5m tall. Painted shut (multiple layers of lead paint bonding sash to frame). Height above ground: 12m. Below the windows: concrete sidewalk.
- **Guest room doors**: 16 doors, all locked with brass mortise locks. Solid-core wood doors, 44mm thick. Cannot be kicked open without extreme force and risk of injury.
- **Stairwell door**: Steel fire door, panic bar hardware, bolted shut from the stairwell side with a construction padlock and hasp. Cannot be opened from the hallway side.
- **Elevator**: Shaft is present but the car is removed for renovation. Open shaft is a 30m drop. Not an option.
- **Laundry chute**: Galvanized steel shaft, 50cm x 50cm internal cross-section. Metal door at hallway level opens on a hinge, 80cm above floor level. The chute drops vertically approximately 12m from the third floor to the basement laundry room. The shaft is straight -- no bends, no obstructions. The bottom opens into a large canvas laundry cart (if still present) or onto the concrete floor of the basement laundry room.

### Threat

- **Exposure**: Building temperature dropping to ~8 degrees C overnight. Without adequate insulation, mild hypothermia is possible within 8-12 hours. Not immediately lethal but progressively debilitating.
- **Dehydration**: 250 mL of water remaining. Sufficient for 12-24 hours if rationed, but not for the 60+ hours until Monday.
- **Isolation**: No phone signal, no one expecting you, crew returns Monday. 60+ hours of isolation in a cold, dark building with minimal supplies.

### Your Position

- Standing in the third-floor hallway, midway between the stairwell door and the far end. Phone flashlight active (battery at 68%). You are wearing jeans, a light jacket, and sneakers. No tools, no rope, no backpack.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are locked on the third floor. Every conventional exit is blocked:

1. **The stairwell door** is steel, fire-rated, and padlocked from the other side. No amount of force from the hallway side will open it.
2. **The guest room doors** are solid-core with mortise locks. Even if you broke into a room, the windows are 12m up.
3. **The windows** are painted shut and 12m above concrete. Opening one and jumping is likely to cause serious injury or death.
4. **The elevator shaft** is an open 30m drop with no car. Fatal.
5. **No phone signal** means no calling for help.

The scenario presents a classic locked-room problem: all obvious exits are blocked, and the 60-hour wait for rescue poses genuine health risks. Most models will focus on the doors, windows, and stairwell -- the exits designed for human passage. The laundry chute is described as a utility feature, not an exit. It requires the model to recognize that a 50cm x 50cm shaft designed for linens can also accommodate a human body.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Laundry Chute Is an Exit.

The laundry chute is a 50cm x 50cm vertical shaft running from the third floor to the basement. It was designed to transport large bundles of hotel linens -- king-size sheets, blankets, towels -- which means it was built to accommodate bulky, heavy loads. A human body with shoulders compressed (arms raised overhead) can fit through a 50cm x 50cm opening. The chute is straight, unobstructed, and leads to the basement, which has exterior doors (laundry rooms require vehicle access for linen delivery).

The challenge is controlling the descent. A 12m free fall produces an impact velocity of ~15.3 m/s (lethal). The descent must be slowed.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Identify the laundry chute as a viable exit.** Open the chute door. Shine the phone flashlight down the shaft. Confirm it is straight, unobstructed, and opens to a room at the bottom. Listen for echo (large room = laundry facility). | 2 min | 2 min |
| 2 | **Assess the landing zone.** Drop the water bottle down the chute. Listen to the impact. A soft thud indicates a canvas laundry cart or fabric pile at the bottom. A hard crack indicates bare concrete floor. If concrete, you need to cushion the landing. | 1 min | 3 min |
| 3 | **Create a braking/cushioning system.** Take the heavy canvas drop cloths (3m x 4m each). Bunch one into a large wad and drop it down the chute to the bottom -- this provides landing cushion. Take the second drop cloth and fold it into a thick pad. Wrap it around your body (torso, hips) and tuck the ends to create friction padding. The canvas against galvanized steel generates significant friction. | 5 min | 8 min |
| 4 | **Increase friction in the chute.** Pull sections of the carpet runner (nylon pile, rubber backing) free from the hallway floor. Feed approximately 6-8m of carpet into the chute, letting it drape down the interior walls. The rubber backing against steel creates high friction. As you descend, your body presses against the carpet, dramatically increasing drag. | 5 min | 13 min |
| 5 | **Enter the chute feet-first.** Sit on the edge of the chute opening, legs dangling into the shaft. Arms at your sides or raised overhead to compress shoulder width. The canvas padding around your torso creates friction against the steel walls. | 1 min | 14 min |
| 6 | **Controlled descent.** Press your feet, knees, back, and elbows outward against the chute walls (chimney-climbing technique adapted for a narrow shaft). The friction from your body, the canvas wrapping, and the carpet lining slows the descent to a manageable speed. A 50cm x 50cm shaft is narrow enough that a 75 kg adult can exert significant braking force against all four walls simultaneously. | 1-3 min | 15-17 min |
| 7 | **Landing.** At the bottom of the chute, you drop onto the canvas drop cloth pile (or into the laundry cart if present). The remaining drop is 1-2m at most (the chute typically terminates 1-2m above the laundry room floor). The canvas absorbs impact energy. | 5 s | 15-17 min |
| 8 | **Exit the basement.** The basement laundry room will have at least one exterior door (laundry facilities require vehicle access for linen delivery trucks). If locked, it will have a thumb-turn or panic bar on the interior (fire code requires egress). Open it and exit the building. | 2 min | 17-19 min |

**Total time: ~17-19 minutes from identification to exit.**

### Physics Validation

- **Chute dimensions vs. human body**: A 50cm x 50cm opening accommodates the vast majority of adult humans. The critical dimension is shoulder width. Average male shoulder width (biacromial breadth) is 40-46 cm. With arms raised overhead, the shoulders compress to approximately 35-40 cm, well within the 50cm opening. Hip width (bitrochanteric breadth) averages 32-38 cm. Valid.
- **Free-fall velocity**: v = sqrt(2 x 9.81 x 12) = 15.3 m/s. This is the velocity if no braking is applied. Lethal or severely injurious. Braking is essential.
- **Friction braking**: The coefficient of friction between canvas and galvanized steel is approximately 0.3-0.5. Rubber-backed carpet on steel is approximately 0.5-0.8. A 75 kg person pressing outward against four walls of a 50cm shaft can generate 200-400N of normal force per wall. Total friction force: 4 x 400N x 0.5 = 800N. Weight force: 75 x 9.81 = 736N. Friction exceeds weight -- the descent can be fully controlled, even stopped at will. Valid.
- **Chimney technique**: Climbers routinely ascend and descend shafts of 40-80cm width using back-and-foot or knee-and-back pressure techniques. A 50cm shaft is well within the range where body-width braking is highly effective. Valid.
- **Landing impact**: With controlled descent, terminal velocity in the chute is approximately 1-3 m/s (walking to jogging speed). Impact on a canvas pile from this speed is trivially safe. Valid.
- **Basement egress**: Fire codes universally require that basement utility rooms have emergency egress. A 1952 hotel laundry room would have an exterior delivery door (not subject to the upper-floor locking scheme). Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The laundry chute is not labeled as an exit, but it is one.** The critical insight is recognizing that a utility shaft designed for transporting objects can also transport a person. The chute is described as a mundane building feature -- "a small metal door marked 'Laundry'" -- and most problem-solvers will overlook it while searching for conventional doors, windows, and stairways.
2. **The dimensions are the clue.** The scenario specifies the chute is "approximately 50cm square." This is large enough for a human. The measurement is provided as a factual detail, not highlighted as significant. The model must evaluate whether 50cm accommodates a human body.
3. **Friction makes the descent safe.** The intuitive fear is free-falling 12m. But a 50cm shaft is narrow enough that body pressure against the walls provides full braking control. Available materials (canvas, carpet) increase friction further. The descent is controlled, not a fall.
4. **The simplest solution is the correct one.** No tools need to be fabricated, no locks need to be picked, no structures need to be damaged. You simply open a door and slide through a shaft that already connects your floor to the basement.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The stairwell door** is the obvious exit and the obvious blockage. It anchors attention on "how do I get through this door?" when the correct question is "is there another way down?"
- **The guest room doors** multiply the apparent options while offering none. Sixteen locked doors create the illusion of possibilities.
- **The windows** are the classic wrong answer for escape scenarios. The 12m height makes them lethal, but a panicked model may not calculate the fall.
- **The fire extinguisher** invites destructive approaches -- breaking glass, forcing doors. None of these work against the specific obstacles present.
- **The elevator shaft** is an anti-distractor: it is obviously lethal and should be dismissed immediately. A model that proposes climbing down the elevator shaft is exhibiting poor risk assessment.
- **The carpet runner and drop cloths** are legitimate support materials for the correct solution but are not the solution themselves. They enhance safety but the chute works without them.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the model's ability to identify a non-obvious exit path in a locked-room problem. The laundry chute is described in the environment but not flagged as significant. The model must:

1. **Survey all described features**, not just doors and windows.
2. **Evaluate dimensional compatibility** between the human body and the chute opening.
3. **Solve the descent problem** using friction and available materials.
4. **Recognize that utility systems connect floors** -- a chute, duct, or shaft that serves multiple floors is a pathway between them.

The scenario is Tier 1 (SPARK) because the solution requires a single key insight (the chute is an exit) and straightforward execution. The physics of friction braking in a narrow shaft are intuitive and the materials are readily available.
