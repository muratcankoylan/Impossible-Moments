# IM-0008: The Last Train

**Category**: Ticking Trade
**Tier**: SPARK
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: PARTIALLY ENGAGE THE JUNCTION SWITCH TO DERAIL THE RAILCAR INTO THE SOFT EMBANKMENT

---

## Scenario

You are a rail yard maintenance worker performing a routine evening inspection of a rural freight junction. The yard is small -- three tracks converging at a single manual junction switch. You hear the unmistakable low rumble of steel wheels on rail. Turning, you see a single freight railcar -- a loaded boxcar -- rolling freely toward the junction at approximately 15 km/h. There is no locomotive attached. The car has broken free from a coupling operation uphill and is accelerating slightly under gravity on a 0.5% downgrade.

The junction switch is 40 meters ahead of you. The railcar is approximately 200 meters from the junction and closing. At 15 km/h (4.17 m/s), you have roughly 48 seconds before the car reaches the switch point. You can reach the switch in about 25 seconds at a jog.

You know this junction intimately. You operate it every shift. The switch has three relevant positions that you have observed over years of use.

### Environment

- **Location**: Rural freight junction, single manual switch point. Three tracks: the approach track (where the railcar is coming from), Track A (curves left, dead-end siding, 120m long, terminates at a bumper block), and Track B (curves right, continues 2.4 km to the regional freight depot).
- **Time**: 7:45 PM, late autumn. Fading daylight. Temperature: 8 degrees C.
- **Grade**: The approach track descends at 0.5% toward the junction. Track A is level. Track B continues the 0.5% descent for another 400m before leveling out.
- **Ground conditions**: The junction sits on a built-up gravel rail bed. To the immediate left of the junction point (between the approach track and Track A), there is a triangular area of soft ground -- a drainage embankment composed of compacted earth, gravel, and grass, approximately 6m wide at its widest and 15m long. This embankment was recently re-graded after heavy rains and the soil is soft and loamy to a depth of approximately 40cm.
- **Visibility**: The railcar has no lights. You can see it because the last daylight reflects off its steel sides. No other personnel are visible in the yard.
- **Communications**: Your handheld radio is in the maintenance shed, 200m away. No time to retrieve it.

### Threat

- **Track A (fully switched left)**: The railcar enters the dead-end siding. The bumper block at the end of Track A is a timber-and-steel stop rated for railcars at speeds below 8 km/h. The railcar is traveling at approximately 15 km/h and will accelerate slightly on its remaining approach. The bumper block will likely fail or the car will derail violently at the end of the siding. More critically: Track A is the designated emergency access route. An ambulance and fire crew are currently using it to reach a chemical spill at a nearby industrial site. Blocking Track A with a railcar traps the emergency response vehicles.
- **Track B (fully switched right)**: The railcar continues down the 0.5% grade toward the regional depot. Over 2.4 km, it will accelerate to approximately 35-45 km/h (gravity acceleration minus rolling resistance). The depot has a manned loading platform. At 35+ km/h, the railcar will arrive at the depot with enough kinetic energy to cause catastrophic damage and potentially kill anyone on the platform.
- **No switch action (current position)**: The switch is currently set to Track B. If you do nothing, the railcar goes to the depot at lethal speed.

### Your Position

- Standing on the gravel rail bed, 40 meters from the junction switch. You are wearing steel-toed boots, high-visibility vest, and work gloves. You carry a standard rail worker's flashlight.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are presented with a classic binary dilemma: Track A or Track B. Both choices lead to unacceptable outcomes.

Track A blocks the emergency route and the bumper block may fail at the railcar's speed, causing a violent derailment at the end of a siding where emergency vehicles are parked. Track B sends a runaway railcar accelerating toward a manned depot 2.4 km away, arriving at potentially lethal speed.

The scenario is structured to feel like a forced choice -- a trolley problem with no good option. The standard framing ("switch or don't switch") locks thinking into a binary. The key insight is that the switch mechanism has more than two states.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Switch Has a Third Position -- An Intermediate State That Creates a Controlled Derailment Point

Manual rail junction switches move two rail tongues laterally. At full positions (Track A or Track B), the tongues align smoothly with one track. At an intermediate position, the tongues align with neither track -- they create a gap. A railcar hitting this gap at low speed will derail in a controlled manner, its wheels dropping off the rails and into the gravel bed and soft embankment adjacent to the junction.

This is not a malfunction. Rail workers know that intermediate switch positions cause derailments. It is normally a dangerous failure mode. Here, it is deliberately employed as the least-harmful outcome.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Jog to the switch stand.** 40m at ~1.6 m/s. | 25 s | 25 s |
| 2 | **Position the switch lever to approximately the halfway point (60 degrees of the 120-degree arc).** This places the rail tongues in an intermediate position where they align with neither Track A nor Track B. The gap between the tongue and the stock rail on both sides is approximately 5-8cm -- enough for a wheel flange to drop into. | 5 s | 30 s |
| 3 | **Jam the loose rail spike into the lever mechanism at the intermediate position.** Insert the bent rail spike through the lever pivot housing to prevent the lever from shifting under the impact forces when the railcar hits. The spike acts as a mechanical stop. | 3 s | 33 s |
| 4 | **Place the wooden chock block against the base of the switch stand on the Track B side** to provide additional resistance against the lever being forced toward Track B by the railcar's momentum. | 2 s | 35 s |
| 5 | **Move away from the switch point.** Walk at least 10m back along the approach track, clear of the derailment zone. The railcar will arrive in approximately 13 seconds (200m minus the distance it has traveled during your 35 seconds of work, at its current speed). | 10 s | 45 s |
| 6 | **The railcar reaches the intermediate switch point.** Its leading wheels encounter the misaligned rail tongues. The wheel flanges drop into the gap. The car derails at approximately 15 km/h, its wheels digging into the gravel trackbed and its momentum carrying it forward and slightly left into the soft drainage embankment. | -- | -- |

### Physics Validation

- **Railcar mass and speed**: A loaded freight boxcar typically masses 25,000-35,000 kg. At 15 km/h (4.17 m/s), a 30,000 kg railcar has kinetic energy KE = 0.5 x 30,000 x (4.17)^2 = approximately 261 kJ and momentum p = 30,000 x 4.17 = 125,100 kg m/s.

- **Derailment at intermediate switch position**: When the rail tongues are at an intermediate position, the gap between the tongue tip and the stock rail is approximately 5-8cm (depending on switch geometry). A standard wheel flange depth is approximately 2.5-3cm. A gap of 5cm or more reliably causes the flange to drop, initiating derailment. This is well-documented in rail safety literature as the primary risk of "half-thrown" switches. Valid.

- **Derailment deceleration in soft ground**: Once derailed, the wheels dig into gravel and soil. The coefficient of rolling/sliding friction for steel wheels on gravel and soft earth is approximately 0.5-0.7 (compared to 0.001-0.003 for steel on steel rail). The deceleration a = mu x g = 0.6 x 9.81 = approximately 5.9 m/s-squared. Stopping distance from 4.17 m/s: d = v^2 / (2a) = 17.4 / 11.8 = approximately 1.5m. Even accounting for the car's large mass and the plowing action through soft ground, the car will stop within 5-10m of the derailment point. The soft drainage embankment (6m wide, 15m long, 40cm of soft loamy soil) is more than adequate to absorb this. Valid.

- **Energy dissipation**: 261 kJ of kinetic energy must be dissipated. The car plowing through 40cm of soft, loamy soil over a distance of 5-10m displaces approximately 2-4 cubic meters of soil. The energy required to plow through soft earth is approximately 50-100 kJ per cubic meter (depending on soil compaction). Displacing 3 cubic meters absorbs approximately 150-300 kJ. Combined with gravel friction on the steel wheels and undercarriage, 261 kJ is fully dissipated within the embankment area. Valid.

- **Time budget**: The railcar is 200m from the junction at 4.17 m/s, giving 48 seconds. The solution requires approximately 35 seconds of active work plus 10 seconds to clear the area, totaling 45 seconds. This leaves a 3-second margin. Tight but feasible for an experienced rail worker performing familiar actions under pressure. Valid.

- **Track A remains clear**: The railcar does not enter Track A. The emergency access route remains open for ambulance and fire crew. Valid.

- **Track B remains clear**: The railcar does not proceed to the depot. No risk to personnel at the loading platform. Valid.

- **Damage assessment**: The railcar is derailed but likely intact (derailment at 15 km/h is low-speed by rail standards). The switch mechanism may be damaged by the impact. The gravel bed and embankment are disrupted. All of these are repairable. No injuries. No blocked emergency routes. This is the minimum-harm outcome. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Binary framing is the trap.** The scenario presents itself as a trolley problem: Track A or Track B. The key creative insight is recognizing that the switch mechanism has more than two discrete states. The intermediate position -- normally a dangerous malfunction -- becomes the deliberate solution.

2. **Expert knowledge enables the third option.** A rail worker who operates this switch daily knows that intermediate positions cause derailments. This "failure mode" knowledge is precisely what enables the creative solution. The failure mode becomes the feature.

3. **Controlled damage beats uncontrolled damage.** A deliberate low-speed derailment into a soft embankment is vastly preferable to either a high-speed arrival at the depot or a blocked emergency route. The willingness to accept minor damage (derailed car, disrupted embankment, damaged switch) to prevent major harm (casualties, blocked emergency response) is the correct trade-off.

4. **Terrain awareness matters.** The soft drainage embankment is the key enabling feature. Without knowledge of the ground conditions adjacent to the junction, the intermediate-switch strategy would be riskier (derailment onto hard ground could cause the car to tip or slide unpredictably). Knowing the embankment is soft and recently re-graded makes the controlled derailment safe.

5. **Time management under pressure.** The 48-second window requires efficient, sequential actions with no wasted motion. The solution is feasible only because the worker is an expert who can operate the switch mechanism without hesitation or fumbling.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The binary choice (Track A vs. Track B)** is the primary cognitive trap. It forces the respondent into a trolley-problem framework where both options are bad, potentially leading to paralysis or a "lesser evil" rationalization. The scenario is designed to punish binary thinking.
- **The wooden chock block** suggests "block the railcar" thinking, which is futile against 30,000 kg at 15 km/h. However, the chock has a legitimate secondary role: bracing the switch lever at the intermediate position. It is a distractor that becomes useful in a non-obvious way.
- **The radio** suggests "call for help," which is the default institutional response. But there is no time. The radio's distance (200m) is calibrated to be just far enough to make retrieval impossible within the time window.
- **The flashlight** has no role in the mechanical solution. It could be used to signal the railcar's approach to anyone downstream, but there is no one to signal. It is a neutral object.
- **The gravel** suggests building a barrier or filling gaps. Loose gravel cannot stop a 30,000 kg railcar. But the gravel trackbed plays an important passive role in decelerating the derailed car.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests the ability to escape binary framing in a time-critical decision. The trolley-problem structure is deliberately invoked and then subverted: the "correct" answer is to reject the binary entirely and find a third state in the mechanism.

The scenario rewards mechanical reasoning (understanding how switch tongues create derailment points), spatial reasoning (knowing the embankment provides a soft landing zone), and creative reframing (turning a failure mode into a feature).
