# IM-0049: The Cob Wall

**Category**: Wrong Toolbox
**Tier**: SPARK
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: BUILD A COB WALL FROM CLAY SOIL, SAND, STRAW, AND WATER

---

## Scenario

You are at a rural homestead in southwest England. A severe storm two days ago destroyed the windbreak wall that protected your kitchen garden from prevailing westerly winds. The wall was 1.2m tall and 8m long. Without it, the wind funnels between your house and the barn, accelerating to gusts of 40-50 km/h across the garden. Your seedlings -- representing three months of food -- are being shredded. You need a replacement windbreak wall within the next 48 hours before the next storm system arrives.

You have no vehicle (the access road is washed out from the storm and will not be passable for at least a week). The nearest building supply store is 15 km away, and even if you could reach it, you have no money for materials -- the storm also took down your fence line, and all your cash reserves went to replacement fencing wire that was ordered before the storm.

There is no concrete, no cinder blocks, no lumber, no bricks anywhere on the property. The destroyed wall was dry-stone, built from fieldstone gathered over decades. Those stones are now scattered across a flooded drainage ditch and are not recoverable in the timeframe.

### Environment

- **Location**: Rural Devon, England. Rolling hills, clay-rich soil. Maritime climate: damp, mild (average temperature 12 degrees C), frequent rain.
- **Terrain**: Gently sloping pasture surrounding the homestead. The garden is a 10m x 15m plot on the south side of the house.
- **Wind exposure**: Prevailing westerly winds funnel between the house (north side) and barn (south side, 12m gap). The destroyed wall spanned this gap. Without it, wind accelerates through the gap (Venturi effect) to 40-50 km/h at garden level.
- **Required wall dimensions**: 8m long, minimum 1.2m tall, positioned to block the westerly wind corridor. The wall does not need to support a roof or carry any load other than its own weight and wind resistance.
- **Soil type**: Devon red clay soil. High clay content (30-40%), naturally occurring. Subsoil (below 15cm topsoil) is dense, sticky, red-brown clay. Available in unlimited quantities -- the property sits on a clay deposit.
- **Weather window**: 48 hours of dry, mild weather (12-15 degrees C, light winds) before the next storm system arrives. After the next storm, intermittent rain for a week.

### Threat

- **Crop loss**: Without the windbreak, the garden seedlings (lettuce, chard, beans, peas, brassicas) will be destroyed by wind desiccation and mechanical damage within 2-3 more days of exposure. This represents 3 months of food production.
- **Timeline**: 48 hours until the next storm. The wall must be structurally sound enough to resist 50 km/h wind gusts at that point.

### Your Position

- Standing in the garden, surveying the gap where the wall used to be. You have two days of food, clean water from a well, and basic hand tools.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You need a wall. You have no wall-building materials: no bricks, no blocks, no concrete, no lumber, no stone. The conventional material list for building a wall is completely absent. Every known "wall material" is unavailable.

The available materials -- dirt, sand, straw, and water -- do not register as "building materials" to most modern thinkers. Dirt is what you build ON, not what you build WITH. Straw is animal bedding, not structural material. The mental model of "wall construction" is anchored to industrial materials (concrete, brick, block, wood), and when those are absent, the problem appears unsolvable.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: Cob Is One of the Oldest Building Materials on Earth. You Have All the Ingredients.

Cob (from Old English "cob" meaning a lump or rounded mass) is a building material made from clay-rich subsoil, sand, straw, and water, mixed together and built up in courses without formwork. Devon, England is the historic heartland of cob construction -- thousands of cob buildings and walls across the county are 200-500+ years old and still standing.

Cob requires no tools beyond what you have, no purchased materials, no forms, no mortar, no kiln. It is mixed by foot (trampling), shaped by hand, and built in courses that dry to a compressive strength of 0.5-1.0 MPa -- comparable to soft brick.

### Step-by-step

| Step | Action | Time Cost | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Prepare the foundation.** Dig a shallow trench along the 8m wall line, approximately 30 cm wide and 15 cm deep. This removes topsoil (which contains organic matter that weakens cob) and provides a stable base. Fill the trench with a 5 cm layer of compacted sand for drainage (prevents water wicking up into the cob wall). | 2 hr | 2:00 |
| 2 | **Mix the cob.** On the tarp (spread flat as a mixing surface), create the cob mix: approximately 3 parts clay subsoil to 1 part sand, with straw added generously (about 15-20% by volume). Add water until the mix is the consistency of stiff bread dough -- it should hold its shape when formed into a ball but not be so wet that it slumps. Mix by stomping with boots (traditional Devon method). Each batch: ~100 kg of clay, ~35 kg of sand, ~15 kg of straw, ~20 liters of water. | 1 hr per batch | 3:00 |
| 3 | **Build the first course (lift).** Take handfuls of the cob mix (each "cob" weighing 2-5 kg) and stack them along the trench, pressing each lump firmly into the ones below and beside it. Build the first course approximately 30 cm wide and 30 cm tall along the full 8m length. The wide base (30 cm) provides stability. Press and smooth the surface. Total material for first course: approximately 0.3m x 0.3m x 8m = 0.72 m^3 = approximately 1,440 kg of wet cob. | 4 hr | 7:00 |
| 4 | **Allow the first course to firm up.** Cob needs to partially dry between courses to support the weight above. In 12-15 degrees C with light wind, a 30 cm course firms to load-bearing stiffness in approximately 4-8 hours. Build the first course by early afternoon on Day 1, and it will be firm enough for the second course by late evening or early morning Day 2. Cover with tarp overnight if dew is expected. | 6-8 hr (drying, no labor) | ~15:00 (end of Day 1) |
| 5 | **Build the second course (Day 2 morning).** Stack a second 30 cm lift on top of the first. Scratch (scarify) the top of the first course with the pitchfork before applying the second -- this roughened surface provides mechanical bond between courses. The second course can be slightly narrower (25 cm) as the wall tapers. Material: ~0.25m x 0.3m x 8m = 0.6 m^3 = ~1,200 kg. | 3.5 hr | 18:30 |
| 6 | **Allow the second course to firm up.** Same drying process. 4-8 hours. | 4-6 hr | ~24:00 |
| 7 | **Build the third course (Day 2 afternoon).** Third lift: 25 cm wide, 30 cm tall. This brings the wall to 0.9m. Material: ~0.25m x 0.3m x 8m = 0.6 m^3 = ~1,200 kg. | 3.5 hr | 27:30 |
| 8 | **Build the fourth course (Day 2 evening).** Final lift: 20 cm wide, 30 cm tall. Total wall height: 1.2m. Slight taper from 30 cm at base to 20 cm at top is structurally optimal (lower center of gravity, wider base). Material: ~0.20m x 0.3m x 8m = 0.48 m^3 = ~960 kg. | 3 hr | 30:30 |
| 9 | **Cap the wall.** Cover the top of the wall with the tarp, secured with stones or stakes. This protects the fresh cob from rain until it has cured sufficiently (about 1-2 weeks for surface hardening). Ideally, a permanent cap of thatch or tile would be added later, but the tarp protects for now. | 30 min | 31:00 |

**Total labor: approximately 17-18 hours over 2 days. Total drying/waiting time: approximately 14-16 hours. Within the 48-hour window.**

### Physics Validation

- **Cob compressive strength**: Properly mixed cob (clay-sand-straw-water) dries to a compressive strength of 0.5-1.0 MPa. For comparison, common building brick is 10-20 MPa. Cob is weaker than brick but entirely adequate for a non-load-bearing windbreak wall 1.2m tall. The wall supports only its own weight and wind forces. Valid. (Reference: Keefe, 2005, "Earth Building: Methods and Materials, Repair and Conservation")

- **Wind resistance**: Wind pressure at 50 km/h (13.9 m/s): P = 0.5 x rho x v^2 = 0.5 x 1.225 x 13.9^2 = 118 Pa. On a wall 1.2m tall x 8m long = 9.6 m^2 face area, total wind force = 118 x 9.6 = 1,133 N. The wall's base (0.3m wide x 8m long) sits on a foundation trench, and its own weight (approximately 4,800 kg when partially dried) provides stabilizing force. Overturning moment from wind = 1,133 x 0.6m (center of pressure) = 680 Nm. Restoring moment from self-weight = 4,800 x 9.81 x 0.15m (center of gravity) = 7,063 Nm. Safety factor against overturning: 7,063/680 = 10.4. The wall is stable against 50 km/h winds by a large margin. Valid.

- **Drying time between courses**: Cob drying rate depends on temperature, humidity, wind, and wall thickness. In Devon conditions (12-15 degrees C, 70-80% relative humidity, light wind), a 30 cm thick course achieves sufficient surface firmness (not full cure, but enough to support the next lift) in 4-8 hours. Full cure requires 4-8 weeks, but the wall is structurally functional for wind resistance within 48 hours of building. Valid. (Reference: UK Earth Building Association guidelines)

- **Material quantities**: Total wall volume (tapered): approximately 2.4 m^3. Wet cob density: approximately 2,000 kg/m^3. Total material: approximately 4,800 kg. Clay subsoil needed: approximately 3,600 kg (75% of mix). Sand: approximately 1,000 kg. Straw: approximately 200 kg. All within the available quantities (unlimited clay, 500 kg sand, 200 kg straw). Sand is tight but sufficient. Valid.

- **Historical precedent**: Cob construction in Devon, England dates to at least the 13th century. Approximately 20,000 cob buildings survive in Devon today. Many are 200-500 years old. The longest continuously inhabited cob building in England dates to approximately 1420. Cob walls 30 cm or thicker are wind-resistant, weather-resistant (with proper cap and base), and structurally sound. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Clay soil is a construction material, not just ground.** The most fundamental insight is that the "dirt" beneath your feet is a building material with a millennium of proven structural use. Devon red clay, when mixed with sand and straw, becomes cob -- a material that has outlasted most modern buildings.

2. **Cob requires no specialized tools or skills.** It is mixed by foot (stomping), formed by hand, and requires no formwork, mortar, kiln, or machinery. The tools required (spade, wheelbarrow, boots, hands) are precisely what is available. Cob is arguably the most accessible construction technique in human history.

3. **The "Wrong Toolbox" framing hides the solution.** When you think "build a wall," you think "bricks, blocks, concrete, lumber." When those materials are absent, the problem seems unsolvable. But the correct material set (clay, sand, straw, water) was available all along -- it just was not in the mental category of "wall-building materials."

4. **Straw is structural reinforcement, not just filler.** Straw in cob functions like rebar in concrete: it provides tensile strength to a material that is strong in compression but weak in tension. The straw fibers bridge cracks and distribute stress, dramatically improving the wall's resistance to cracking and impact.

5. **A tapered wall is smarter than a vertical one.** Building wider at the base (30 cm) and narrower at the top (20 cm) lowers the center of gravity, increases the base footprint, and improves wind resistance, all while using less material for the upper courses.

## Distractor Analysis

- **Straw bales** are the most tempting alternative. They are large, stackable, and immediately available. But straw bale construction requires protection from rain (rendering or cladding), which is not available, and 8 bales do not provide enough length for the 8m wall.
- **The tarp** suggests a quick windbreak, but tarps fail in high winds. The tarp is better used as a mixing surface and curing cover.
- **The pitchfork** suggests stacking straw, not mixing cob. Its actual best use is scarifying cob courses for bonding and mixing straw into the cob.
- **"Just dig a berm"** is a reasonable but inferior approach -- an unreinforced earth berm erodes in rain. Adding sand and straw (making cob) transforms unstable soil into a durable building material.
