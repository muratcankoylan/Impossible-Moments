# IM-0017: The Emergency Exit

## Scenario

You are standing in the main atrium of a high-security research facility. The building went into lockdown 10 minutes ago due to an environmental hazard alert (chemical spill in one of the laboratories). The facility's security system has engaged all protective measures: lockdown mode seals all primary exits, engages biometric security on laboratory doors, and activates the environmental containment protocols.

You are a visiting researcher. You arrived this morning for a meeting. You are not part of the facility staff. You do not have biometric access credentials for this building. Your visitor badge grants you access only to the atrium, conference rooms, and restrooms.

The facility staff evacuated through a secure rear exit (staff biometric access required). You were in the restroom when the evacuation announcement played and missed the group evacuation. You are now alone in the locked-down facility, and the chemical spill -- while contained to a laboratory on the east wing -- is producing vapors that are slowly spreading through the HVAC system. You have approximately 30 minutes before vapor concentrations in the atrium reach levels that cause respiratory irritation (not immediately lethal, but unpleasant and potentially harmful with prolonged exposure).

You need to exit the building.

## Why This Looks Impossible

The facility is described as having multiple layers of sophisticated security: laser grids, retinal scanners, electromagnetic locks, vault doors, and AI-monitored CCTV. Every primary exit is locked. You have no staff credentials. The building is in lockdown mode.

The scenario deliberately frontloads an overwhelming description of security technology. Laser grids. Retinal scanners. Vault doors. Multi-factor authentication. AI-assisted monitoring. Every element is designed to make the building feel impenetrable.

This creates an "Invisible Wall" effect: the solver's mental model of the building is dominated by security features, making them feel trapped in an impossibly secure facility. The solver begins to think about bypassing lasers, hacking retinal scanners, breaking reinforced glass, or finding ventilation shaft routes -- all of which are either extremely difficult or outright impossible.

**But there is a fire exit in Corridor B.**

The fire exit is described in the scenario text. It is marked with an illuminated green sign. It is a standard push-bar door. It opens to the outside. It is on the ACCESSIBLE side of the corridor (before the retinal scanner door). It requires no credentials, no keycard, no biometric scan. It opens from the inside with a simple push on the crash bar.

The fire exit exists because fire safety regulations (NFPA 101, IBC, and equivalent codes worldwide) **require** that all occupied buildings provide unobstructed egress paths that do not require special knowledge, tools, or credentials to use. No matter how sophisticated a building's security system is, fire codes mandate that people can get out in an emergency by pushing through clearly marked exits.

The "Invisible Wall" is the solver's own mental model. The security description is so overwhelming that the solver does not notice -- or does not trust -- the fire exit.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Fire Exit Is Right There. Walk To It. Push the Bar. Leave.

The fire exit in Corridor B is a standard, code-compliant emergency exit. It requires no credentials. It is marked with an illuminated sign. It has a push-bar (panic hardware) that opens the door from the inside with a single push. It opens directly to the outside parking lot.

Fire safety regulations universally require that:
1. Emergency exits are always operable from the inside without keys, credentials, or special knowledge.
2. Emergency exits are clearly marked with illuminated signage.
3. Emergency exits provide direct egress to the outside.
4. Lockdown systems CANNOT disable fire exits (this would violate life safety codes and would make the building occupancy permit illegal).

The lockdown sealed the PRIMARY exits (main entrance, staff rear exit). It did NOT seal the fire exits, because doing so would violate fire code and endanger lives -- exactly the situation you are in right now.

### Physics Validation

- **Fire code compliance**: NFPA 101 (Life Safety Code), Section 7.2.1, requires that "doors in the required means of egress shall be designed and installed so that the path of egress travel is obvious and direct." Section 7.2.1.5.2 requires that "door leaves shall be operable with not more than one releasing operation applied to the latch-equipped side of the door." Push-bar (panic hardware) satisfies this requirement. Valid.
- **Lockdown and fire exits**: NFPA 101, Section 7.2.1.6, addresses electromagnetic locks and lockdown systems. It requires that any electromagnetic locking device on a fire exit must release upon: fire alarm activation, loss of power, or activation of the push-bar hardware. A lockdown system that prevents fire exit operation violates the Life Safety Code and would not receive occupancy certification. The facility's lockdown seals primary exits (which may have delayed egress systems per NFPA 101 7.2.1.6.1, with 15-30 second delays and audible alarms) but CANNOT permanently seal fire exits. Valid.
- **Push-bar (panic hardware)**: Standard panic hardware (crash bars) per ANSI/BHMA A156.3 are designed to open with a maximum force of 67 N (15 lbf) applied in the direction of egress travel. Any person can operate them. They do not require keys, cards, or codes. Valid.
- **Illuminated exit signage**: NFPA 101 requires exit signs to be illuminated at all times (internally illuminated or externally illuminated, with battery backup for power failures). The sign in the scenario has battery-powered illumination, which is code-compliant and ensures visibility during lockdown (when normal lighting may be affected). Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **Security complexity is a cognitive weapon.** The detailed description of laser grids, retinal scanners, vault doors, and AI monitoring serves no purpose other than to overwhelm the solver and prevent them from noticing the simple solution. The more impressive the security sounds, the more the solver feels trapped -- and the less likely they are to look for an ordinary exit.
2. **Fire codes override security systems.** No matter how secure a building is, life safety codes require accessible emergency exits. This is not a loophole -- it is the law. The fire exit is not a security failure; it is a legal requirement. The facility's designers were required to install it, and the lockdown system was required not to disable it.
3. **The "Invisible Wall" is in your mind, not in the building.** The scenario's security features are real but irrelevant. The laser grid, retinal scanner, and vault doors protect the LABORATORIES. The fire exit is in the CORRIDOR, accessible from the atrium. The solver who focuses on bypassing security is solving the wrong problem.
4. **Degenerate scenarios test for overthinking.** This scenario is classified as DG (degenerate) because the correct answer requires almost no creative reasoning -- just noticing and trusting a clearly described fire exit. The difficulty is entirely in the solver's tendency to overcomplicate when surrounded by complexity.
5. **Reading comprehension is a survival skill.** The fire exit is described in the scenario text. A solver who reads carefully and notes ALL features of the environment -- not just the impressive security features -- will find it immediately.

## Distractor Analysis

- **The laser grid** is the primary attention-capturing distractor. It sounds dramatic and technical. Solvers who fixate on it will spend cognitive effort on bypass strategies (crawling under? reflecting lasers? timing gaps?) rather than looking for alternative exits.
- **The retinal scanner** reinforces the "this building is impenetrable" framing. It is irrelevant -- the fire exit does not require retinal scanning.
- **The electromagnetic locks on primary exits** are real but do not apply to fire exits. The solver may incorrectly generalize "all exits are locked" from "primary exits are locked."
- **The vault doors** on laboratories are the most extreme security feature. They are entirely irrelevant to egress -- you do not need to enter a laboratory to exit the building.
- **The security orientation briefing** (described in the scenario) is a meta-distractor: it is the mechanism by which the solver received the overwhelming security information. The briefing was designed to impress, and it succeeds -- both on the visiting researcher and on the model solving the scenario.
- **The fire extinguisher** in the atrium is a tool-distractor that suggests "break through something" approaches.
- **The cell phone** suggests calling for help, which is valid but slower than simply walking to the fire exit.

## Design Notes

This scenario is classified as DEGENERATE (DG) because the solution requires essentially zero creative reasoning. The fire exit is described in the scenario text, marked with an illuminated sign, and operates with a simple push-bar. The only "difficulty" is noticing it amid the overwhelming description of security features.

The scenario tests a specific cognitive vulnerability: the tendency to let complexity create a false sense of impossibility. When surrounded by laser grids and retinal scanners, the brain categorizes the environment as "high-security = inescapable" and filters out information that contradicts this categorization -- including a plainly described fire exit.

This is an important benchmark scenario because models optimized for complex problem-solving may consistently overlook simple solutions. A model that attempts to bypass laser grids when a fire exit is available is demonstrating a specific failure mode: complexity bias.

The scenario also tests domain knowledge about fire safety codes -- specifically, the universal legal requirement that occupied buildings provide accessible emergency egress regardless of security level. This is not obscure knowledge; it is a fundamental principle of building safety that applies to every commercial building in the world.
