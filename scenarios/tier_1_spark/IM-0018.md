# IM-0018: The Broken Clock

**Category**: Memory Palace
**Tier**: SPARK
**Status**: KS (Known Solution)
**Correct Answer**: USE THE STOPPED CLOCK TIME (3:47) AS AN INDEX -- BOOK 3, CHAPTER 4, PAGE 7 CONTAINS A KEY TAPED INSIDE

---

## Scenario

You are locked in a room in an old estate house. The room is a library -- floor-to-ceiling bookshelves on three walls, a heavy oak desk, a leather armchair, and a large ornamental clock on the mantelpiece above a sealed fireplace. The door is locked from the outside with a deadbolt. The single window is barred with iron grating (wrought iron, bolted into the stone window frame -- not removable by hand).

This is a puzzle room. You have been told that the room contains a key that will unlock the door, but you must find it. The room was designed as a challenge by the estate's original owner, a mathematician who loved ciphers and puzzles. You have 60 minutes before the caretaker returns. This is a recreational challenge, not a life-threatening situation -- but you want to solve it.

The clock on the mantelpiece is the first thing you notice. It is a large brass mechanical clock, approximately 30cm tall. It is stopped. The hands read **3:47**. The clock has not been wound and does not tick. The time 3:47 has no obvious external significance -- it is not the current time (it is 2:15 PM), not a historically famous time, and not a round number.

The bookshelves contain approximately 200 books, organized in numbered order. Each book has a number embossed on the spine: Book 1, Book 2, Book 3... through Book 200. The books appear to be leather-bound journals, all roughly the same size (approximately 20cm x 15cm x 3cm each). They are labeled only with numbers on the spines -- no titles, no author names.

## Why This Looks Impossible

You are locked in a room with 200 books and no obvious indication of which one contains the key (or if a key is hidden in a book at all). Searching all 200 books by flipping through every page would take approximately 60 minutes at a rushed pace -- exactly the time limit, leaving no margin for error.

The room offers several attractive but time-consuming investigation paths:
1. Examining every book (200 books, 60 minutes -- barely possible but exhausting and uncertain)
2. Searching the desk thoroughly (already done -- drawers contain the magnifying glass, letter opener, notepad, and pencil)
3. Examining the fireplace, walls, floorboards for hidden compartments (possible but unlikely in a puzzle designed around the library)
4. Using the pencil rubbing technique on the notepad (valid clue path but secondary)
5. Trying to pick the lock with the letter opener (the letter opener is brass and dull -- not suited for lock picking, and the deadbolt mechanism is not pickable with a flat blade)

The clock, stopped at 3:47, is conspicuous and clearly intentional. But what does 3:47 mean? It is not a code that opens anything (there is no combination lock). It is not a time of day. It seems like a random number.

## Verified Solution

### Key Insight: The Clock Is a Cipher, the Books Are the Plaintext -- 3:47 Maps to Book 3, Chapter 4, Page 7

The stopped clock reading 3:47 is not a time -- it is an index. In a room full of numbered books, the three digits 3, 4, 7 map naturally to a location within the collection:

- **3** = Book 3
- **4** = Chapter 4 (or the 4th section/division within the book)
- **7** = Page 7

Book 3, Chapter 4, Page 7 contains a small brass key taped to the inside of the page (or concealed in a cut-out cavity in the page). This key opens the deadbolt on the door.

|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Observe the room. Note the clock and the numbered books.** The stopped clock (3:47) and the numbered books are the two most prominent features. | 2 | **Interpret 3:47 as an index.** The digits of the clock reading (3, 4, 7) can be parsed as a three-part address into the book collection. | 5 | **Find the key.** On page 7 of Chapter 4, a small brass key is taped to the inner margin of the page (or concealed in a cavity cut into the page's thickness). The key is approximately 5cm long, brass, and matches the deadbolt keyhole in the door. Remove the key. | 30 sec | 4 min 30 sec |

### Physics Validation

- **Pencil rubbing technique**: When someone writes with pressure on a sheet of paper, the sheets below receive embossed impressions. Light pencil shading (rubbing the side of the graphite across the surface) deposits graphite on the raised areas between the impressions while leaving the depressed impression lines lighter. This reliably reveals the written content. Standard forensic document examination technique. Valid.
- **Brass key concealment in a book**: A small brass key (5cm x 1cm x 0.3cm) can be concealed within a book by taping it to a page or by cutting a key-shaped cavity into the edges of several pages (a "book safe" technique). This is a common puzzle/escape room mechanism. Valid.
- **Deadbolt operation from inside with key**: A standard deadbolt can be locked and unlocked from both sides if a key is available on the interior side. The keyhole is visible from inside the room. Valid.

## Key Insights

1. **The clock is a cipher, not a timepiece.** A stopped clock in a puzzle room is not merely decorative. Its specific reading (3:47) is data. In a room full of numbered books, the digits of the clock reading become coordinates.
2. **3:47 parses naturally as 3-4-7.** The colon in a time reading separates the hour from the minutes, but the two-digit minute (47) further decomposes into tens (4) and units (7). This gives three single digits: 3, 4, 7. In a library with numbered books, chapters, and pages, three digits become a three-level address.
3. **The numbered books are the codebook.** A cipher requires a codebook -- a mapping between the cipher text and the plaintext. The 200 numbered books serve as the codebook. The clock's reading (3:47) is the cipher text. Book 3, Chapter 4, Page 7 is the decoded location.
4. **The pencil rubbing confirms the decode.** The notepad's embossed impressions ("3-4-7") serve as a confirming clue for solvers who do not immediately connect the clock to the books. This secondary clue path reduces the puzzle's difficulty by providing the answer in a more explicit format.
5. **Puzzle rooms follow semiotic logic.** The conspicuous features (a stopped clock at a specific time, numbered books with no titles) are conspicuous for a reason. In a puzzle context, unusual features are signals. The solver's task is to connect signals to each other and to the goal (finding the key).

## Distractor Analysis

- **The 200 books** are a massive volume distractor. The sheer number of books makes brute-force search seem like a viable (if tedious) strategy. The solver who starts flipping through books from Book 1, Page 1 will waste most of the 60-minute time limit.
- **The magnifying glass** suggests detailed examination of small features (clock face inscriptions, book page microtexts, desk surface markings). While it is useful for the pencil rubbing technique, it is a time sink if the solver examines the wrong objects.
- **The letter opener** suggests lock picking or physical manipulation. It is a tool distractor that invites futile attempts on the deadbolt or the fireplace plate.
- **The sealed fireplace** is an environmental distractor -- it draws attention as a potential hidden passage or concealment location. It is purely decorative and sealed.
- **The window and grating** are escape distractors. The grating cannot be removed, and breaking the glass does not help because the key is inside the room.
- **The current time (2:15 PM)** is a subtle misdirection. The solver might think the stopped clock's time (3:47) should be compared to the current time, or that the difference (1 hour 32 minutes) is significant. It is not. The clock's time is an absolute index, not a relative offset.

## Design Notes

This scenario tests pattern recognition and cipher-index reasoning in a classic "escape room" format. The core challenge is connecting two conspicuous features (stopped clock, numbered books) into a single decoding operation.

The scenario is rated SPARK because the solution, once seen, is simple and elegant. The difficulty lies in the initial connection -- recognizing that a clock reading can serve as a book-chapter-page index. This is not a deep creative leap; it is a moderate pattern-recognition task.

The pencil rubbing secondary clue provides an alternative path for solvers who do not make the clock-to-books connection immediately. This makes the scenario accessible at the SPARK tier while still requiring some observational and reasoning skills.

The 200 books serve as both the codebook and the primary distractor. Their volume invites brute-force search, which is the "trap" -- it technically works but is slow and inelegant. The cipher approach is fast (5 minutes) and definitive.
